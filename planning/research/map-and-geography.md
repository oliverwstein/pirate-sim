### 1.1 GREATER ANTILLES — Spanish Core

---

**HAVANA** *(La Habana)*
- **Coordinates:** 23.137°N, 82.359°W
- **Founded:** 1514 (first site); relocated to current position 16 November 1519
- **Control:** Spanish 1519–1762 (throughout our period)
- **Population:** ~1,500 (1600) | ~3,000–4,000 (1650) | ~7,000–10,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Bottle-neck natural harbor entered via a narrow channel (~200m wide between Morro headland and the Punta). Inner bay is extremely sheltered, depth 10–12m. Excellent fresh water from rivers. Protected from all swells. The finest harbor in the Spanish Caribbean.
- **Strategic Significance:** The mandatory assembly point for the entire *Flota de Indias* system. All homeward-bound treasure fleet components (New Spain *flota* from Veracruz + Tierra Firme *galeones* from Cartagena/Portobelo) rendezvoused here before sailing the Gulf Stream home. Copper from Havana foundries supplied New World artillery. The city controlled access to the Straits of Florida.
- **Fortification:** VERY HIGH. Castillo de la Real Fuerza (1558); Castillo de los Tres Reyes del Morro (completed 1630) on eastern headland; Castillo de San Salvador de la Punta (1589) on western side. By 1700, full circuit of city walls plus outer harbor forts. The most strongly fortified port in the Americas.

---

**PORTOBELO** *(Porto Bello)*
- **Coordinates:** 9.554°N, 79.655°W
- **Founded:** 1597 by Francisco Velarde y Mercado; replaced Nombre de Dios
- **Control:** Spanish 1597–1739 (throughout our period; raided 1601, 1668, 1679)
- **Population:** ~500 (1600) | ~2,000–3,000 during fair seasons (1650) | ~1,500 permanent + 3,000–8,000 during annual fair (1700)
- **Harbor:** Deep natural harbor, excellent shelter on three sides from hills. Depth 12–15m. Chronically unhealthy—yellow fever, malaria rampant. Fresh water abundant from surrounding rainforest. Henry Morgan took it in 1668; it was then ransomed for 100,000 pieces of eight.
- **Strategic Significance:** One of only two authorized Atlantic ports of the Spanish Empire (with Veracruz) for the Tierra Firme fleet. Silver from Peru (via Panama City overland across the Isthmus) was loaded here. Annual fair (feria) generated enormous commerce. Drake died of dysentery offshore in 1596 and was buried at sea in a lead coffin.
- **Fortification:** HIGH. Castillo de San Felipe (17th c.), Castillo de Santiago, Castillo de San Jerónimo. Three forts guarded the harbor entrance on both sides plus the town. Repeatedly rebuilt after attacks. Despite fortifications, Morgan took it in a bold night assault 1668.

---

**CARTAGENA DE INDIAS**
- **Coordinates:** 10.400°N, 75.500°W
- **Founded:** 1 June 1533 by Pedro de Heredia
- **Control:** Spanish 1533–throughout period. (Drake sacked it 1586; it has never been permanently taken by a foreign power.)
- **Population:** ~2,000 (1600) | ~5,000 (1650) | ~8,000–10,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Large natural bay entered through two channels: Bocachica (the main, southern approach) and Bocagrande (silted up by 1700 after French attack 1697 prompted deepening of Bocachica only). Depth in main harbor 8–12m. Excellent shelter. Fresh water from Turbaco hinterland.
- **Strategic Significance:** Primary port for the Tierra Firme fleet and the *asiento* slave trade. Exported Bolivian silver (via Bogotá mule route), emeralds from Muzo mines, hides, and indigo. The wealthiest city on the Spanish Main. Sir Francis Drake ransomed it for 107,000 ducats (1586). Admiral Baron de Pointis and the buccaneers sacked it again in 1697 for ~10 million livres.
- **Fortification:** VERY HIGH. Castillo San Felipe de Barajas (begun 1536, massively expanded 1657–1762)—the largest Spanish fortification in the Americas. City walls (muralla), Fort San Sebastián del Pastelillo, Castle of San Fernando de Bocachica. One of the most fortified cities in the New World.

---

**VERACRUZ** *(Heroica Veracruz / originally Villa Rica de la Vera Cruz)*
- **Coordinates:** 19.212°N, 96.133°W
- **Founded:** 1519 by Hernán Cortés (first site at Quiahuiztlan; modern site established by 1600)
- **Control:** Spanish 1519–throughout period. Raided repeatedly: Hawkins 1568, Lorencillo/Grammont 1683.
- **Population:** ~5,000 (1600) | ~6,000 (1650) | ~6,000–7,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** POOR natural conditions. Open roadstead exposed to "northers" (*nortes*)—fierce northerly winter storms that made the anchorage extremely dangerous from October to April. Ships had to shelter behind the offshore San Juan de Ulúa reef-island and fort. Depth 8–10m at the fort anchorage. The harbor was considered the most dangerous in the Caribbean due to nortes and yellow fever.
- **Strategic Significance:** The sole authorized Atlantic outlet for New Spain (Mexico). All silver from Mexico City and Manila galleon goods transshipped from Acapulco passed through here. Dominated the Gulf of Mexico trade.
- **Fortification:** HIGH. Castle/fort of San Juan de Ulúa on offshore reef island—the most important fortification. Controlled since 1535; massively upgraded 1580s–1600s. City walls rebuilt after 1683 sack.

---

**SANTO DOMINGO**
- **Coordinates:** 18.463°N, 69.936°W
- **Founded:** 5 August 1496 by Bartholomew Columbus (oldest continually occupied European settlement in the Americas)
- **Control:** Spanish 1496–throughout period (briefly occupied by Drake 1586 for ransom)
- **Population:** ~4,000 (1600) | ~3,500 (1650) | ~5,000 (1700) *(declining as center of empire shifted west)*
- **Harbor:** Mouth of the Ozama River. Depth acceptable for period ships (4–6m); inner river anchorage sheltered. Not a deep-water harbor; the river entrance is shallow and requires careful navigation.
- **Strategic Significance:** Administrative capital of Hispaniola and original seat of Spanish colonial government in the Americas. Headquarters of the Audiencia. Cultural and ecclesiastical center of early Spanish colonialism (first cathedral, first university in Americas). By 1600, overshadowed by Havana and Cartagena economically, but still symbolically the "first city."
- **Fortification:** MODERATE-HIGH. Fortaleza Ozama (1502–1508, oldest European fort in the Americas). City walls. Torre del Homenaje. Adequately but not overwhelmingly fortified.

**SANTIAGO DE CUBA**
- **Coordinates:** 19.984°N, 75.817°W
- **Founded:** 1514 by Diego Velázquez de Cuéllar; original capital of Cuba until 1553 (moved to Havana)
- **Control:** Spanish 1514–throughout period
- **Population:** ~1,500 (1600) | ~2,000 (1650) | ~3,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Excellent enclosed harbor, entering via a narrow channel flanked by hills. Very sheltered. Depth 10–15m. Good water supply from surrounding mountains.
- **Strategic Significance:** Eastern Cuba's main port; terminus for small coastwise trade. Proximity to Windward Passage made it important for monitoring traffic. Mining (copper) in the nearby mountains. Second city of Cuba.
- **Fortification:** MODERATE. Castillo del Morro (San Pedro de la Roca, 1638–1700), a UNESCO site—dramatically sited on a promontory commanding the harbor entrance. One of the best-preserved Spanish forts in the Caribbean.

---

**PORT ROYAL** *(Caguaya/Cagway until 1655)*
- **Coordinates:** 17.938°N, 76.841°W
- **Founded:** Spanish presence from 1494; English 1655; destroyed by earthquake 7 June 1692
- **Control:** Spanish (nominal) 1494–1655; English/English Crown 1655–1692; rebuilt, smaller, thereafter
- **Population:** ~200 (1660) | ~6,000–8,000 (1680s, peak) | ~500 (1700, after earthquake/tsunami)
- **Harbor:** Kingston Harbour—one of the largest natural harbors in the world. Depth 15–20m. Completely sheltered by the Palisadoes tombolo. Fresh water from Rio Cobre via Kingston. Could accommodate hundreds of vessels simultaneously.
- **Strategic Significance:** By the 1670s–1692, the commercial capital of the English Caribbean and the largest city in English America. Entrepôt for the buccaneers: Henry Morgan based here. Sugar, logwood, slaves, contraband silver all passed through. Five forts (Fort James, Fort Charles, Fort Carlisle, Fort Rupert, Morgan's Line) defended it before the 1692 earthquake destroyed two-thirds of the city.
- **Fortification:** HIGH (pre-1692). Fort Charles and Fort James were substantial. Earthquake sank approximately half the city into the harbor.

**SPANISH TOWN** *(Santiago de la Vega)*
- **Coordinates:** 17.993°N, 76.957°W
- **Founded:** c.1534 by Diego Columbus
- **Control:** Spanish 1534–1655; English 1655–; official capital of Jamaica throughout (Port Royal was informal/commercial capital)
- **Population:** ~600 (1600) | ~1,200 (1650) | ~2,500 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Inland capital, ~12km from Kingston Harbour via Rio Cobre. No direct harbor—served by Port Royal/Kingston.
- **Strategic Significance:** Administrative and legal capital of Jamaica throughout the period. The seat of the island's governor.
- **Fortification:** LOW. Inland position. Fort Augusta guarded the harbor approach near Kingston.

---

**KINGSTON**
- **Coordinates:** 17.993°N, 76.793°W
- **Founded:** After 1692 earthquake (settlers moved from Port Royal)
- **Control:** English 1692–
- **Population:** ~0 (pre-1692) | ~3,000–5,000 (1700, rapidly growing)
- **Harbor:** Same Kingston Harbour as Port Royal. Excellent depth and shelter.
- **Strategic Significance:** Replaced Port Royal as the commercial hub of Jamaica. Rose rapidly in the early 18th century.
- **Fortification:** Low initially; Fort Augusta and various batteries added 1700s–1740s.

**SAN JUAN, PUERTO RICO**
- **Coordinates:** 18.466°N, 66.116°W *(Old San Juan islet)*
- **Founded:** 1521 by Juan Ponce de León (moved from Caparra)
- **Control:** Spanish 1521–throughout period. Withstood Drake's attack 1595; defeated Cumberland 1598 (English held briefly then lost to disease); defeated Dutch attack 1625.
- **Population:** ~1,500 (1600) | ~2,500 (1650) | ~4,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** San Juan Bay: entered through a narrow channel between headlands. Depth 8–12m. Excellent shelter. El Morro on the northwest headland. Fresh water from Bayamón River on the mainland.
- **Strategic Significance:** "The key to the Indies" (*Llave de las Indias*)—critical waypoint controlling the Mona Passage and northeast Caribbean approaches. Major defensive position guarding the Puerto Rico Channel against Dutch and English ships sailing south from North America. Also a waypoint for treasure fleets.
- **Fortification:** VERY HIGH. El Morro (Castillo San Felipe del Morro, begun 1539, massively expanded 1587–1600s)—six-story fort with walls 6m thick; Castillo San Cristóbal (17th c.); La Fortaleza (1533). One of the most heavily fortified cities in the Americas.

### 1.2 LESSER ANTILLES — English, French, Dutch

---

**BRIDGETOWN, BARBADOS**
- **Coordinates:** 13.097°N, 59.613°W *(Carlisle Bay port)*
- **Founded:** 1628 by English settlers
- **Control:** English 1628–throughout period (never seriously contested)
- **Population:** ~1,500 (1640) | ~7,000 (1650, with large enslaved population) | ~15,000+ (1700)
- **Harbor:** Carlisle Bay—an open roadstead on the southwestern coast. NOT an enclosed harbor; exposed to swells from the south and west. Depth 8–12m at anchorage. Ships needed good ground tackle. Fresh water from Bridgetown springs (limited).
- **Strategic Significance:** Barbados was the windward-most English island—ships from Europe made landfall here first. The "most eastern" major English island served as the gateway to the English Caribbean. By 1680s, most productive sugar colony in the Americas per acre. The island's location outside the hurricane belt's normal track made it relatively safer. Major transshipment point.
- **Fortification:** MODERATE. Fort Charles, later various batteries. The open roadstead was itself defensible by ships.

---

**BASSETERRE, ST. KITTS** *(St. Christopher)*
- **Coordinates:** 17.300°N, 62.733°W
- **Founded:** 1627 (English settlement; French arrived same year—island was jointly held 1627–1713)
- **Control:** English and French 1627–1713; Treaty of Utrecht 1713 gave full island to Britain
- **Population:** ~500 (1630) | ~3,000–4,000 (1650) | ~5,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Open roadstead on SW coast. Shallow (3–5m close in), not excellent natural shelter. Ships anchored in the roadstead.
- **Strategic Significance:** St. Kitts was the first successful English colony in the Caribbean (1623/1627) and the "Mother Colony" that sent settlers to Nevis, Antigua, Montserrat. Also simultaneously the first French Caribbean colony. The unusual joint occupation created constant friction and two military confrontations (1627, 1666). Sugar production; pivotal Leeward Islands entrepôt.
- **Fortification:** MODERATE. Brimstone Hill Fortress (begun 1690s) on a volcanic plug—later called the "Gibraltar of the West Indies." English built it to dominate the island after the French were expelled from the northern section.

**ENGLISH HARBOUR, ANTIGUA**
- **Coordinates:** 17.000°N, 61.767°W
- **Founded:** English settlement of Antigua from 1632; English Harbour as naval base in regular use by late 17th century; Fort Berkeley built 1704
- **Control:** English 1632–throughout period (France occupied 1666–1667 briefly)
- **Population:** Small naval garrison + few hundred
- **Harbor:** EXCELLENT. Deeply enclosed natural hurricane hole—one of the finest in the Caribbean. Deep water (10–14m) right to the rocky shores. Protected by surrounding hills from all swell directions. One of the few harbors ships could use during hurricane season. Careening wharves from 1700s.
- **Strategic Significance:** The best natural sheltered anchorage in the Leeward Islands. Became the primary Royal Navy careening and refit base for the Eastern Caribbean. Ships could lie safely at anchor even in bad weather. The Royal Navy began using it as a safe haven in the 17th century. Fort Berkeley (1704) guarded the harbor mouth.
- **Fortification:** MODERATE (growing). Fort Berkeley 1704; Shirley Heights batteries added later 18th century.

**FORT-ROYAL (FORT-DE-FRANCE), MARTINIQUE**

- **Strategic Significance:** Capital and chief port of French Martinique; major center of the sugar trade. Privateering/buccaneering base. During the wars of Louis XIV, major French naval rendezvous point in the Eastern Caribbean. The strongest French position in the Lesser Antilles.
- **Fortification:** MODERATE-HIGH. Fort Saint-Louis (built 1638–1671, massively improved by Vauban-influenced engineers later 17th c.). City defended by the fort and harbor batteries.

**BASSE-TERRE, GUADELOUPE**

**CAP-FRANÇAIS (CAP-HAÏTIEN), SAINT-DOMINGUE**

The main port for the massively profitable Saint-Domingue sugar, indigo, and coffee trade.

### 1.3 DUTCH CARIBBEAN

---

**WILLEMSTAD, CURAÇAO**
- **Coordinates:** 12.117°N, 68.933°W
- **Founded:** Dutch West India Company seized from Spain 1634; Punda district established 1634
- **Control:** Dutch WIC from 1634–throughout period
- **Population:** ~500 (1640) | ~2,000 (1660) | ~5,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** EXCELLENT. Sint Anna Bay—a deep natural harbor (Schottegat) entered through a narrow channel. Depth 12–15m. Completely sheltered. Fresh water from cisterns (island is arid). The channel is so narrow one ship passes at a time.
- **Strategic Significance:** The premier Dutch smuggling and free-trade entrepôt in the Caribbean. Curaçao was outside the hurricane belt's worst tracks. Major slave trade depot under the WIC *asiento* arrangements. Contraband trade with the Spanish Main (Venezuela, Cartagena) flourished—Spanish colonists desperate for goods not supplied by the official *flotas* bought via Curaçao's free port. Also a major salt source (Bonaire). Center of Dutch Caribbean power.
- **Fortification:** MODERATE-HIGH. Fort Amsterdam (begun 1634) guarding the harbor entrance. Various batteries. Not as massive as Spanish fortifications, but strategically located.

---

**ORANJESTAD, SINT EUSTATIUS (ST. EUSTATIUS / "STATIA")**
- **Coordinates:** 17.483°N, 62.983°W
- **Founded:** Dutch from 1636; briefly English, then back to Dutch
- **Control:** Dutch 1636–, with brief English and French occupations
- **Population:** ~500 (1650) | ~1,200 (1680) | ~3,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Open roadstead on the western coast under the volcanic plug. No enclosed harbor. Ships anchored in the lee of the island. Depth adequate (8–10m) but exposed.
- **Strategic Significance:** "The Golden Rock"—arguably the greatest free-trade entrepôt of the 17th century Caribbean. St. Eustatius changed hands 22 times between European powers but remained a free port. Sugar, contraband, slaves, and military supplies all traded openly regardless of war. Hugely important as a neutral marketplace during the wars of 1665–1720. The island had no natural advantages except its neutrality and Dutch commercial organization.
- **Fortification:** LOW-MODERATE. Fort Oranje (1636). Small garrison only.

**PARAMARIBO, SURINAME**

- **Strategic Significance:** The main port for the profitable Dutch sugar, cotton, and coffee plantations of Suriname. Large enslaved population. Used Maroon (escaped slave) resistance as a constant security concern. Far outside the main Caribbean theater but important as part of Dutch Guiana.
- **Fortification:** MODERATE. Fort Zeelandia (1667) guarded the river approach.

### 1.4 PIRATE BASES

---

**TORTUGA** *(Île de la Tortue)*
- **Coordinates:** 20.040°N, 72.790°W
- **Founded:** Spanish originally; French and English settlers arrived 1625; French control established c.1640–1654 under Levasseur, then formally French from 1665
- **Control:** Disputed Spanish/French/English 1625–1640; French WIC governor from 1640 (briefly Spanish reconquest 1635); firmly French from 1665
- **Population:** ~500 (1640) | ~2,000 (1660) | ~3,000 (1680) | ~1,500 (1700, declining as Saint-Domingue grew)
- **Harbor:** Cayona Bay on southern coast—a sheltered anchorage suitable for careening and small-vessel operations. Depth 5–8m. Tortuga itself is rocky and hilly.
- **Strategic Significance:** THE classic pirate/buccaneer base. The headquarters of the Brethren of the Coast from c.1630–1670. After Levasseur's fortification of the island (1640), it was nearly impregnable. Henry Morgan, Jean le Vasseur, and numerous famous buccaneers operated from here. The island was strategically placed on the northern coast of Hispaniola across from the buccaneer hunting grounds. After the 1670s, it gradually yielded in importance to Petit-Goâve and Port Royal.
- **Fortification:** HIGH (for its size). Fort de Rocher (Le Vasseur's fort, 1640)—built on a 100-foot crag with a single approach path; reputedly impregnable. Lost its importance after Le Vasseur's assassination 1652.

---

**NASSAU (NEW PROVIDENCE / CHARLES TOWN)**
- **Coordinates:** 25.048°N, 77.353°W *(Nassau harbor)*
- **Founded:** English settlement from ~1666 (Eleutheran Adventurers earlier, 1648); Charles Town laid out c.1670; renamed Nassau 1695 by Nicholas Trott
- **Control:** English 1666–; burned by Spanish 1684; rebuilt; pirate republic effectively 1706–1718; Governor Woodes Rogers established Crown control 1718
- **Population:** ~300 (1680) | ~500 (1700) | ~2,000 (1715, pirate republic period)
- **Harbor:** Nassau Harbour between New Providence and Hog Island (now Paradise Island). Relatively shallow (3–5m) making it inaccessible to deep-draft warships—a deliberate advantage for pirates. Well-sheltered.
- **Strategic Significance:** During 1706–1718, the center of the "Flying Gang" pirate republic—Blackbeard, Charles Vane, "Calico Jack" Rackham, Benjamin Hornigold, and others used Nassau as their base. The shallow harbor made Royal Navy intervention difficult (the navy couldn't get in). Position at the northern end of the Bahamas gave easy access to Spanish shipping lanes. Woodes Rogers arrived as governor 1718, offering pardons—ending the "Golden Age of Piracy" era at Nassau.
- **Fortification:** LOW pre-1718. Fort Nassau (1695) was modest. Rogers rebuilt/expanded it after 1718.

### 1.5 SPANISH MAIN — SOUTH AND CENTRAL AMERICA

---

**MARACAIBO**
- **Coordinates:** 10.633°N, 71.633°W
- **Founded:** 1529 (first settlement by Ambrosio Alfínger); permanently established 1574 by Pedro Maldonado
- **Control:** Spanish 1574–throughout period. Raided by Henry Morgan 1669 (famous through the Maracaibo lagoon engagement)
- **Population:** ~1,000 (1600) | ~2,500 (1650) | ~3,500 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Access via a narrow bar at the entrance to Lake Maracaibo—depth on the bar limited to 3–4m, making it inaccessible to large vessels. Lake itself is enormous and deep. The bar was the critical chokepoint exploited by Morgan.
- **Strategic Significance:** Gateway to Lake Maracaibo and the highly productive agricultural region of the Venezuelan interior. Cacao, hides, tobacco. The bar made it defensible but also inconvenient for large-scale commerce. Henry Morgan's 1669 raid (capturing the bar fort and trapping his own escape) is one of the most dramatic episodes of Caribbean piracy.
- **Fortification:** MODERATE. Fort San Carlos de la Barra on the entrance sandbar. Several other positions.

---

**LA GUAIRA** *(port for Caracas)*
- **Coordinates:** 10.600°N, 66.933°W
- **Founded:** 1588 as a formal port
- **Control:** Spanish 1588–throughout period
- **Population:** ~500 (1600) | ~1,000 (1650) | ~2,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Very exposed open roadstead on the steep northern coast of Venezuela. Ships anchored in the open sea. The coast rises immediately to mountains behind—Caracas sits 900m up the coastal range. Small vessels could be hauled ashore. Extremely limited shelter; a "norther" could be catastrophic.
- **Strategic Significance:** The only viable port serving Caracas and the rich cacao-growing valleys of the Venezuelan interior. Venezuelan cacao was among the highest quality in the Americas and heavily traded. The challenging port conditions and dramatic topography made it awkward but irreplaceable.
- **Fortification:** MODERATE. Fort San Carlos and various coastal batteries protected the landing.

---

**TRINIDAD (PUERTO DE ESPAÑA / PORT OF SPAIN)**
- **Coordinates:** 10.652°N, 61.518°W
- **Founded:** Formal Spanish settlement 1592 (Antonio de Berrío); port of Spain established as a modest town
- **Control:** Spanish 1592–1797 (throughout our period, though barely maintained)
- **Population:** ~200 (1600) | ~500 (1650) | ~1,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Gulf of Paria—large, sheltered inland sea between Trinidad and the Venezuelan mainland. Depth adequate (8–10m). Excellent shelter. Fresh water from many rivers.
- **Strategic Significance:** Consistently neglected by the Spanish Crown despite excellent agricultural potential. Trinidad was seen as a backwater. However, the Gulf of Paria was strategically valuable as it gave access to the Orinoco delta and the mythical El Dorado interior. Dutch, English, and French vessels regularly traded contraband here despite Spanish prohibition. Became a major smuggling point.
- **Fortification:** VERY LOW. Token Spanish garrison. Essentially undefended.

---

**TOBAGO**
- **Coordinates:** 11.250°N, 60.667°W *(Man O'War Bay area)*
- **Founded:** Disputed: Courlanders (Latvians) 1654, Dutch multiple times 1628–1676, English, French; settled and re-settled many times
- **Control:** Extremely disputed: Courlander 1654–1666, Dutch 1658, English 1666, French 1677–1678, effectively abandoned/fought over 1678–1763 (English from 1763)
- **Population:** ~300 (various periods); often nearly deserted between colonial attempts
- **Harbor:** Several excellent natural harbors including Great Courland Bay (NW) and Store Bay. Rockly Bay. Man O'War Bay in the NE.
- **Strategic Significance:** Tobago changed hands more times (approximately 31 by various counts) than any other territory in the history of colonialism, primarily because of its excellent harbors and fertile land. Its position between the Lesser Antilles chain and South America made it a coveted staging post. Its bays were used as careening and watering stops. The famous buccaneer/privateer base was Scarborough Bay area.
- **Fortification:** LOW-MODERATE. Fort James (Dutch/English) and other ephemeral fortifications depending on which nation held it.

---

**MARGARITA ISLAND** *(Isla Margarita)*
- **Coordinates:** 11.050°N, 63.883°W *(La Asunción)*
- **Founded:** 1526 by Marcelo de Villalobos
- **Control:** Spanish 1526–throughout period
- **Population:** ~2,000 (1600) | ~2,500 (1650) | ~3,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Pampatar Bay on eastern coast: good anchorage, partial shelter. La Asunción is inland on the island.
- **Strategic Significance:** Famous for the pearl fisheries of the Río de las Perlas (Gulf of Paria area/Cubagua Island nearby)—pearls were a major early colonial export and the primary reason Spain settled this area first. By 1600 the pearl beds were largely exhausted (overharvested). Margarita remained a small agricultural outpost and salt-gathering center. Occasional target for Dutch and English traders.
- **Fortification:** MODERATE. Castillo de San Carlos de Borromeo (Pampatar, 1662) guarding the main anchorage. Fort Santiago de la Vigía.

---

**CAYENNE, FRENCH GUIANA**
- **Coordinates:** 4.933°N, 52.333°W
- **Founded:** French attempt 1643; permanent settlement 1664 (French Guiana formally)
- **Control:** French from 1664 (Dutch briefly 1664; English briefly 1667); French confirmed by Treaty of Breda 1667
- **Population:** ~200 (1670) | ~800 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Cayenne Island at the mouth of several rivers. River estuary approach, depth 4–6m. Tidal influence.
- **Strategic Significance:** Equatorial outpost of the French empire. Positioned at the eastern gateway to the Guiana coast. Attempted as a major colony by several ambitious schemes including the Kourou Expedition-style efforts. Primarily valuable for its position and for trading with the interior (furs, Annatto dye). Outside the Caribbean proper but included as a French Atlantic Americas trading point.
- **Fortification:** LOW. Fort Cépérou guarded the town.

---

**CAMPECHE** *(San Francisco de Campeche)*
- **Coordinates:** 19.850°N, 90.533°W
- **Founded:** 1540 by Francisco de Montejo
- **Control:** Spanish 1540–throughout period. Raided by pirates with extraordinary frequency: Mansvelt 1663, Henry Morgan 1663, Grammont 1672, etc.—Campeche was raided more times than almost any other port.
- **Population:** ~3,000 (1600) | ~4,000 (1650) | ~5,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** Open bay on the Gulf of Mexico. Exposed; depth limited (3–5m close in). No natural shelter from northers. Ships used a mooring ground some distance offshore.
- **Strategic Significance:** Primary export port for the logwood (*palo de tinta*) of the Yucatán Peninsula. Logwood was the critical dye source for Europe's textile industry (producing deep blues, blacks, and purples) and was worth more by weight than most other Caribbean products. Spanish monopoly on logwood was contested by English logwood cutters in Belize (from ~1638) and by pirates who raided Campeche for logwood cargoes. Also shipped cacao, hides, and silver from New Spain's interior.
- **Fortification:** LOW-MODERATE initially; HIGH by 1700. After repeated raids, the Spanish built impressive city walls with bastions (1686–1704)—the only fully walled colonial city in the Americas that still survives largely intact. Fort San Miguel, Fort San José, etc.

---

**BELIZE (LOGWOOD SETTLEMENTS)**
- **Coordinates:** ~17.250°N, 88.767°W *(Belize River mouth area)*
- **Founded:** English logwood cutters from approximately 1638 (traditionally linked to marooned sailor Peter Wallace/Wallis)
- **Control:** Informal English settlements; Spanish claimed sovereignty; no formal colony until late 18th century
- **Population:** ~100–300 English and enslaved workers (fluctuating through our period)
- **Harbor:** Belize River mouth, shallow (2–3m) and obstacle-strewn. Small craft only.
- **Strategic Significance:** The English logwood trade in Belize was economically vital—logwood (*palo de tinta*) fetched £100–200/ton in London. English settlers constantly harassed by Spanish attempts to expel them (1717, 1730, 1754, etc.) but always returned. This ongoing smuggling/logging operation was a persistent irritant to Spanish sovereignty and a key source of cheap logwood for England's textile trade. No permanent defenses.
- **Fortification:** NONE formally; settlers maintained small armed camps.

### 1.6 NORTH AMERICAN TRADE PARTNERS

---

**CHARLESTON** *(Charles Town, South Carolina)*
- **Coordinates:** 32.783°N, 79.933°W
- **Founded:** 1670 by English settlers (relocated to present site 1680)
- **Control:** English/English Crown 1670–
- **Population:** ~200 (1680) | ~2,000 (1700) | ~3,500 (1720)
- **Harbor:** Charleston Harbor: entrance across a bar (2.4–3m depth—limiting), then wide inner harbor. Good shelter.
- **Strategic Significance:** The major port of the Carolina colony. Key Caribbean trade connection for rice, deerskins, and naval stores (tar, pitch, turpentine) heading to Barbados and other islands. Received sugar, slaves, and manufactured goods in return. Also important as a pirate supply/re-sale point.

---

**BOSTON**
- **Coordinates:** 42.360°N, 71.057°W
- **Founded:** 1630 by Puritan settlers
- **Control:** English/English Crown 1630–
- **Population:** ~7,000 (1690) | ~12,000 (1710)
- **Harbor:** Boston Harbor: excellent natural harbor with many islands. Good depth (6–8m in main channel). Fresh water from Charles River and springs.
- **Strategic Significance:** The largest and most commercially sophisticated port in English North America. Dominated the trade in salt fish (essential protein for Caribbean plantations and enslaved populations), barrel staves (essential for sugar hogsheads), flour, and timber to the sugar islands. This trade—fish and provisions southward, sugar and molasses northward—was the economic backbone of New England. Boston merchants were deeply involved in Caribbean commerce and some in privateering/piracy.

**NEW YORK** *(New Amsterdam 1626–1664; New York from 1664)*
- **Coordinates:** 40.714°N, 74.006°W
- **Founded:** Dutch WIC 1626; renamed New York after English conquest 1664
- **Control:** Dutch WIC 1626–1664; English/English Crown 1664–(Dutch briefly retook 1673–1674)
- **Population:** ~1,500 (1660) | ~4,000 (1680) | ~7,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** New York Bay/East River: excellent deep-water harbor (10–15m). One of the finest natural harbors on the eastern seaboard.
- **Strategic Significance:** Under Dutch control (1626–1664), New Amsterdam was a major free-trade port serving as an important smuggling channel for goods bypassing English Navigation Acts. After English conquest, New York became a key port in the illegal trade with pirates (notably Thomas Tew and the Red Sea men who operated from here) and a recipient of pirate plunder. Governor Benjamin Fletcher of New York (1692–1698) notoriously connived with pirates.

**PHILADELPHIA**
- **Coordinates:** 39.952°N, 75.165°W
- **Founded:** 1682 by William Penn and Quaker settlers
- **Control:** English (Proprietary Colony) 1682–
- **Population:** ~500 (1690) | ~5,000 (1710) | ~10,000 (1720)
- **Harbor:** Delaware River; depth adequate (5–7m). Well inland, protected. Fresh water abundant.
- **Strategic Significance:** Rapidly growing; by 1700s becoming one of the most important trading cities in English North America. Grains, flour, and provisions to Caribbean; sugar, molasses, and manufactured goods return. Quaker merchants sometimes bypassed Navigation Acts trading with French and Dutch colonies.

### 1.7 BERMUDA

**BERMUDA (ST. GEORGE'S)**
- **Coordinates:** 32.379°N, 64.678°W *(St. George's, the main 17th-century port)*
- **Founded:** Accidental wreck of the Sea Venture 1609; permanent settlement 1612
- **Control:** English/Virginia Company, then English Crown from 1684
- **Population:** ~300 (1620) | ~2,000 (1650) | ~4,000–5,000 (1700)
- **Harbor:** St. George's Harbour: sheltered natural harbor. Very shallow entrance (2–3m) making it inaccessible to large warships. Deep within—perfect for small fast vessels.
- **Strategic Significance:** Bermuda's strategic position at ~32°N, 64°W placed it in the westerlies/trade wind transition zone—ideally positioned as a waypoint between England, the Caribbean, and the American colonies. Bermudian cedar shipbuilding produced fast, lightweight sloops ideally suited to privateering and inter-island trade. Bermudian mariners were the most skilled small-craft sailors in the Atlantic; they were hired as pilots throughout the Caribbean. Many early Nassau settlers came from Bermuda. Key waypoint for ships en route England-Caribbean, and for homeward ships moving from the Caribbean up the Gulf Stream.
- **Fortification:** MODERATE. St. Catherine's Fort (1612 earthwork, rebuilt 1614 and 1700s). Gates' Fort, Smith's Fort. Given the shallow entrance, the harbor was naturally defensible.

## SECTION 2: WIND AND CURRENT MODEL

### 2.1 Trade Winds (Northeast Trades)

**Direction and Zone:**
- In the Caribbean (5°N–25°N), trades blow from the ENE to NE, approximately bearing 050°–070° True
- North of 25°N the trades give way to variable winds and the westerlies
- The trade wind belt shifts seasonally: it reaches furthest north (to ~25°N) in northern summer, retreats to ~15°N in winter
- In the open Caribbean, winds are more consistently E to ENE; near the islands they are deflected by topography

**Typical Strength:**
- Force 4–5 Beaufort (11–21 knots) in the core trade wind belt year-round
- Stronger in winter/spring (Nov–April): Force 5–6 (17–27 knots) regularly
- Lighter and more variable in summer/hurricane season (June–October): Force 3–4 (7–16 knots), with many calms

**Seasonal Variation:**
- October–April: Strongest, most reliable trades; best time for westward transatlantic sailing; NE to ENE
- May–September: Trades weaken; SE winds become more common in the southern Caribbean; ITCZ encroaches from the south; variable weather

**Navigational Implications for 1600–1720:**
- Ships from Europe used the Canaries Current south to ~12°N then west with the trades to Barbados or the Windward Islands (approx. 21–28 days Canaries-to-Barbados in good conditions)
- Homeward-bound: Ships beat north from the Caribbean to ~30–35°N to pick up the westerlies, then NE to the Azores and Europe
- The Spanish *flota* system was designed around the trades: outbound to the Caribbean via Dominica/Martinique, then worked westward island by island to the Spanish Main with the following wind and current; returned by gathering at Havana and riding the Gulf Stream and westerlies home

---

### 2.2 Hurricane Season

**Official Season:** June 1 – November 30

**Historical Understanding in 1600–1720:**
- Contemporary mariners (per William Dampier, 1700) recognized July, August, September as peak hurricane months
- 18th-century Admiral Vernon called August–September the "hurricane months"
- Insurance underwriters typically raised premiums July 15–October 15

**Statistical Peak:** Approximately September 10 (modern data; consistent with historical records)

**Frequency (historical average):** ~7 hurricanes per season; ~3 major (Category 3+)

**Geographic Impact Zones:**
- The Lesser Antilles (Barbados through the Virgin Islands) catch storms tracking WNW from the open Atlantic
- Hispaniola, Puerto Rico, Cuba are in the main hurricane tracks September–October
- Gulf of Mexico (Campeche, Veracruz) vulnerable July–October
- Bermuda (at ~32°N) catches late-season storms (September–October) as they recurve NE
- The southern Caribbean (Curaçao, Caracas coast, Trinidad, Barbados to a lesser extent) is *below* the main hurricane belt—these areas see relatively few direct hits

**Notable Historical Hurricanes in the Period:**
- 1666: Great Hurricane of Martinique (devastating)
- 1667: Hurricane destroyed a Dutch fleet attacking Barbados
- 1672: Campeche hit
- 1681: Hurricane season particularly severe
- 1695: Hurricane devastated Jamaica
- 1707: Major hurricane hit the Gulf
- The 1692 earthquake (not a hurricane, but the destruction of Port Royal) demonstrates the broader pattern of catastrophic weather and seismic events

---

### 2.3 Ocean Currents

**Caribbean Current:**
- Direction: WNW (approximately 280°–310°)
- Speed: 1–3 knots (average ~1.5 knots in the central Caribbean, accelerating to 2–3 knots near the Yucatán Channel)
- Origin: The South Equatorial Current splitting at ~10°N off the Venezuelan coast; the North Equatorial Current contributing via the Lesser Antilles passages
- Effect: Aids ships traveling WNW from the Windward Islands toward the Spanish Main and Jamaica. Ships going eastward (e.g., from Jamaica toward Barbados) must work against it

**Yucatán Current:**
- The Caribbean Current accelerates through the Yucatán Channel (between Cuba and Mexico) to 2–4 knots
- Then enters the Gulf of Mexico and circulates in the Gulf Loop Current

**Gulf Stream (Florida Current in the Straits portion):**
- Speed: 3–5 knots in the Straits of Florida (between Cuba and Florida); 2–3 knots along the US East Coast
- Direction: NNE through the Straits; then NE off the Carolinas; then splits ~40°N
- Temperature: Warm (25–30°C), noticeably warmer than surrounding water—visible to experienced navigators by water color change
- Discovery/Use: Juan Ponce de León identified it 1513; Spanish ships used it systematically by the 1540s as the standard homeward route
- Strategic significance: The single most important oceanographic feature for Spanish colonial logistics. Ships departing Havana entered the Gulf Stream and rode it N to ~35°N (off Cape Hatteras), then turned E with the westerlies for Spain

**North Equatorial Current:**
- Flows westward at 0.5–1 knot across the tropical North Atlantic at roughly 10°N–20°N
- Combines with the trade winds to carry ships from the Canaries to the Caribbean

**Guiana (North Brazil) Current:**
- Flows NW along the South American coast (4°N–10°N) at 1–3 knots
- Carries ships along the Guiana/Venezuela coast toward Trinidad and the Caribbean; also carries the ITCZ migration patterns

**Counter-Currents and Gyres:**
- Columbia-Panama Gyre: A counterclockwise eddy in the SW Caribbean (Panama/Colombia coast) that opposes the main Caribbean Current
- This makes sailing from Cartagena toward Portobelo (ENE) particularly difficult—ships must work against both the gyre and the trade winds

---

### 2.4 ITCZ / Doldrums

**Location:**
- The Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) sits approximately:
  - December–February: 3°N–5°N (well south of the main Caribbean; affects only Suriname/Guiana coast)
  - June–August: 8°N–12°N (affects the southern Caribbean—Trinidad, Tobago, Venezuela coast)
- The ITCZ brings calms, variable winds, frequent squalls, and heavy rain

**Navigational Significance:**
- Ships sailing from the Caribbean south to Guiana/Suriname or returning north from there had to cross the ITCZ
- Called the "Doldrums" by English sailors—potentially trapping sailing ships for days or weeks in windless, humid conditions
- The *volta do mar* principle (going out wide in the Atlantic to avoid the Doldrums and find favorable winds) was well understood by experienced navigators

---

### 2.5 Key Passages

**WINDWARD PASSAGE**
- **Location:** Between SE Cuba (Guantánamo Province) and NW Haiti; centered ~20°N, 74°W
- **Width:** ~80km (50 miles)
- **Depth:** 1,700m (deep water—no navigation hazards in the main channel)
- **Wind Characteristics:** NE trades funnel through, providing excellent reaching conditions for westbound ships. Current sets NW at 1–2 knots. Fast, reliable passage for ships entering the Caribbean from the NE Atlantic or for ships departing west toward Cuba and the Gulf of Mexico.
- **Historical Use:** The primary route for ships coming from European waters (via Barbados or the Windward Islands) bound for Havana, Cuba, or the Gulf of Mexico. Columbus used it on multiple voyages; it was on the main Spanish plate fleet route.

**MONA PASSAGE**
- **Location:** Between Hispaniola (W) and Puerto Rico (E); centered ~18°30'N, 67°53'W
- **Width:** ~130km (80 miles)
- **Hazards:** Variable tidal currents; sandbanks extending from both coasts; Mona Island in the middle creates additional complications
- **Wind Characteristics:** NE–E trades push through; can be confused in the lee of Hispaniola's mountains (creating wind shadows and squalls on the western side)
- **Historical Use:** The standard northern route for ships entering the Caribbean from the NE—the "normal" track for vessels arriving from the Canaries along the 20°N latitude. Also the route for ships from Puerto Rico and Hispaniola heading to the Mona Island anchorage (a useful water/provision stop).

**STRAITS OF FLORIDA**
- **Location:** Between Cuba (S) and Florida (N); approximately 23°–25.5°N, 79°–82°W
- **Width:** ~150km
- **Gulf Stream Speed:** 3–5 knots NNE
- **Wind Characteristics:** In winter, "northers" blow S/SW down the Straits and can create very rough short-period seas against the north-flowing Gulf Stream—dangerous conditions. In summer, light variable winds.
- **Historical Use:** The essential homeward route for the Spanish treasure fleet. Ships leaving Havana entered the Straits, rode the Gulf Stream NNE to ~30°–32°N, then turned E for Spain. The critical chokepoint—French, English, and Dutch corsairs stationed ships at the northern exit of the Straits to intercept Spanish homebound ships.

**ANEGADA PASSAGE**
- **Location:** Between the British Virgin Islands (W) and the northern Leeward Islands (Anguilla, St. Maarten area, E); approximately 18°30'–18°45'N, 63°–64°W
- **Width:** ~60km between Anegada and Anguilla
- **Hazards:** Anegada Island ("the Drowned Island") barely rises above sea level and is surrounded by dangerous reefs extending well offshore—the greatest single navigation hazard in the northeastern Caribbean; responsible for many wrecks
- **Wind Characteristics:** Open to Atlantic swells and trades; boisterous easterly conditions typical. Current sets WNW at 1–2 knots.
- **Historical Use:** The northernmost of the main eastern Caribbean passages; used by ships entering from north of the Windward Islands chain. Important for ships from England approaching the Leeward Islands. Experienced navigators steered well south of Anegada.

**YUCATÁN CHANNEL**
- **Location:** Between Western Cuba and Yucatán Peninsula; ~21°N, 84.5°W
- **Width:** ~200km
- **Current:** The Yucatán Current flows N at 2–4 knots through the channel (the main supply of the Gulf Loop Current and Gulf Stream)
- **Wind Characteristics:** Moderate trades in winter; light in summer; susceptible to Gulf of Mexico "northers" in winter
- **Historical Use:** The gateway between the Caribbean and the Gulf of Mexico. Ships bound from Havana to Veracruz or Campeche passed through here or along the Cuban coast.

**DOMINICA/MARTINIQUE CHANNEL ("the Saints" passage)**
- **Location:** Between Dominica (N) and Martinique (S); ~14°50'N, 61°10'W
- **Width:** ~40km; deep water throughout
- **Historical Use:** One of the main passages through the Windward Islands chain for ships arriving from Europe; strong NE trades create excellent broad-reach conditions through the channel.

## SECTION 5: GEOGRAPHIC HAZARDS

### 5.1 Major Reef Systems

**GREAT AND LITTLE BAHAMA BANKS**
- **Location:** 22°–27°N, 74°–79°W
- **Description:** Vast carbonate platform. Great Bahama Bank is essentially a submerged plateau barely 3–6m deep over an area of ~96,000 km². Appears as clear blue-green water with sudden ground visibility. The edge (a wall dropping to 2,000m+) is close to some passages. Approached at night or in poor visibility, the Banks have claimed hundreds of ships.
- **Navigation:** Experienced pilots used water color, soundings, and the smell of turtle grass to detect proximity. Ships approaching Nassau from the east had to navigate the tongue of the ocean between the two banks.

**SILVER SHOALS (ABROJOS BANK / AMBROSIAN BANK)**
- **Location:** ~20°30'N, 69°00'W — North of Hispaniola, east of the Windward Passage
- **Description:** A shallow carbonate plateau with numerous coral heads, some just below the surface. Extends approximately 100km E–W. Charts were inadequate in the 17th century.
- **Historical significance:** The 1641 Spanish *flota* (Nuestra Señora de la Concepción and others) was wrecked here in a hurricane—the silver was not recovered until the 1680s when William Phips, with an English expedition, salvaged ~32 tons of silver, netting a 10,000% return. This extraordinary treasure recovery was one of the most famous events of the period.

**SERRANILLA AND SERRANA BANKS**
- **Location:** ~15°–17°N, 79°–82°W (western Caribbean)
- **Description:** Isolated reefs far from any major island, rising abruptly from deep water. Near the main trade route from Jamaica to Portobelo/Cartagena. Responsible for numerous wrecks on ships that misjudged their westward run.

**ANEGADA ISLAND REEFS**
- **Location:** ~18°45'N, 64°20'W — northernmost of the British Virgin Islands
- **Description:** Anegada barely rises 8m above sea level and is surrounded by a coral reef extending many kilometers to the north and east. The island is nearly invisible at night or in bad weather until ships are already on the reef.
- **Historical significance:** One of the most dangerous navigation hazards in the Caribbean, responsible for more documented wrecks than almost any other site. Experienced captains gave it wide berth.

**PEDRO BANK (PEDRO CAYS)**
- **Location:** ~16°50'N–17°30'N, 76°–78°W (south of Jamaica)
- **Description:** Large isolated shallow bank about 150km south of Jamaica. Depths of 9–20m over a broad area. Coral heads in places. Not immediately dangerous to deep-draft ships but a concern for vessels driven south of Jamaica in storms.
- **Strategic:** Turtle hunting ground; occasional buccaneer watering/provisioning stop.

**JARDINES DE LA REINA (GARDENS OF THE QUEEN)**
- **Location:** ~20°45'N–21°30'N, 78°30'–82°W (south coast Cuba)
- **Description:** Labyrinthine archipelago of mangrove islands and coral reefs stretching ~350km along the southern coast of central Cuba. Depth 1–5m throughout much of the area.
- **Navigation:** Essentially impassable to deep-draft ships without local pilots. Provided hiding places for pirates and smugglers.

**LOS ROQUES ARCHIPELAGO**
- **Location:** ~11°50'N, 66°40'W (off Venezuela)
- **Description:** Coral atoll with surrounding reefs and very shallow inter-island waters. Beautiful anchorages but treacherous approaches.

**TURNEFFE ATOLL AND BELIZE BARRIER REEF**
- **Location:** ~16°–18°N, 87°–88°W (Belize coast)
- **Description:** The Mesoamerican Barrier Reef is the second longest barrier reef in the world. It runs parallel to the Belize coast approximately 30–40km offshore. The entire Belize coast between reef and shore is a labyrinth of cayes and shallow channels (1–3m).
- **Navigation:** Impenetrable to large ships without specialized knowledge. Provided sanctuary for English logwood cutters who knew the channels; Spanish warships could not pursue effectively.

### 5.2 Dangerous Passages

**TORTUGA CHANNEL (between Tortuga and Hispaniola)**
- ~20°N, 73°W; narrow, poorly charted; strong currents; used by buccaneers who knew it well, dangerous for others

**CAYMAN TRENCH APPROACHES**
- The abrupt walls of the Cayman Trough make soundings useless as depth indicators near the Caymans; the reefs around Grand Cayman appear with minimal warning

**FLORIDA REEF TRACT**
- ~24.5°–25.5°N along the Florida Keys; the shallow reef tract runs parallel to the keys inside the Gulf Stream; ships carried too far west by the Gulf Stream (or lost in northers) could pile onto the reef; the 1715 Treasure Fleet (11 ships wrecked in a hurricane near Florida) is the defining example, though just after our period ends

**DOMINICAN REPUBLIC NORTH COAST APPROACHES**
- The north coast of Hispaniola from Monte Cristi to Cap-Français has fringing reefs and shoal patches making nighttime approaches dangerous; the 1495 wreck of the Santa María nearby set the precedent

---

### 5.3 Key Storm Anchorages

**ENGLISH HARBOUR, ANTIGUA (17°00'N, 61°46'W)**
- The most famous hurricane hole in the Eastern Caribbean. Natural rock walls shield it from all directions; depth 10–14m. Ships could survive a direct hurricane here.

**PORT ANTONIO, JAMAICA (18°10'N, 76°27'W)**
- Excellent double-bay natural harbor on the NE coast; deep, sheltered; used by merchant ships avoiding storms.

**KINGSTON HARBOUR (17°58'N, 76°48'W)**
- Surrounded by the Palisadoes barrier; one of the largest naturally sheltered harbors in the Western Hemisphere.

**SAMANÁ BAY, HISPANIOLA (19°10'N, 69°20'W)**
- Large, enclosed bay on the NE coast; excellent shelter; used by Spanish ships and buccaneers; Columbus anchored here.

**GULF OF PARIA (TRINIDAD, 10°30'N, 61°30'W)**
- The inland sea between Trinidad and Venezuela; completely sheltered by land on all sides; essentially a hurricane-proof anchorage for ships caught in the southern Caribbean.

**CARENAGE (GRENADA, 12°03'N, 61°45'W)**
- Small enclosed harbor; the name itself (*carenage* = careening place) indicates its historical use.

**SPEIGHTSTOWN ROADS (BARBADOS, 13°15'N, 59°38'W)**
- The northwest coast of Barbados offered better shelter than Carlisle Bay in certain wind conditions; used as an alternative anchorage.

### 5.4 Careening Beaches and Locations

Careening (hauling a ship onto her side on a beach to scrape and caulk her hull) was essential maintenance, typically needed every 3–6 months in tropical waters where *Teredo navalis* (shipworm) and barnacles could render a wooden hull unserviceable within a year.

**CAREENING BAY / BLOODY POINT AREA, TOBAGO**
- Multiple beaches on the NW and SW coasts of Tobago. Used extensively by Dutch, English, and French vessels and by pirates during the contested colonial period. Sandy beaches with gentle slope and adequate offing.

**LITTLE CAYMAN AND CAYMAN BRAC**
- (~19°40'N, 80°04'W) Remote; long sandy beaches; turtles provided food supply. Used by English and Dutch traders and occasionally pirates.

**CORN ISLANDS (ISLAS DEL MAÍZ), NICARAGUA**
- (~12°10'N, 83°04'W) Remote from Spanish authority; English and later pirate usage. Excellent beaches.

**ISLE OF PINES (ISLA DE LA JUVENTUD), CUBA**
- (~21°45'N, 82°50'W) South of Cuba; somewhat isolated from main Spanish authority; used by corsairs careening in the Jardines de la Reina area.

**GRAND CAYMAN SOUTH COAST (VARIOUS)**
- Long sandy beaches; remote; freshwater from wells. Frequently used by English traders and buccaneers. The Caymans were essentially uninhabited in the early 17th century (first permanent settlers c.1661) but were known and used as provisioning stops.

**CAYONA BEACH, TORTUGA, HISPANIOLA**
- The main careening beach of the western buccaneers. The sheltered southern coast of Tortuga had suitable beaches and the buccaneer community provided security.

**BAIE DE SAINT-MARC / SAINT-DOMINGUE COAST**
- Various beaches on the western coast of Hispaniola used by buccaneers who initially subsisted by hunting feral cattle and pigs here.

**ROATAN, BAY ISLANDS, HONDURAS**
- (~16°22'N, 86°32'W) Well-sheltered bays; English privateers used these extensively; fresh water available; outside effective Spanish patrol range.

**ELEUTHERA AND EXUMAS, BAHAMAS**
- Long sandy beaches on isolated Bahamian islands; used by wreckers, privateersmen, and pirates; very shallow waters meant no large Spanish men-of-war could follow.

**ENGLISH HARBOUR CAREENING WHARVES, ANTIGUA**
- From the late 17th century onward, English Harbour had constructed careening wharves and later a purpose-built naval dockyard; the finest formal careening facility in the English Caribbean.

### Key Regulatory Frameworks Affecting Trade Routes (for event simulation)
- **Spanish *flota* system** (1566–1790): Annual convoys; legally restricted trade to licensed Spanish ships via Cádiz/Seville → Veracruz + Cartagena/Portobelo → Havana → home
- **English Navigation Acts** (1651, 1660, 1663): Required English colonial goods to be carried on English ships via English ports; created incentive for smuggling
- **Dutch *free trade* policy**: WIC and independent Dutch merchants traded freely with Spanish, English, and French colonies regardless of official restrictions—Curaçao and St. Eustatius were deliberate free ports
- **Treaty of Windsor (1680s) / Neutrality treaties**: Spain and England agreed to "no peace beyond the line" ceased formally by ~1670; after this, piracy was technically illegal even in peacetime; governments issued fewer letters of marque; "golden age of piracy" became technically criminal
- **Treaty of Ryswick (1697)**: Ended Nine Years' War; France formally received western Hispaniola (Saint-Domingue)
- **Treaty of Utrecht (1713)**: Ended War of the Spanish Succession; Britain received Gibraltar, Minorca, Newfoundland, Nova Scotia, Hudson Bay, St. Kitts; Spain granted Britain the *asiento* (slave trade monopoly) and "Ship of Permission" annual trade rights

## Gaps and Uncertainties

1. **Population figures for 1600–1700** are order-of-magnitude estimates only. Colonial census data is fragmentary and often counts only free white males; total populations (including enslaved Africans and indigenous peoples) were typically 3–5× the free population by 1700. Simulation should implement this as a range rather than a fixed number.

2. **Harbor depths** are given in approximate historical ranges based on modern charts and period pilot accounts; actual 17th-century depths at specific anchorages may have differed due to silting, dredging, and sea-level change.

3. **Campeche city founding**: I give 1540 (Montejo's permanent settlement); some sources say 1517 (Hernández de Córdoba's first contact) or 1531 (earlier failed settlement).

4. **Transit times** for adverse (upwind) passages vary enormously with crew skill, vessel type, and seasonal wind conditions. The given ranges are for competent, experienced mariners on well-fitted vessels. Poorly equipped ships could take 2–3× longer.

5. **Tobago colonial history** is extremely complex—the island reportedly changed hands 31 times. The periods given are approximate; more detailed year-by-year control history would require Andrews (1978) or Thornton's Caribbean history.

6. **English Harbour, Antigua** was used informally from the late 17th century but not formally established as a Royal Navy dockyard until the 1720s–1730s (Fort Berkeley 1704 is the earliest firm date). For the period 1600–1655, it was primarily used by merchant and privateering vessels.

7. **Petit-Goâve exact founding** as a settlement is unclear—buccaneer presence from c.1659–1665; formal French recognition from 1670. The coordinates given (18.433°N, 72.867°W) are approximate.

8. **Belize settlements**: The exact founding date and locations of early English logwood camps in Belize are disputed; 1638 is traditional, but some evidence suggests 1650s. There was no formal town.
