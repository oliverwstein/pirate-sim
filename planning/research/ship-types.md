## Research Report: Caribbean Ship Types, 1600–1720

### Summary

This report synthesizes data from Wikipedia maritime articles, naval rating records, buccaneer/pirate histories, and cross-referenced with the key scholarly sources (Rodger 2004, Lavery 1983–84, Davis 1962, Goldenberg 1976, Exquemelin 1678). All quantitative values are parameterized for simulation use with confidence ranges where historical variability is high. Currency throughout is in pieces of eight (PoE; 1 PoE ≈ 1 Spanish real de a ocho ≈ 0.25 English shillings in 1650, rising to ~0.27 shillings by 1700; £1 sterling ≈ 8–9 PoE for the period).

**Atlantic/Caribbean premium:** Wages for Caribbean runs ran ~20–30% higher than North Sea/Baltic runs, due to disease mortality risk and remoteness. Surgeons commanded a premium: £3–5/month (≈ 48–80 s) for Caribbean routes.

**Important note on merchant surgeon:** Most Caribbean merchant ships did not carry a dedicated surgeon; often a mate had some training. Proper surgeons commanded 2–3× ordinary seaman wage.

### 2c. Pirate Wage/Share Structure (c. 1690–1720)

Pirates operated on a **no purchase, no pay** (share/prize) system, not wages. Rediker (2004) and Johnson (1724) *A General History of the Pyrates* document this in detail:

**Typical pirate articles (confirmed for multiple crews, c. 1715–1725):**

| Role | Share of Prize |
|---|---|

| Quartermaster | 1.5 shares |
| Master Gunner | 1.25 shares |
| Carpenter/Boatswain | 1.25 shares |
| Common pirate | 1 full share |
| Boy | 0.5 share |

| Injury | Payment (PoE) |
|---|---|
| Loss of right arm | 600 PoE |
| Loss of left arm | 500 PoE |
| Loss of right leg | 500 PoE |
| Loss of left leg | 400 PoE |
| Loss of eye | 100 PoE |
| Loss of finger | 100 PoE |

*Note:* These figures are from Morgan's buccaneers (Exquemelin 1678) and golden age pirate articles (Bartholomew Roberts' articles, c. 1721, per Johnson 1724). Amounts varied by crew but the system was consistent.

**Expected prize income:** A successful pirate on a good cruise might earn the equivalent of 5–10 years' merchant wages in a single year. Bartholomew Roberts' crew reportedly divided prizes of £50,000+ (≈ 450,000 PoE). An individual share on a major prize could be 200–1,000 PoE.

## SECTION 4: CAREENING

### Frequency by Region/Vessel Type

In the Caribbean, hulls foul with barnacles, weed, and tropical shipworm (*Teredo navalis*) far faster than in northern waters:

| Vessel Type | Careening Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Sloop/Pinnace (uncoppered) | Every 2–4 months | Critical for speed; pirates careened ~quarterly |
| Brigantine/Snow/Bark (uncoppered) | Every 3–5 months | |
| Merchantman/Frigate (uncoppered) | Every 4–6 months | |
| Ship of the Line (uncoppered) | Every 4–8 months | Required careening wharf; highly disruptive |
| Any vessel (coppered, post-1780) | Every 12–18 months | Copper sheathing not yet available in 1600–1720 period |

**Note:** Copper sheathing was not introduced until the 1770s (Royal Navy) and 1780s (general). During 1600–1720, all Caribbean hulls required frequent careening.

### Duration

| Vessel Size | Careening Duration |
|---|---|
| Sloop/Pinnace | 3–7 days (beach careening; one side at a time) |
| Brigantine/Snow | 5–10 days |
| Bark/Merchantman | 7–14 days |
| Frigate | 10–21 days |
| Ship of the Line | 3–6 weeks (requires careening wharf; guns/stores removed) |

### Careening Process

1. Find sheltered beach with tidal range > 4–6 ft (harder in Caribbean's small tidal range of 1–2 ft); or use careening wharf at Port Royal, Bridgetown, etc.
2. Remove all stores, guns, ballast (~2–4 days)
3. Heel ship over with tackles from masthead to shore anchors
4. Scrape barnacles; caulk seams; apply tallow or tar mixture ("breaming")
5. Flip and repeat on other side
6. Reload stores (~2–4 days)

**Total downtime including preparation:** 1–6 weeks depending on vessel size. Ships of the line careened at sea by "Parliamentary heel" (shifting guns/ballast to one side) if no wharf was available, but this was dangerous.

**Critical bottleneck:** Masts, cordage, canvas, pitch, and iron were ALL European imports. A ship in the Caribbean that lost a mast or needed new rigging faced a major supply problem — requiring either a captured prize ship as source or a long wait for a supply convoy.

### 5c. Approximate Build Costs by Region (1650–1720)

Labour and materials varied significantly by location:

| Build Location | Cost Premium vs. English Shipyard | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| English shipyard (Thames) | Baseline | ~£5–8/ton for merchant; ~£10–15/ton for warship |
| New England | ~80–90% of English cost | Cheap timber; skilled labour available |

**Caribbean environment factor:** The *Teredo navalis* (shipworm) was catastrophically destructive to unsheathed wooden hulls in tropical Caribbean waters. A hull that would last 20 years in the English Channel might be severely compromised in 3–4 years without frequent careening and bottom treatment. This was a significant operational constraint for all Caribbean vessels.

In the Caribbean with its predictable trade winds (E/NE), windward ability was critically important for commerce raiding and escape.

- Rig types, approximate tonnage ranges, crew ratios — confirmed across multiple sources

- Pirate share/articles system — confirmed per Johnson (1724) and Rediker (2004)
- Fluyt cost/crew efficiency advantage — well documented
- Careening necessity and rough frequency — confirmed
- Ship rating system and gun counts — well documented

**Estimated / medium confidence:**
- Build costs in PoE: converted from English £ figures at 8–9 PoE/£ exchange; this rate fluctuated significantly. Treat as ±25%
- Merchant wages: Davis (1962) provides English data; Caribbean wage premium estimated at 20–30%
- Monthly operating costs: constructed from known crew sizes × wages + victualling; does not include depreciation/major repair
- Galleon crew figures: vary enormously by source; treasure fleet vs. war galleon distinction important

- French buccaneer wage/share arrangements (Exquemelin mentions some figures; check original French editions)

- Slave ship build/conversion costs for specifically 1600–1660 period — most documented data is 1750–1800
