## 1. Per-Faction Sailor Supply: Europe, 1660–1720

### 1.1 England / Great Britain

**Primary recruitment geography:** England's seamen pool concentrated in the *West Country* (Devon, Cornwall, Bristol), the *Thames estuary* (London, Harwich, Chatham), and *northern fishing ports* (Yarmouth, Hull, Newcastle). Bristol was by 1680 England's second-largest city and an active slave-trade hub; Devon ports supplied the bulk of both Royal Navy and privateer crews. West Country men feature heavily in pirate crew lists — Bartholomew Roberts was born in Pembrokeshire (Wales), Blackbeard likely in Bristol. Samuel Bellamy was born in Devon (Hittisleigh, 1689).

**Apprenticeship / training:** No formal naval academy existed for common sailors in this period. Boys entered the sea as cabin boys or via merchant apprenticeships typically at ages 10–13; by 18–20 they were rated "ordinary seamen." Able seamen with 3–5 years' experience were the scarce and contested resource.

**Impressment:** The Royal Navy used impressment beginning formally in **1664** (wartime use only, legally traced to Edward I). Eligible men were *"seafaring men aged 18–55"*. Press gangs operated from coastal ports — Portsmouth, Plymouth, Harwich, Yarmouth were prime hunting grounds. The legal power applied only to seafarers (merchant sailors, longshoremen, fishermen, collier crews); random inland impressment was illegal and rare. [VERIFIED from Wikipedia: Impressment]

*Quantitative snapshot (mid-18th century, nearest available data):*

| Period | Royal Navy | Privateers | Merchant | **Total British sailors** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1736–38 (peacetime) | 14,845/yr | — | 35,239/yr | **~50,084** |
| 1756–63 (Seven Years' War) | 74,771/yr | 3,286/yr | 37,584/yr | **~115,641** |
| 1775–83 (Am. Rev. War) | 67,747/yr | 3,749/yr | 44,947/yr | **~116,443** |

[VERIFIED: Wikipedia Impressment, citing Fischer & Nordvik 1990]

These figures are a generation after our 1660–1720 period, but the structural ratios are applicable: the total **peacetime English sailor pool was roughly 40,000–55,000 men**; wartime demand more than doubled it, creating an acute shortage of experienced seamen. The *period 1701–1714 (War of Spanish Succession)* would have seen conditions similar to the Seven Years' War column, with massive privateer commissions issued from Jamaica, Barbados, and the North American colonies supplementing the Royal Navy.

**Naval wages:** Royal Navy wages were fixed in **1653** and not raised until the Spithead mutiny of **1797** — a 144-year freeze. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Impressment, citing Rodger 1986] In our simulation period, an ordinary seaman earned approximately **19–24 shillings/month** naval pay. Six months' back pay was withheld as an anti-desertion bond; wages could run 1–2 years in arrears. Volunteer recruits received a sign-up bounty of ~2 months' wages. Merchant sailors earned **roughly equal pay in peacetime, up to double in wartime** (when risk premium and scarcity drove merchant wages up). Bart Roberts was earning **less than £4/month** (~80 shillings) as second mate on a slave ship — a reasonably senior position. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Bartholomew Roberts]

**Foreign-born sailors in English service:** Navigation Acts (1651, 1660, 1696) required ¾ of merchant crews to be English subjects — but enforcement was lax in the Caribbean, and by the 1690s-1710s mixed crews of English, Irish, Welsh, Scottish, and Dutch mariners were common on both merchant and privateer vessels.

### 1.2 Spain

**Primary recruitment geography:** Spanish colonial trade was legally controlled through the **Casa de Contratación** in Seville (later partly at Cadiz). By law, only Spanish subjects could sail the Indies trade, and only from designated Castilian ports. In practice the most skilled Spanish mariners came from the **Basque Country** (Vizcaya, Guipúzcoa) — Basque shipbuilders and pilots dominated the galleon construction industry and provided disproportionate numbers of captains and pilots. Andalusian ports (Seville, Cadiz, Huelva) provided common seamen. **Canary Islands** mariners were a secondary source; the Islands were a standard mid-Atlantic waypoint and many Canarians joined returning flota ships.

**The Flota system and crew size:** The *Flota de Indias* convoy was the backbone of Spanish colonial recruitment. A typical galleon (500–800 tons) carried 50–100 sailors, with larger escort warships (men-of-war, 60+ guns) carrying 300–500. The annual convoy from Seville/Cadiz to Veracruz + Portobelo, rendezvous at Havana, meant Spanish sailors spent 6–18 months at sea per voyage. Spanish Caribbean ports (Havana, Cartagena, Vera Cruz) had permanent harbor pilots and a small resident maritime population, but most fleet sailors were peninsular transients.

**Colonial naval forces:** Spain maintained the *Armada de Barlovento* (Windward Fleet), a permanent Caribbean squadron based at Veracruz/Havana tasked with convoy escort and anti-piracy. This squadron employed roughly 600–1,200 men year-round in the late 17th century [INFERRED from fleet size records], drawn from both peninsular assignments and Caribbean-born subjects. Recruitment to this force was a genuine colonial sailor source; local Creole sailors (Spanish-descended Caribbean-born) gradually became more prominent over the 17th century.

**Pay and incentives:** Spanish naval sailors were paid the *sueldo* (fixed monthly wage) plus a share of prize money on captures. Pirate accounts consistently describe Spanish merchant sailors as poorly armed and quick to surrender — consistent with low morale driven by irregular pay and the violence of the Indies trade.

### 1.3 France

**Primary recruitment geography:** French Atlantic sailors came overwhelmingly from **Brittany** (Saint-Malo, Brest, Nantes) and **Normandy** (Rouen, Honfleur, Dieppe). Saint-Malo was the epicenter of French privateering — the corsair tradition was deeply embedded in Breton maritime culture, and Saint-Malo produced privateers such as Jean Bart (Dunkirk-born but serving the French Crown) and later Robert Surcouf. A secondary pool came from **Basque ports** on the French side (Bayonne, Saint-Jean-de-Luz) — these Basque fishermen/mariners had worked the Grand Banks and Caribbean routes since the early 16th century and were technically proficient deep-water sailors.

**The *Ordonnance de la Marine* (1681):** Colbert's naval ordinance created the *Inscription Maritime* — a systematic registration of French seafarers who were obligated for naval service. This was France's equivalent of impressment but more formalized: coastal men registered with the Admiralty and were called up in rotation (*classes*), serving ~1 year in every 3–4. The system produced a trained reserve but was widely evaded; Caribbean colonial ports were outside its effective reach. [INFERRED from standard historiography on the French Navy]

**Colonial recruitment — Petit-Goave and Tortuga:** French buccaneers operated from Tortuga (from ~1630) and later Petit-Goave on Saint-Domingue. These were not formal naval crews but self-organized raiders. The Governor of Petit-Goave routinely issued blank *lettres de marque* (letters of marque) to buccaneers [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Privateer], creating a semi-legal privateer force that required no metropolitan recruitment. By 1663, an estimated **~1,000 buccaneers** (English, French, Dutch combined) operated from Jamaica and Tortuga in ~15 ships. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Buccaneer]

### 1.4 Dutch Republic

**Primary recruitment geography:** The Dutch sailor pool was centered on **Amsterdam**, **Zeeland (Middelburg, Flushing/Vlissingen)**, and the **North Holland** ports (Hoorn, Enkhuizen, Medemblik). The VOC and WIC chambers in each city recruited directly from their home regions. Crucially, Dutch maritime employers were far less xenophobic than the Spanish or English: the VOC routinely recruited **German, Scandinavian, and Baltic sailors** when Dutch manpower was short, and many VOC ships carried majority non-Dutch crews by the early 18th century.

**VOC scale as a reference:** Between 1602–1796, the VOC sent **nearly 1 million Europeans** to its Asian trade on 4,785 ships — roughly **5,000 men/year** on average across all routes. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Dutch East India Company] The WIC (West India Company, founded 1621) operated on a smaller scale in the Caribbean/Atlantic; Dutch Caribbean presence was concentrated at Curacao (entrepôt for contraband trade with the Spanish Main), Sint Eustatius, and Suriname.

**Curacao as a sailor node:** Curacao functioned as the Dutch entrepôt hub for the entire Caribbean basin. Its small but strategically positioned harbor attracted a cosmopolitan transient sailor population — Dutch, Jewish merchants, Sephardic traders, and sailors from across the Atlantic passed through. The permanent Dutch population was small (~1,000–2,000 in 1680) but Curacao's active trade role meant a far larger transient maritime population.

**Dutch naval manning:** The States Navy (admiralties of Amsterdam, Zeeland, Friesland, etc.) relied on a mix of volunteers and press-ganged men. Dutch and French naval desertion rates were catastrophically high — **up to 90% annually** on some ships. The Dutch vessel *Staaten Generaal* (complement 550) lost 428 men to desertion; the *Delft* (complement 350) lost 340. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Impressment, citing Frykman 2009] This structural problem meant Dutch naval strength was less reliable than British, despite competitive ship quality.

## 2. Colonial Sailor Supply: Caribbean Ports

**The fundamental dynamic:** Caribbean colonial ports did *not* generate self-sustaining sailor populations in the 17th century. They were **nodes on a transient maritime labor circuit**, not breeding grounds. The reasons:

1. **High mortality:** Disease (yellow fever, malaria, dysentery) killed European migrants at catastrophic rates. Barbados lost ~40–50% of incoming indentured servants within 4 years in the 1640s-1660s [from the Demographics research file]. The same mortality regime applied to sailors in Caribbean harbors.

2. **Economic structure:** Caribbean ports existed to export (sugar, tobacco, silver, slaves) and import (manufactured goods, food, servants). They generated demand for shipping but not a permanent mariner class until late in the period.

3. **Transient character:** Nassau (New Providence/Bahamas) circa 1713–18 had **~100 permanent inhabitants but over 1,000 pirates** using the harbor — a 10:1 transient-to-resident ratio. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Nassau, Bahamas] Port Royal Jamaica was described as the busiest Caribbean port in the 1670s-80s with buccaneers as a major economic driver, but the 1692 earthquake and subsequent decline demonstrate how dependent it was on transient maritime labor.

**Local sailor generation — what existed:**
- *Harbor pilots and boat crews:* Every port maintained a small cadre of local pilots (often mixed-race or creole in Spanish ports), small-craft operators (lighters, canoes), and harbor workers who accumulated genuine maritime skill. These 10–30 men per port constituted the stable "floor" of local maritime expertise.
- *Fishing populations:* Some smaller islands developed permanent fishing populations who provided a pool of trained boat-handlers. These men could be recruited to ships in emergencies.
- *Maroon/escaped slave maritime communities:* On Jamaica and parts of Hispaniola, escaped enslaved people with maritime skills formed communities in inaccessible coastal areas. Some became pirates or were recruited by pirates.

**Rough replenishment model for simulation:**
- A port with 5,000 population might generate **5–15 new trained sailors/year** organically (apprenticed boys reaching working age, fishermen converting to deep-sea work).
- A port's effective sailor pool is better modeled as: **(local permanent residents with seafaring skills) + (transient sailors currently in port)**.
- The transient pool fluctuates enormously: near-zero when no ships are in harbor, up to several hundred when a fleet is fitting out.
- Natural replacement of *transient* sailors depends on the port's position in trade circuits: a hub like Port Royal or Havana sees continuous sailor turnover; a backwater like Petit-Goave or Tortuga sees periodic surges when buccaneers return from raids.

## 3. Pirate Recruitment: Demographics and Sources

**Who became a pirate?** The historiography (Rediker 1987, 2004; Cordingly 1994) and primary sources (Captain Charles Johnson's *General History of the Pyrates*, 1724) converge on a clear profile:

**Primary demographic: disgruntled merchant and ex-naval sailors.** Piracy was fundamentally a labor dispute conducted at gunpoint. The most common pathway was:
1. Sailor serves on merchant or privateer ship
2. War ends → privateering commissions revoked → no steady employment
3. Former privateer turns pirate, often recruiting from his existing crew

The transition from privateer to pirate after the **Peace of Utrecht (1713)** is the best-documented example. Privateers who had operated from Jamaica, Barbados, and North America during Queen Anne's War (1701–1714) "turned en masse to piracy" after the treaty. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Golden Age of Piracy] Blackbeard "had often distinguished himself for his uncommon boldness and personal courage" as a privateer sailing from Jamaica during the war before moving to Nassau and joining Hornigold ~1716. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Blackbeard] The Nassau pirate republic (~1713–18) was almost entirely populated by ex-Queen Anne's War privateers.

**Secondary demographic: forced recruits.** Pirates regularly forced skilled specialists — carpenters, surgeons, navigators — to remain with the crew. Bart Roberts himself was *forced* into piracy when Howell Davis captured the slave ship *Princess* on the Gold Coast (1719). [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Bartholomew Roberts] Skilled men who were forced often became willing converts once they experienced the share system. Unskilled forced men were typically released.

**Tertiary demographic: escaped enslaved people and freedmen.** The *Whydah* crew is explicitly described as including "freed African slaves, displaced English seamen, Native Americans, and a scattering of social outcasts from Europe and elsewhere." [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Whydah Gally] Estimates from the trial records of the *Whydah* survivors suggest roughly **25–30% of Bellamy's crew were of African origin**. On some pirate ships this proportion was higher: Bartholomew Roberts' articles explicitly addressed the status of "negro" crew members (they received equal shares). [INFERRED from Rediker 2004]

**Crew size ranges:**
- Small sloop (6 guns): ~30–70 men (Blackbeard's early sloop, 1716–17: "6 guns and about 70 men" per contemporary report) [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Blackbeard]
- Medium brigantine/sloop: 80–120 men
- Large ship converted to pirate use: Blackbeard's *Queen Anne's Revenge* (40 guns): **300+ crew** [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Blackbeard]
- Bartholomew Roberts at peak: multiple ships with ~500+ combined crew (later 400 prizes captured)
- Samuel Bellamy's fleet at peak: flagship *Whydah* (150 crew assigned) plus 4 escort vessels [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Whydah Gally]

**Motivational framing (primary source, Roberts in Johnson 1724):**
> *"In an honest service there is thin commons, low wages, and hard labour. In this, plenty and satiety, pleasure and ease, liberty and power."*

This statement — comparing merchant service (£2–4/month, poor food, brutal conditions) to pirate life (no wages but full share of prizes, democratic governance) — encapsulates the recruitment pitch. Pirates offered a fundamentally different economic model: **no wages, but a lottery share of prize money** that could produce a year's legitimate wages in a single successful capture.

## 4. Privateer Recruitment: Pay Structure and Mechanics

**Letters of marque and crews:** A privateer captain obtained a *lettre de marque* (letter of marque) from a colonial governor or metropolitan authority. The commission specified enemy nationality and duration. Investors (*owners* or *owners-syndicate*) provided the ship and fitting-out costs. Crews were recruited at port with handbills and tavern canvassing. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Letter of Marque, Privateer]

**Pay structure — share system:**
- **No base wage** on most privateers (unlike naval or merchant service)
- Prizes divided by percentage: typically **10% to the Crown** (via Admiralty), **30–40% to ship's owners**, **10–20% to captain**, remainder split among crew by rank/rating
- A common seaman's share might equal **2–6 months' merchant wages** for a single good capture, or zero for a fruitless voyage
- Disability compensation ("no prey, no pay" but "Jamaica Discipline" privateers codified injury scales: loss of right arm = 600 pieces of eight; left arm = 500; right leg = 500; left leg = 400; eye = 100) [INFERRED from buccaneer articles describing Morgan's raids; formalized in later pirate articles]

**Crew size vs. merchantmen:** Privateers carried **3–4× the crew of an equivalent merchant vessel** because they needed fighting men to board prizes and crew captured ships. This created intense labor demand during wartime, directly competing with both the Royal Navy and merchant trade for the same pool of experienced seamen.

**Recruitment speed:** An experienced privateer captain with a known record could fill a 60-man crew in 1–2 weeks at a Caribbean port during wartime, since unemployed seamen were abundant and the share system was attractive. In peacetime or after a string of failures, the same captain might wait months. Henry Morgan assembled **~2,000 buccaneers for the Panama raid (1671)** across multiple ships within a few months — demonstrating the scalability of the share-based system when reputation was high. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Henry Morgan]

## 5. Recruitment Friction and the Labor Market

**The core tension:** The British sailor pool (the most studied) was roughly **40,000–55,000 in peacetime**, expanding to **100,000–120,000 in major wartime** — but the wartime expansion took 1–2 years to materialize and was filled partly by naval impressment of merchant sailors, partly by new volunteers, and partly by accepting landsmen at reduced ratings. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Impressment data table]

**Post-war demobilization surges:**
The clearest quantitative case is after the War of Spanish Succession (ended Treaty of Utrecht, April 1713). England alone demobilized roughly **40,000–60,000** naval and privateer sailors [INFERRED: wartime naval figures + privateer commissions in Caribbean]. Most of these men flooded into ports simultaneously looking for merchant berths. The result:
- Merchant wages fell sharply as labor supply exceeded demand
- Significant fraction of men "on the account" (turned pirate) or moved into Atlantic fishing
- Nassau became a pirate haven almost overnight (1713–15)
- The 1715 Spanish treasure fleet disaster off Florida attracted additional fortune-hunters (Bellamy, Hornigold, Henry Jennings), amplifying the pirate surge

**Signs a captain used to gauge labor availability:**
1. **Tavern credit:** If sailors were running tabs at taverns rather than paying cash, they were broke and available
2. **Wage offers:** When going rates at the quayside dropped below the 20-shilling/month benchmark, surplus labor was available; when captains had to offer 30+ shillings or advance pay, labor was tight
3. **Petition lists:** Colonial governors' records show sailors petitioning for relief pay when unemployed — a leading indicator of labor surplus
4. **Ship availability vs. crew:** A captain could observe other ships sitting at anchor, fitted out but unable to sail for want of crew — a signal of labor scarcity

**Geographic friction:** Caribbean ports faced a structural disadvantage vs. European home ports. European-born sailors were always a transient visit (one or two voyages); they did not settle. This meant a Caribbean captain recruiting crew had to:
- Accept whoever was currently in port (limited choice)
- Pay a **tropical risk premium** of 20–40% above Atlantic rates [INFERRED from standard historiography; tropical mortality justified this]
- Or recruit from local creole/mixed-race populations, free Blacks, or escaped enslaved people — which many captains did, especially in smaller ports

**"No peace beyond the Line":** The old doctrine that European peace treaties didn't apply in the Caribbean (loosely in force until the 1670s) created persistent privateering/quasi-piracy even in nominal peacetime. This gave Caribbean-based captains a continuous extralegal option for employment, blurring the merchant/privateer/pirate distinction for individual sailors.

## 6. Mortality and Replenishment Rates

**Voyage mortality — the 50% estimate:** Wikipedia's Impressment article notes that *"for major voyages, shipowners and governments routinely estimated that 50% of the sailors would die due to scurvy."* [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Impressment, citing Price 2017:12–23] This is the extreme case for transoceanic voyages without fresh provisions. More practical estimates for Atlantic/Caribbean routes:

| Route type | Estimated crew mortality | Primary cause |
|---|---|---|
| Europe–Caribbean single voyage (6–10 weeks) | 3–8% | Storm, accident, disease at Caribbean port |
| Caribbean station (12 months) | 20–35% | Yellow fever, malaria, dysentery |
| Slave ship triangle voyage (~12 months) | 15–25% | Disease, violence, accident |
| Pacific circumnavigation / Pirate Round | 40–60% | Scurvy, disease, battle |

[INFERRED: consistent with mortality data in the Demographics research file for Caribbean populations; verified against slave ship mortality estimates in the literature]

**Caribbean port mortality is the dominant factor**, not voyage mortality. Yellow fever killed newly-arrived Europeans at ~30–50% in the first two years of residence ("seasoning"); those who survived became partially immune. This means:
- A crew of 100 arriving in Port Royal from England would lose ~20–30 men in the first Caribbean season
- Replacements had to come from the same small pool of "seasoned" local sailors

**Desertion as a form of "loss":** British naval desertion averaged **25% annually**. [VERIFIED: Wikipedia Impressment, citing Rodger 1986] French and Dutch rates were higher — up to 90% on some Dutch ships. For Caribbean merchant crews, desertions of 10–20% per voyage were common; sailors who liked a port simply walked off. This is economically equivalent to mortality for crew-pool modeling purposes.

**Replenishment timescale:**
- A port that loses 30% of its transient sailor pool (disease + desertion) will replenish to normal levels within **1–3 months** if it is on an active trade route (next ship arrives, some sailors stay)
- For an isolated port off the main trade lanes, replenishment might take **6–18 months** (wait for the annual flota or next merchant voyage)
- A pirate port like Nassau replenished its floating population **opportunistically**: every captured ship added potential recruits; every prize condemned at a friendly port brought new men

## 7. Simulation Calibration Parameters

Based on the above, the following parameter ranges are suggested for the pirate-sim port sailor pool model:

### 7.1 Port sailor pool — base generation rate
| Port type | Organic new sailors/year | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Major European hub port (Seville, Amsterdam, Bristol) | 500–2,000/yr | Apprentice programs, fishing → deep sea conversion |
| Major Caribbean entrepôt (Port Royal, Havana, Curacao) | 20–60/yr | Mostly creole/harbor pilots; bulk is transient |
| Small colonial port (Tortuga, Petit-Goave, Nassau) | 5–15/yr | Almost entirely transient visitors |
| Buccaneer/pirate haven (Nassau 1713–18) | Negative organic (high desertion) | Grows only via ships arriving |

### 7.2 Trade-route multiplier for transient pool
Each merchant ship arriving at a port brings some sailors who linger, desert, or seek work:
- Small sloop (30–50 tons): 6–10 crew, ~1–2 may remain available
- Brigantine (80–150 tons): 12–20 crew, ~2–4 may remain
- Large merchantman (200–400 tons): 20–40 crew, ~3–8 may remain
- Naval vessel in port refit: 50–300 crew, ~5–20% may desert/become available

### 7.3 Faction-specific crew recruitment rates
| Faction | Peacetime fill rate | Wartime fill rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| England (merchant/privateer) | 1–2 weeks (familiar ports), 2–4 weeks (Caribbean) | 3–7 days (press gangs active) | Impressment dramatically accelerates wartime |
| Spain | 2–6 weeks (must wait for flota season) | Same | Restricted to Spanish subjects; fewer local options |
| France | 2–4 weeks (Inscription Maritime reserve) | 1–2 weeks | Corsair culture; Brittany/Basque reserves |
| Dutch | 1–3 weeks (cosmopolitan; accepts foreigners) | 1–2 weeks | High desertion risk offsets fast recruitment |
| Pirate/Buccaneer | 1–4 weeks (word of mouth, reputation) | Variable | Share system attracts; reputation drives surges |

### 7.4 Post-war demobilization event
When a major war ends (e.g., Peace of Utrecht 1713), model a **sailor surplus shock**:
- Caribbean ports receive a **+200–400% temporary surplus** of unemployed sailors over 6–18 months
- Piracy probability for individual sailors increases dramatically (historically: Nassau went from 0 to 1,000+ pirates in ~24 months)
- Merchant wages drop 20–30% (labor surplus depresses wages)
- Recovery to pre-war equilibrium takes approximately **3–5 years** as sailors disperse into other trades, die, or emigrate

### 7.5 Mortality rates (annual, Caribbean service)
| Crew type | Annual mortality % | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Freshly arrived European sailor | 25–40% | "Seasoning" yellow fever + other disease |
| Seasoned Caribbean sailor | 8–15% | Residual disease + accident + combat |
| Naval crew on Caribbean station | 15–25% | Disease-dominant; scurvy if poorly supplied |
| Pirate crew (active raiding) | 10–20% (non-combat) + combat variable | Violent death risk adds ~5–15%/yr during active raiding |

## Research Gaps and Uncertainties

1. **Spanish crew wage data**: Precise wage rates for Spanish colonial naval and merchant sailors in 1660–1720 are not readily available from Wikipedia sources. Spanish naval pay structure needs verification from: Haring, *Trade and Navigation between Spain and the Indies* (1918); or AGI (Archivo General de Indias) secondary sources.

2. **French privateer pay scales**: The specific share ratios used by French corsairs operating from Saint-Malo vs. Petit-Goave are not verified here. The French *ordonnance* system deserves a dedicated search.

3. **Dutch WIC crew lists**: The WIC's Caribbean operations are less documented than the VOC's; specific crew sizes for Curacao-based Dutch merchant vessels (~1660–1720) would improve the Dutch Caribbean port model.

4. **Slave sailor recruitment**: Some enslaved men served as sailors on both legal and pirate ships. The proportion is uncertain — estimates of 25–30% African-origin on some pirate crews may be high for the broader period; more systematic data from trial records (Nassau piracy trials, 1718–1724) would help.

5. **Seasonal patterns**: The hurricane season (June–November) effectively closed Caribbean sailing routes for large ships. The simulation should model this as a **periodic sailor pool accumulation** in port during hurricane season, followed by a departure surge in November–December. Specific timing data is in the Navigation Methods research file.
