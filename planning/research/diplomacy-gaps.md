## GAP 1: Anglo-Spanish War (1625–1630) — Caribbean Theater

**Previously:** Synthesized only from adjacent sources; specific fleet deployments and Caribbean engagement details were vague.

### Overview

The Anglo-Spanish War of 1625–1630 was primarily a European conflict, but it had one major, documented Caribbean engagement — the **Battle of St. Kitts (1629)** — plus the indirect Caribbean consequence of driving English interest toward permanent forward bases in the region.

**Dates:** 1625–1630 (declared by Charles I in 1625; Treaty of Madrid November 15, 1630)
**Belligerents:** England + Dutch Republic vs. Spain

### Principal Caribbean Engagement: Battle of St. Kitts, 1629

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Date** | 17 June – 7 September 1629 |
| **Spanish commander** | Admiral Fadrique Álvarez de Toledo Osorio |
| **Spanish sub-commander** | Antonio de Oquendo |
| **Spanish strength** | ~4,000 men, 20 galleons (*Armada de Sotavento* en route to New Spain) |
| **English defender** | Thomas Warner (Governor, St. Kitts) |
| **English/French settlers** | ~3,000 combined |
| **Result** | Spanish victory — complete |

**Sequence of events:**
- Spanish fleet dropped anchor at **Nevis** first; captured and destroyed several English ships anchored there; soldiers ashore demolished structures and captured settlers
- Irish Catholic indentured servants on Nevis swam out to the Spanish ships crying "Liberty, joyful Liberty" — preferred Catholic Spain to Protestant plantation owners (source: Paquette/Engerman, *The Lesser Antilles in the Age of European Expansion*, 1996, p. 93)
- Spanish moved to **Saint Kitts** September 7, 1629 and burned the entire settlement
- **Materiel captured:** 9 English ships, 171 artillery pieces; ~3,100 prisoners
- ~700 colonists transported back to England on Spanish-allotted shipping; 200–400 evaded capture in the hills
- After Treaty of Madrid (November 15, 1630): Spanish departed; fugitives returned; colonization resumed

**Simulation significance:** The Spanish *Armada de Sotavento* (Windward Fleet) routinely patrolled Caribbean approaches to New Spain. Encounters with English/French settlements were standing orders. The 1629 action shows: fleet of 20 galleons + 4,000 men could easily liquidate a 3,000-settler colony; the settlers were not military opposition.

### The Cádiz Expedition (1625) — Fleet Specifics

The main English offensive operation of the war was not in the Caribbean but at **Cádiz**:
- **English fleet:** ~100 ships, ~15,000 sailors and soldiers (October 1625)
- **Dutch allies:** 15 additional warships under William of Nassau to guard the English Channel
- **Commander:** Sir Edward Cecil (1st Viscount Wimbledon), appointed by the Duke of Buckingham
- **Outcome:** Catastrophic failure; English wasted time, city's modernized defenses held, troops undisciplined; fleet returned December 1625 without prize

The Cádiz failure mattered for the Caribbean because:
1. England's weak naval performance convinced no Pacific expedition was possible
2. Led directly to the **Providence Island Company** (chartered 1629, colony established 1630) as an alternative, indirect strategy against Spanish Caribbean trade
3. Parliament's disgust at the expedition's failure contributed to the constitutional crisis that eventually led to Civil War — with direct Caribbean consequences (see Gap 5)

### The Providence Island Company — Simulation Mechanics Connector

The Providence Island Company (chartered 1629) was founded by the same Puritan lords who would later lead the Parliamentary opposition:
- **Investors included:** Robert Rich (Earl of Warwick), William Fiennes (Lord Saye and Sele), Robert Greville (Lord Brooke), John Pym, John Hampden
- **Initial capitalization:** ~£200 per member × 20 members = ~£4,000; recapitalized with additional £100,000 in 1637 when prospects brightened
- **Privateering arrangement:** Islands functioned as privateer bases; colonists paid **one-fifth (20%) of all plunder** to the Company; Company had tacit royal agreement (despite official Spanish neutrality) to pay its own expenses
- **Spanish capture:** Adm. Francisco Díaz Pimienta destroyed colony May 1641

**Simulation note:** The Providence Island Company model is the cleanest example of a *commercial entity operating as a proxy war corporation* — structurally similar to the WIC but English and Puritan. It represents the template later followed at Port Royal. The 20% rake-off rate is the most concrete "company privateer fee" figure available.

### What's Still Missing

- Specific Caribbean letters of marque issued by England *against Spain* during 1625–1630 (names of captains, ships, bond amounts)
- Whether the Dutch WIC ran concurrent operations in the Caribbean during the Anglo-Spanish war years that directly impacted the English theater
- English casualty figures at St. Kitts/Nevis 1629

## GAP 2: Third Anglo-Dutch War Caribbean Operations (1672–1674)

**Previously:** Rate-limit failures; only indirect coverage via Franco-Dutch War material.

### Context and Complication

The Third Anglo-Dutch War (March 27, 1672 – February 19, 1674) was fought simultaneously with the broader **Franco-Dutch War (April 1672 – September 1678)**. England fought alongside France against the Dutch Republic for its two-year span, then Parliament forced Charles II to make peace (Treaty of Westminster, February 19, 1674), while France continued fighting until 1678. This creates a complex Caribbean picture: England's Caribbean theater was brief, while France's continued.

**English Caribbean theater (March 1672 – February 1674):**
- The primary theater was the North Sea/English Channel; the Caribbean was genuinely secondary during England's involvement
- **No major English fleet deployment to the Caribbean** during this specific war is documented
- English colonial governors held letters of marque authority against the Dutch, but the Second Anglo-Dutch War had already exhausted the main Dutch Caribbean possessions

**What did happen in the Caribbean:**

### Tobago — The Key Caribbean Focus

Tobago changed hands repeatedly during and around this period:
- **1666:** English captured Tobago from Dutch brothers Adrian and Cornelius Lampsins (who held the island under title of *Barons of Tobago* since 1662)
- **1673:** Dutch Adrian Lampsins briefly recaptured Tobago
- **1673:** English Sir Tobias Bridge retook Tobago; Adrian Lampsins killed in battle
- **1672–1674:** During the brief English rule period, plantation culture began — sugar, cotton, indigo; enslaved Africans imported
- After England's exit from the war via Treaty of Westminster (February 19, 1674): the Franco-Dutch War continued; France eventually secured Tobago

### De Ruyter's Caribbean Operations

The Third Anglo-Dutch War's main Caribbean-adjacent naval action was **Dutch Admiral Michiel de Ruyter's operations** in the wider Atlantic theater. During the Second Anglo-Dutch War, de Ruyter had swept the West African coast and appeared off Barbados (1665). In 1672–1674, de Ruyter was focused on the North Sea battles (Solebay, June 1672; four battles June-July 1673 against Anglo-French fleet). His fleet did not deploy to the Caribbean during the Third Anglo-Dutch War.

### Franco-Dutch War Caribbean Operations (the continuation after 1674)

After England exited the war, French Admiral **Jean II d'Estrées** became the dominant Caribbean naval figure:
- **Battle of Tobago (1677):** D'Estrées' French fleet engaged Dutch fleet off Tobago — French eventually secured the island
- French operations continued against Dutch Curaçao and remaining Dutch Caribbean possessions through 1678
- **Treaties of Nijmegen (September 1678):** Dutch ceded Tobago to France; Dutch Caribbean holdings reduced

**Simulation significance for the 1672–1674 period specifically:**
- English colonial governors with letters of marque against Dutch (issued in 1665 context) were active but most did not fight — many privateers did not take up Dutch commissions (the Henry Morgan article confirms Morgan did not take up letters of marque against the Dutch in 1665 when authorized; other privateers similarly reluctant)
- Morgan's Panama raid (January 28, 1671) was the *aftermath* of the Second Anglo-Dutch War period, technically in the interwar period before the Third Anglo-Dutch War began
- The real Caribbean action of this era (1672–1678) was **French vs. Dutch**, not English vs. Dutch; England's Caribbean governors were largely inactive as combatants after Treaty of Westminster

### What's Still Missing

- Specific documented English Caribbean engagements during the narrow 1672–1674 window (if any beyond Tobago)
- Pares (1936) and Haring (1910) on this specific period remain unverified at page-level; these would likely clarify whether any significant English Caribbean naval action occurred
- Names of specific English ships or captains in any 1672–1674 Caribbean letters of marque

## GAP 3: Nine Years' War in the Caribbean — Granular Campaign Details

**Previously:** Wikipedia's dedicated `Nine_Years'_War_in_the_Caribbean` article was rate-limited; coverage synthesized from adjacent sources.

*Note: The Wikipedia page at that exact URL does not exist (returned 404). The information below is drawn from the Raid on Cartagena (1697) article, the Jean-Baptiste du Casse article, the Henry Every article, and adjacent sources.*

### War Dates and Structure

**European:** May 1689 – September 20, 1697 (Peace of Ryswick signed)
**Caribbean:** Effectively concurrent; first major Caribbean action late 1689

**Key French commanders in the Caribbean:**
- **Jean-Baptiste du Casse** — Governor of Saint-Domingue from October 1, 1691
- **Bernard Desjean, Baron de Pointis** — French naval commander, Caribbean theater
- **Jean Fantin** — French privateer, early operations

### Du Casse's Operations: Documented Chronology

| Date | Action | Details |
|------|--------|---------|
| June 1689 | Attack on Surinam | Du Casse attacked Berbice and Fort Zeelandia (Surinam) — Dutch territory |
| 1689 | Attack on Saint Kitts | Du Casse attacked alongside Jean Fantin; William Kidd (then a French-English privateer) mutinied from Fantin's crew during this action |
| 1691 | Appointed Governor | Du Casse becomes first Governor of Saint-Domingue, October 1, 1691 |
| 1692 | Attack on Port Royal | Du Casse's forces raided Port Royal immediately after it was devastated by the June 7 earthquake — a uniquely opportunistic attack on a city in ruins |
| 1694 | Attack on Jamaica | Du Casse's forces raided eastern parishes of Jamaica; burned plantations, carried off ~1,300 enslaved people; forces subsequently **defeated at Carlisle Bay** and withdrew |
| 1694 | Attacks on Martinique environs | English Commodore Wilmot raided Martinique and Guadeloupe in counter-operation |

### Raid on Cartagena, 1697 — Detailed Breakdown

This is the most operationally significant single action of the Nine Years' War in the Caribbean.

**Planning phase:**
- By 1695, French Navy had declined to the point it could no longer face Anglo-Dutch fleet in open battle → shifted to *guerre de course* (commerce raiding)
- Baron de Pointis successfully argued to Louis XIV for a bold attack on Cartagena, the richest city in the Spanish Caribbean

**Fleet composition (Pointis):**
| Component | Count |
|-----------|-------|
| Capital ships (ships of the line) | 7 |
| Frigates | 3 |
| Smaller vessels | Several (unspecified number) |
| Soldiers | ~1,200 |
| Buccaneers (contributed by du Casse) | ~650 |
| **Total combatants** | **~1,850** |

**Timeline:**
- **January 7, 1697:** Fleet departed Brest, France
- **March 3, 1697:** Fleet arrived Saint-Domingue
- Pointis requested du Casse's assistance; du Casse agreed only reluctantly (preferred an attack on Portobelo)
- **~April 1697:** Combined fleet of ~1,850 appeared before Cartagena
- **~April/May 1697:** French captured both outer fortresses with only 60 men lost
- **May 6–24, 1697:** French plundered the city; total loot estimated at **10–20 million livres**
  - (Note: the existing diplomacy.md states "~8–10 million livres"; the Wikipedia Raid article gives 10–20 million; these are not reconciled in sources)
  - **Pointis's share for Louis XIV:** 2 million livres (delivered to France)
  - **Pointis's personal enrichment:** Immense (the "rest" per Wikipedia)

**The buccaneer betrayal:**
- Pointis sailed directly for France without distributing the buccaneers' promised **20% share** of the loot
- Outraged buccaneers returned independently to Cartagena and **sacked the already-weakened city a second time** — this time with rape, extortion, and murder (without Pointis's moderating influence)
- Du Casse sailed to France personally to claim the buccaneers' share from Louis XIV directly
- **Du Casse's compensation:** 1.4 million francs paid by Louis XIV as substitute for the buccaneers' promised share
- **Du Casse's promotion:** Admiral; made Chevalier in the Order of Saint Louis

**Return journey:**
- English Admiral John Nevell sent from Cádiz to intercept Pointis; three-day chase
- Nevell caught only one French ship — a hospital ship infected with yellow fever
- Yellow fever spread through English and Dutch pursuit fleets: **1,300 English sailors killed, 6 captains dead, Nevell himself died**; only one captain in the Dutch squadron survived
- French losses also severe to yellow fever, but Pointis reached France

**Simulation significance:**
- The buccaneer betrayal mechanic is documented and specific: the promised share was 20% of total loot; when denied, buccaneers executed independent re-sack. This is a clean precedent for "faction betrayal → independent action" game mechanics
- The 1.4 million francs compensation figure is the only documented case of a metropole *retroactively paying* buccaneers for a crown operation that cheated them

### St. Kitts, 1689 — The Opening Caribbean Action

- **July 1689:** French Governor du Parquet's forces (from Martinique, under du Casse's strategic direction) overran the **English half of St. Kitts**
- ~8,000 English colonists expelled or fled
- St. Kitts remained French until Peace of Ryswick (1697) — when it was partially restored to joint Anglo-French status

**Communication lag for Ryswick:** Peace of Ryswick signed **September 20, 1697**. The existing diplomacy.md notes 6–8 weeks lag to the Caribbean. This would put Caribbean reception at approximately **late November 1697** — consistent with historical pattern. See Gap 7 for fuller analysis.

## GAP 4: Western Design / 1655 Jamaican Expedition — Fleet Composition and Orders

**Previously:** Rate-limited; coverage synthesized from Jamaica history and Port Royal articles.

### Complete Fleet and Force Composition

The Western Design is now fully documented via the dedicated Wikipedia article.

**Naval component (Admiral William Penn):**
| Component | Count |
|-----------|-------|
| Warships | 18 |
| Transport vessels | 20 |
| **Total ships** | **38** |

This was **the largest English fleet ever sent to the Caribbean** at that date.

**Land component (General Robert Venables):**
| Source of troops | Count | Quality |
|-----------------|-------|---------|
| Soldiers sailed from England | 2,500 | Mostly untrained recruits; "relatively few were willing to serve in an area notorious for sickness and disease" |
| Volunteers raised in Barbados, Montserrat, Nevis, St Kitts | 3,000–4,000 | Untrained, poorly disciplined |
| **Total troops (6 regiments of ~1,000 each)** | **~5,500–6,500** | |

*Note: The infobox on the Wikipedia Western Design article states "8,000 troops" total, which is consistent with the 5 English regiments brought up to strength + a sixth regiment from Caribbean recruits.*

**Leadership:**
- **Admiral William Penn** — fleet command
- **General Robert Venables** — land forces
- **Daniel Searle** — Governor of Barbados, participated as advisor
- **Edward Winslow** — civilian commissioner (former Governor of Plymouth Colony); died during the expedition (Barbados, May 1655)
- **Gregory Butler** — second civilian commissioner
- These civilian commissioners' instruction that troops must *not* plunder but preserve colonies intact for colonization destroyed morale

**Strategic planning and Cromwell's orders:**
- Council of State first discussed "the Design" **June 1654**
- Primary intelligence came from **Thomas Gage** — Dominican friar turned Protestant, ~decade in New Spain; Gage assured them Hispaniola and Cuba were "weakly defended and could easily be seized" — this proved *incorrect*
- Cromwell's goal: seize a permanent Caribbean base to threaten Spanish silver routes; part of broader Protestant foreign policy goal of wresting the Americas from Catholic Spain
- Dutch refused to join despite Cromwell's offer of Protestant union between the two republics

**Timeline:**
| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| December 1654 | Fleet left Portsmouth |
| January 1655 | Arrived Barbados; additional Caribbean troops recruited |
| April 1655 | **Attack on Hispaniola repulsed** — heavy casualties from disease + Spanish guerrilla fighting; the English army was routed multiple times |
| May 19–27, 1655 | **Jamaica seized** — Spanish garrison (2,400 men under Juan Ramírez de Arellano and Bernardino de Meneses) retreated inland; Spanish freed their enslaved Africans → these became the **Maroons** |
| Late 1655 | Penn and Venables returned to England on separate ships (each hoping to blame the other); both charged with desertion, dismissed from service |

**Spanish defense of Jamaica:** The island's garrison was ~1,500 men when the English arrived with 8,000. The garrison did not fight in the field but conducted guerrilla resistance from the mountains; some Spanish garrisons held out for years. The freed Maroons (formerly enslaved Africans) continued guerrilla resistance against English control for decades after 1655.

**What went wrong:**
1. Joint command between Penn and Venables generated immediate friction; they "regarded each other with distrust and suspicion" from the start
2. Troops were underfed, undertrained, and demoralized
3. Civilian commissioner prohibition on plunder removed the economic incentive for Caribbean recruits
4. The attack on Hispaniola (April 1655) was the wrong strategic target; Gage's intelligence was false; the Spanish had reinforced
5. Jamaica was a consolation prize, not the intended conquest — but it proved far more valuable in the long run

**Simulation significance:**
- Penn's fleet composition (18 warships + 20 transports = 38 ships) is the concrete English naval deployment baseline for a major Caribbean expedition in this period
- The civilian commissioner role — with authority to override military plunder orders — is a documented mechanic for metropolitan control constraining local operators
- The Cromwellian model: the Crown sends agents with conflicting authorities, creating operational paralysis; this recurs throughout the period

## GAP 5: English Civil War in Barbados — Autonomy vs. Metropole

**Previously:** Wikipedia's dedicated page on this topic does not exist as a standalone article (both "English Civil War in Barbados" and "Barbados in the English Civil War" return 404).

Coverage below is synthesized from: the Barbados Wikipedia article, the Thomas Modyford article, and the Western Design article. Additional detail from Modyford's Barbados period.

### Barbados's Factional Position (1642–1652)

Barbados occupies a unique position in the Civil War period: it was wealthy enough to sustain genuine neutrality and distant enough that Parliament could not immediately enforce compliance.

**Key figures:**
- **Governor Philip Bell** (in office 1641–1650): Declared and maintained effective neutrality; refused to enforce either side's trade restrictions; Barbados traded with Dutch merchants, Royalist ships, and Parliamentary ships indiscriminately
- **Lord Francis Willoughby** (5th Baron Willoughby of Parham): The Royalist governor who replaced Bell; attempted to make Barbados a Royalist stronghold
- **Thomas Modyford**: Future Governor of Jamaica; arrived Barbados 1647 with £1,000 down payment and £6,000 credit, rapidly built fortune from sugar; rose to Speaker of the House of Assembly; initially sided with Willoughby's Royalist faction

### The Barbados Neutrality Period (~1643–1650)

During Bell's governorship:
- Barbados functioned as a de facto free port — traded with all comers regardless of English political divisions
- Dutch merchants had by the 1640s largely replaced English as primary trade intermediaries for Barbadian sugar
- The island's planter class had an interest in neutrality that transcended English political loyalties: their revenue depended on Dutch credit and Dutch shipping
- This period is the clearest Caribbean example of a colonial assembly using distance and economic importance as leverage against the metropole

### Parliament's Response: Navigation Acts and Force

- **Navigation Act of 1650**: Parliament attempted to cut Royalist colonies (Barbados, Antigua, Bermuda, Virginia) off from Dutch trade
- When Willoughby made Barbados explicitly Royalist, Parliament sent a fleet under **Admiral Sir George Ayscue** to force submission

### Submission of Barbados (January 11, 1652)

**Ayscue's fleet:** Arrived with sufficient force to impose a blockade
**Modyford's switch:** When Ayscue's fleet arrived, Modyford **switched sides** — deserted Willoughby and the Royalist faction, negotiated with Parliament's commissioners

**The Articles of Capitulation (January 1652):**
Key terms negotiated (not surrendered):
- Barbados retained its local assembly and significant self-governance
- Planters' property (including enslaved persons) remained untouched
- Freedom of trade was protected (with the important exception that Dutch trade would be restricted by the 1651 Navigation Act)
- No punishment of Royalist sympathizers

**Significance for simulation mechanics:**
The 1652 Articles established a precedent that Caribbean colonies could **negotiate** the terms of their submission rather than simply capitulate. Barbados traded compliance for autonomy guarantees. This is distinct from simple conquest — the colony's economic importance forced Parliament to bargain. Modyford personally used the transition to secure his own position; his flexibility (switching sides) was rewarded with the Governorship of Barbados appointment he negotiated with the Parliamentary Commissioners.

### Modyford's Barbados Career as Governor Prototype

Before he became Jamaica's most important governor, Modyford's Barbados period established his operational pattern:
- **1651:** Sided with Willoughby (Royalists)
- **1652:** Switched to Parliament, negotiated the Barbados submission — a betrayal that was politically rewarded
- **1660 (Restoration):** Awkward position — he had aligned with Parliament; "put him in an awkward position with the Restoration of the English monarchy"
- Used family connections (Duke of Albemarle) to navigate the transition; appointed Governor of Jamaica 1664

**Simulation significance:** Modyford is a case study in a colonial politician who *survived multiple regime changes* by switching allegiances at the optimal moment and cultivating patron relationships at the metropolitan level. His Barbados career establishes that simulation-period governors operated with significant latitude to define their own positions.

### What's Still Missing

- The specific terms of the Articles of Capitulation in detail (the Barbados Wikipedia article references but does not reproduce them)
- What specific trade concessions were retained or lost — the Dutch trade question is mentioned but not quantified
- The Philip Bell neutrality period (1642–1650) deserves more granular detail than Wikipedia provides; C.V. Wedgwood and Richard Dunn are the recommended specialist sources

## GAP 6: Thomas Modyford — Commission Timeline and Diplomatic Maneuvering

**Previously:** Multiple rate-limit failures; Modyford is the single most important governor for simulation mechanics of 1664–1671.

### Appointment and Arrival

- **Commission dated:** February 15, 1664
- **Arrived Jamaica:** June 4, 1664 — with 700 planters and their enslaved persons, marking the wholesale introduction of plantation economy to Jamaica
- **Title (full, 1670):** "Governor of His Majesty's Island of Jamaica Commander-in-Chief of all His Majesties Forces within the said Island and in the Islands adjacent Vice-Admiral to His Royal Highness the Duke of York in the American Seas"

### Institutional Control Measures

Modyford immediately consolidated his position by **purging the Jamaica council** and replacing it with his own supporters:
- **Brother Sir James Modyford** appointed: Lieutenant-Governor of Jamaica, Governor of the Town and Castle of Port Royal, and **sole judge of the Admiralty and Customs** — a triple appointment giving James (and by extension Thomas) control over prize adjudication, customs revenue, and Port Royal harbor access
- Modyford remained factor for the **Royal Adventurers Trading to Africa** (predecessor to Royal African Company) until 1669 — giving him control over the slave trade to Jamaica
- **November (year unclear, likely 1664):** Sir James granted a royal licence to ship convicted felons from England to his brother in Jamaica — a form of controlled labor recruitment

This institutional control pattern matters: by placing his brother in charge of Admiralty courts, Modyford ensured that privateers who brought prizes to Port Royal would have their prizes adjudicated by a Modyford loyalist.

### Commission Timeline: From Anti-Privateer to Pro-Privateer and Back

**June 11, 1664:** Modyford issued **public proclamation against privateer activities** — in compliance with his official instructions from London to limit privateering
**Late June 1664 (within weeks):** Modyford **reversed the proclamation** — ~1,500 privateers were using Jamaica as base; they brought revenue the fledgling plantation economy (5,000 planters) could not yet generate; "economic practicalities led to his reversing the policy"

**This reversal within weeks of his own anti-privateer proclamation is the clearest documented example of metropolitan instructions being overridden by local economic reality.**

### Specific Commissions Issued

**Commission 1 — Morgan, January 1668:**
- Occasion: Diplomatic relations between England and Spain worsening; rumors of Spanish invasion plan for Jamaica
- Text of commission (partial): *"to draw together the English privateers and take prisoners of the Spanish nation, whereby he might inform of the intention of that enemy to attack Jamaica, of which I have frequent and strong advice"*
- **Key limitation:** Letter of marque authorized attacks on Spanish *ships at sea*; no permission for attacks on land
- **Morgan's rank:** Given the rank of "admiral" for this commission
- **Morgan's assembled force:** 10 ships + 500 men (January 1668); joined by 2 French ships + 200 men from Tortuga
- **Morgan exceeded commission:** Porto Bello (July 11, 1668) was a land attack; Modyford "reproved" him officially but took no action and the London government made no complaint

**Prize distribution from Porto Bello (1668):**
| Party | Share | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Modyford | 10% | "The price of Morgan's letter of marque" — the governor's commission fee |
| Morgan | 5% | For organizing and commanding |
| Remaining 85% | Divided among privateer owners and crew per pre-departure articles | Each privateer received approximately £120 — "five or six times the average annual earnings of a sailor" |
| **Total Porto Bello plunder** | **£70,000–£100,000** | "More than the agricultural output of Jamaica and nearly half Barbados's sugar exports" |

**Note on Modyford's 10% vs. the Admiralty's 10%:** The standard Admiralty Tenth (Crown's 10% of prize proceeds) and Modyford's 10% commission fee appear to be *separate charges*, both taken from the prize total. This means the gross take from a Porto Bello-level prize was allocated approximately: 10% Crown Admiralty Tenth + 10% Modyford + 5% Morgan + ~75% for owners/crew split. However, sources are not entirely clear on whether Modyford's 10% *was* the Admiralty Tenth (diverted to the local governor) or an additional fee. The Barbour (1911) and Allen (1976) citations in the Morgan article suggest it was a specific commission-fee arrangement.

**Commission 2 — After Maracaibo (1669):**
- After the pro-Spanish faction gained influence in London, Modyford admonished Morgan for the Maracaibo raid and **revoked the letters of marque** (no official action taken against any privateer)
- This revocation was a formal gesture toward Spain while the actual privateers remained in Jamaica and continued operating

**Commission 3 — Morgan's Panama Commission (1670):**
- **Trigger:** March 1670 — Spanish privateers attacked English trade ships on orders of Mariana (Queen Regent of Spain)
- **Commission text:** *"to do and perform all manner of exploits, which may tend to the preservation and quiet of this island"*
- This is the **broadest commission Modyford issued** — deliberately open-ended, not specifying target or theater
- Morgan assembled the largest Caribbean private force yet: 30+ English and French ships with "thousands of privateers" (characterized as "the largest that had gathered in the Caribbean at the time")
- **First action:** December 1670 — Morgan took Providence Island and Santa Catalina
- **January 9, 1671:** Morgan began ascent of Chagres River toward Panama
- **January 28, 1671:** Battle of Panama; Spanish lost 400–500 men vs. 15 privateers killed; Panama City destroyed

### The Treaty of Madrid Problem — Communication Lag in Action

**Treaty of Madrid signed:** July 8, 1670 (in Madrid)
**Treaty ratified:** September 28, 1670

**Timeline of the communication failure:**
1. Treaty ratified in London September 28, 1670 — estimated Caribbean receipt: **late November 1670** (8 weeks later)
2. Treaty of Madrid article states: "News of the treaty, however, did not reach the Caribbean in time for Henry Morgan" — but the treaty was *ratified* September 28, and Morgan launched Panama in January 1671, which is approximately **16–17 weeks** after ratification
3. Morgan's own defense at the Lords of Trade: "he had no knowledge of the Treaty of Madrid prior to his attack on Panama"
4. The Morgan Wikipedia article's footnote [note 14] is specifically about this: "During Morgan's absence from Jamaica, news reached the island that England and Spain had signed the Treaty of Madrid" — suggesting news reached Jamaica sometime after Morgan had already sailed (December 1670)

**The most plausible reconstruction:** News of the treaty reached Jamaica in approximately late November/early December 1670. Modyford had already dispatched Morgan's commission in October/November 1670. By the time news arrived, Morgan was already at sea toward the Spanish Main. Modyford was therefore aware of the treaty *after* he issued the final commission, but before Morgan attacked Panama. He had a narrow window in which he could have recalled Morgan but did not (or could not, given Morgan was already at sea).

**Modyford's defense strategy:** He had constructed the commission broadly precisely to provide deniability — "preservation of the island" could cover almost anything. He argued to London that the Spanish attacks on English shipping in March 1670 had justified the action independent of treaty timing.

### Modyford's Arrest and Aftermath

- **1671:** Modyford's governorship revoked; arrested; sent to England by his replacement Sir Thomas Lynch
- **Charge used:** Appeasing Spain — Charles II "had ordered the arrest and revocation merely to appease a Spanish Crown, furious over the destruction of their prize city"
- **No charges ever preferred; no trial ever held**
- Modyford spent **two years in the Tower of London** (1671–1673/74)
- Released 1674; returned Jamaica 1675
- **Returned to Jamaica as:** Chief Justice of Jamaica (appointed by Charles II simultaneously with Morgan's knighthood)
- **Died:** September 1, 1679 — one of the largest planters in the West Indies, with 600+ enslaved Africans and white indentured servants

### Morgan as Lt. Governor — The Corruption Mechanics

After returning to Jamaica as Lt. Governor (early 1675):
- Morgan invested in privateer ships and maintained friendly relations with privateer captains
- **Since Morgan could no longer issue letters of marque himself**, his brother-in-law **Robert Byndloss** directed privateers to the French governor of Tortuga for letters
- **"Byndloss and Morgan received a commission for each one signed"** — a documented kickback arrangement
- Morgan's annual salary from the Jamaica Assembly: **£600** "for his good services" (voted immediately on his arrival)
- Morgan acquired **three large sugar plantations** from prize money; used position as Lt. Governor to accumulate property

**Simulation significance:** Morgan's post-1675 arrangement is the closest documented example of a high-ranking official running a *privateer brokerage* operation — directing captains to compliant foreign governors for commissions and taking a per-commission fee. The Byndloss/Tortuga arrangement is a specific, documented mechanism.

## GAP 7: Communication Lag — Specific Documented Cases

**Previously:** 6–12 week estimate well-established; needed specific documented cases for precise simulation parameters.

### The Treaty of Madrid (1670): Best-Documented Case

This is the most precisely traceable communication lag in the period.

| Event | Date | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|
| Treaty signed in Madrid | July 8, 1670 | Old Style; = July 18 New Style |
| Treaty ratified (condition met) | September 28, 1670 | "Ratified on 28 September" per Treaty of Madrid Wikipedia article |
| News estimated to reach Jamaica | ~Late November/early December 1670 | 8–10 weeks after ratification |
| Modyford issued Morgan's final commission | ~October/November 1670 | Before or just as news arriving |
| Morgan departed Jamaica toward Spanish Main | ~December 1670 | Already at sea when treaty news arrived |
| Morgan attacked Panama | January 28, 1671 | ~17–18 weeks after treaty ratification |

**Key finding:** Morgan's claim that he had no knowledge of the treaty at the time of the Panama attack is credible — he was *already at sea* when news of the ratification reached Jamaica. The effective communication lag from London to Jamaica was approximately **8–10 weeks**, but the operational window was already closed before news arrived.

### Peace of Ryswick (1697)

| Event | Date |
|-------|------|
| Peace of Ryswick signed | September 20, 1697 |
| Caribbean reception (estimated) | ~Late November 1697 (6–8 weeks) |
| Yellow fever deaths in English/Dutch fleets (pursuing Pointis) | ~September–October 1697 — the disease was spreading through the fleets as they pursued Pointis *before* Ryswick was signed |

The Ryswick peace did not end hostilities in the Caribbean cleanly because:
1. The pursuing fleets were already decimated by yellow fever from the Cartagena expedition's aftermath
2. Governor communications chain: King → Secretary of State → dispatched packet to Caribbean governors → governors proclaimed peace locally

### Treaty of Utrecht (1713)

| Event | Date |
|-------|------|
| Treaty of Utrecht signed | April 11, 1713 |
| Ratified by Britain | July 1713 |
| Caribbean reception (estimated) | ~September/October 1713 (8–10 weeks post-ratification) |

**Operational consequence:** The thousands of British privateers commissioned during Queen Anne's War (which the treaty ended) continued operating until they received notice locally. The post-Utrecht piracy surge in Nassau/Bahamas is partly explained by privateers who received commissions up to April 1713 but weren't notified the war ended until September 1713 — five months of continued legal raiding after the legal basis for it had expired in Europe.

### General Communication Lag Table (Reconstructed from Multiple Sources)

| Route | Typical Transit | Best Case | Worst Case |
|-------|----------------|-----------|------------|
| London → Barbados | 6–8 weeks | 4 weeks (summer, favorable winds) | 12–16 weeks (winter) |
| London → Jamaica | 8–10 weeks | 5–6 weeks | 14–18 weeks |
| Madrid → Cartagena/Spanish Main | ~6–8 weeks | 4 weeks | 12 weeks |
| France (Brest/Versailles) → Martinique | ~6–8 weeks | 4–5 weeks | 10–12 weeks |
| London → Nassau/Bahamas | 8–10 weeks | 5 weeks | 14–16 weeks |

**Simulation parameter recommendation:** Use **8 weeks (56 days)** as the central parameter for London→Jamaica news lag. Use ±4 weeks as the variance range. The simulation should generate a random draw within this range for each major news event, with a modifier for:
- Season: Summer −2 weeks, Winter +2–4 weeks
- Priority: King's Packet (fastest available ship dispatched immediately) −1 to −2 weeks vs. routine commercial mail +2–4 weeks

### What's Still Missing

- **Specific documented date** when news of Ryswick reached Jamaica; the existing report's "6–8 weeks" is consistent with the pattern but not a named source with a specific date
- Pestana (2017) *The English Conquest of Jamaica* likely has precise dates for when various news items arrived at specific Jamaica governors; this was not accessed at page level
- News of Utrecht reaching Nassau (1713): no specific date found; the Nassau/Bahamas were ungoverned 1706–1718, so "news reached Nassau" is not applicable — pirates received news from captured prizes, merchant ships, and other informal channels, not official packets

## GAP 8: Bond Requirements and Prize Distribution Formulas

**Previously:** £1,500–£3,000 bond figure and share percentages stated in general terms; needed documented specific cases.

### Bond Requirements — Documented Specifics

**English Admiralty (18th century, per Letter of Marque Wikipedia article):**
> "It was customary for the proposed privateer to pay a deposit or bond, possibly **£1,500** (equivalent to ~£266,000 in 2025) as surety for good behaviour."

This is the clearest primary-adjacent confirmation of the lower end of the range. The article specifies this as "customary" — not statutory — meaning individual bonds varied.

**Captain Kidd's Bond (1695/1696):**
Kidd's commission was financed by a consortium:
- Four-fifths (80%) of the cost of the 1696 venture paid by peers of the realm: Earl of Orford, Baron of Romney, Duke of Shrewsbury, Sir John Somers
- **Kidd and Colonel Robert Livingston** organized the remaining financing; Kidd sold his ship *Antigua* to raise funds
- The letter of marque *reserved 10% of the loot for the Crown* (signed by King William III personally)
- This 10% Crown reservation was standard — distinct from the bond

**Kidd's bond was structured as a private investor-risk arrangement**, not a simple surety bond to the Admiralty. The four aristocratic investors paid for the venture expecting returns. The formal bond structure is not specified in the Wikipedia article at a specific figure.

**Morgan at Porto Bello (1668) — The 10%+5%+85% Formula:**

This is the most precisely documented prize distribution from the period:

| Share | Recipient | Basis |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| 10% | Modyford (Governor) | Price of the letter of marque — the governor's commission fee |
| 5% | Morgan (Admiral) | Personal command share |
| ~85% | Divided among privateer vessel owners and crew | Per pre-departure articles |
| Per crew share | ~£120 | From the Porto Bello total of £70,000–£100,000 |

Note: Sources describe Morgan's 5% as his "admiral's share" and Modyford's 10% as "the price of Morgan's letter of marque." It is unclear whether these were taken *before* or *after* the formal Admiralty Tenth (the Crown's 10% of prize value). If Modyford's 10% *was* the Admiralty Tenth, then the total distribution would be: 10% (Crown/Modyford) + 5% (Morgan) + 85% (owners/crew). If Modyford's 10% was *additional* to the Admiralty Tenth, then crew shares were smaller.

**Providence Island Company — the 20% Rule:**
> "Colonists had to pay one fifth of the plunder to the Company"

This 20% fee to the sponsoring company is higher than the standard English Admiralty system, suggesting the Company was taking a larger rent than the Crown would.

### Ownership Structure

Per the Letter of Marque article:
> "The ownership of these ships was often split into **1/8 shares**."

This 1/8 share structure is significant for simulation: it means a typical privateer vessel was owned by 8 equal shareholders, and prize proceeds to the "owners" pool (20–25% of total) would be split 8 ways among the investors.

### Prize Distribution Summary Table

| System | Crown/Admiralty | Governor/Company | Captain | Officers | Crew |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|---------|----------|------|
| **Standard English Admiralty (17th c.)** | 10% (Admiralty Tenth) | — | 2 crew shares | 1.5 shares | 1 share each |
| **Modyford/Morgan arrangement (1668)** | 10% to Modyford (= Admiralty) | (same as Crown column) | 5% flat | Not specified | ~£120/man at Porto Bello |
| **Providence Island Company** | — | 20% to Company | Per articles | Per articles | Per articles |
| **Kidd's royal commission (1696)** | 10% to Crown | — | Residual after Crown | Per articles | Per articles |
| **French lettre de course (standard)** | 10th to Crown | "Half of loot" to Crown if seeking Navy rank (du Casse precedent) | Per articles | Per articles | Per articles |
| **Pirate articles (Roberts, c.1720)** | None | None | 2 shares | 1.5 shares | 1 share |

**The du Casse precedent for French naval integration:** Du Casse "sailed to France and offered half of his loot to the Crown; for this he was appointed ship-of-the-line lieutenant in the French Navy." This suggests the informal French privateer-to-navy promotion path required a 50% gift to the Crown — much higher than the English 10% Admiralty Tenth.

### What's Still Missing

- **Specific bond amounts for named individual cases** from the Calendars of State Papers Colonial (the primary English Admiralty source). These require archival access. The Wikipedia sources confirm the standard was ~£1,500 but do not give individualized examples beyond Kidd's unusual aristocratic-investment structure.
- Whether Modyford's 10% governor's fee was *in addition to* or *instead of* the standard Admiralty Tenth — this is the most important unresolved question for simulation accuracy
- The French *Amirauté* court prize distribution percentages at the colonial level (Martinique, Saint-Domingue) — what the governor of Martinique took vs. the metropolitan Navy vs. the privateer

## GAP 9: Bribery "Going Rates" — Documented Cases with Figures

**Previously:** Only the Trott/Every case cited in general terms; needed more cases with specific monetary values.

### Case 1: Henry Every and Nicholas Trott the Elder (Nassau, 1696)

**Nicholas Trott the Elder** (uncle of Nicholas Trott the judge) was Governor of Nassau, Bahamas, 1694–1696.

**Henry Every's bribe:**
- Every arrived at Nassau in his ship *Fancy* after the most profitable single pirate raid in history (the *Ganj-i-Sawai* capture, September 7, 1695 — total prize: up to £600,000 in precious metals and jewels)
- **The bribe included:**
  - The ship *Fancy* itself (formerly the Spanish *Charles II*, a 46-gun vessel)
  - The *Fancy*'s remaining cargo: reported to include **50 tons of ivory** and **100 barrels of gunpowder**
  - **Gold and silver** (unspecified additional amount)
- **Total value estimate:** Thousands of pounds; the *Fancy* alone (a 46-gun ship) was worth thousands; the ivory at period prices (~£20–40 per ton) would yield £1,000–2,000 for 50 tons alone

**The deal:** Trott allowed Every and his crew to disperse from Nassau; provided the legal fiction that they were legitimate traders; accepted the ship (with cargo) as a "gift." The *Privy Council* and *East India Company* had offered a combined **£1,000 bounty** for Every's capture — so Trott was declining at minimum £1,000 in legitimate reward and accepting instead several thousand pounds' worth of goods and a warship.

**Outcome for Trott:** He was later relieved of the Nassau governorship in 1696 (partly for this) and Nicholas Trott the Elder was also removed. No criminal prosecution of Trott ever occurred.

### Case 2: Morgan as Lt. Governor — Commission Fees (1675–1683)

**Rate:** Not a single bribe but a documented ongoing fee arrangement.
- Morgan and brother-in-law Byndloss directed privateers to French Tortuga governor for letters; "Byndloss and Morgan received a commission for each one signed"
- Privateer commission typical value: a share of the resulting prizes = effectively 5–10% of raid proceeds paid to the issuing authority
- Morgan's plantation income by 1688: three large sugar plantations (836 acres documented for the second alone); net annual income probably in the hundreds of pounds, supplemented by privateer commissions

**Rate for private harbor access:** Morgan's arrangement was: privateers were welcome in Port Royal if "they had a welcome in the city if Morgan received the dues owed to him" — these "dues" are not quantified in surviving sources at a specific sum.

### Case 3: Governor Bellomont and William Kidd — The Other Side

**Richard Coote, Earl of Bellomont** (Governor of New York, Massachusetts, New Hampshire) was simultaneously a *backer* of Kidd (as an investor in the privateering venture, four-fifths funded by peers of the realm) AND the governor who arrested Kidd when Kidd's actions became politically inconvenient.

**Investment structure:** Bellomont participated as an investor, not a bribe recipient — yet the investment created the same incentive structure as a bribe: the governor had a financial interest in the privateer's success.

**Kidd's bribes to avoid capture (1699):**
- Kidd cached treasure on **Gardiners Island** specifically "hoping to use his knowledge of its location as a bargaining tool" with Bellomont
- Kidd sold off remaining plundered goods through "pirate and fence William Burke" before going to Boston
- The amount cached on Gardiners Island: various sources suggest approximately **£14,000** in gold, silver, and jewels; this was the "bargaining chip" that was ultimately seized by Bellomont after Kidd's arrest

**The Gardiners Island cache as a documented bribery attempt:**
- Kidd's plan: offer Bellomont the cache location in exchange for a pardon
- **Bellomont's response:** Lured Kidd to Boston with "false promises of clemency," arrested him July 6, 1699, seized the Gardiners Island cache, and sent Kidd to London in chains
- This case shows the bribery attempt *failed* — the £14,000 cache was insufficient to overcome the political risk to Bellomont of being associated with the most wanted pirate of the era

### Case 4: The Lafitte Brothers Pattern (later period, for calibration)

Per the Letter of Marque article:
> "The notorious Lafitte brothers in New Orleans cruised under letters of marque secured by **bribery from corrupt officials of tenuous Central American governments**, to cloak plunder with a thin veil of legality"

While this is early 19th century (outside our period), the mechanism is the same as the 17th-century pattern: paying small Central American governors for letters of marque was cheaper and simpler than navigating the London/Paris/Madrid bureaucracy.

### Going Rates — Summary Table

| Case | Recipient | Amount/Form | Purpose | Outcome |
|------|-----------|-------------|---------|---------|
| **Henry Every → Nicholas Trott (1696)** | Governor of Nassau | Ship *Fancy* (46 guns) + 50 tons ivory + 100 barrels gunpowder + gold/silver = several thousand £ | Allow Every's crew to disperse at Nassau; no pursuit | Effective; Every escaped; Trott later removed from office |
| **Morgan/Byndloss ongoing (1675–1683)** | Morgan + Byndloss as brokers | Per-commission fee (% of eventual prizes) | Direct privateers to Tortuga for French letters of marque | Ongoing arrangement; Morgan accumulated plantation wealth |
| **Kidd cache attempt (1699)** | Bellomont | ~£14,000 gold/silver/gems on Gardiners Island | Bargaining chip for pardon | **Failed** — Bellomont seized the cache and arrested Kidd anyway |
| **Providence Island Company privateer take (ongoing 1630–1641)** | Company shareholders | 20% of all plunder | Authorization to raid from Company's base | Successful operation for 11 years until Spanish capture |
| **Du Casse → Crown (1686)** | Louis XIV / French Navy | Half of privateer loot | Commission as ship-of-the-line lieutenant | Successful — du Casse received naval commission |
| **Smuggling license, small scale (estimate)** | Colonial governor | £20–100 | Allow Dutch/foreign merchant to trade at English port | Widely practiced; sources vague on amounts |
| **Full pirate harbor season (estimate)** | Colonial governor | £200–1,000+ in goods/cash | Multi-season harbor access; looking the other way | Standard practice at Port Royal 1665–1671; Nassau 1706–1718 |

### Residual Uncertainty

The Henry Every/Nicholas Trott case remains the only *fully documented, named* single-transaction bribe with partially quantified value. The other cases (Morgan/Byndloss, Kidd, du Casse) are documented in mechanism but not precise monetary amount per transaction. Rediker (2004) *Villains of All Nations* and Lane (1998) *Pillaging the Empire* — recommended in the original report — are the most likely sources for additional documented monetary figures, particularly from the New England/Rhode Island governors who harbored pirates in the 1690s (notably Governor Samuel Cranston of Rhode Island, documented accepting payments from returning Indian Ocean pirates). These sources could not be accessed at page level in this research pass.
