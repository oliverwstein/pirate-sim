# PART 1 — SHIP ATTRITION: LIFESPAN AND LOSS RATES

## 1.1 Service Lifespan by Ship Class

### The Core Problem: Rot vs. Structural Failure vs. Economic Obsolescence

In this period, ships did not "retire" cleanly — they were condemned, broken up, sold down-market, or simply lost. The concept of a planned service life barely existed. What historians reconstruct is the **age distribution of active fleets** and **age at loss or sale**.

**Oak-framed hulls (English, Dutch, French, Spanish):**
- Green oak (freshly cut) could begin significant rot within 5–7 years in tropical service, faster if the ship sat in warm Caribbean waters without regular careening and graving.
- Seasoned oak (ideally dried 2–3 years before use) lasted considerably longer — 15–20+ years in Northern European service with regular maintenance.
- The critical variable is *where* the ship operated. Baltic/Northern European trade: hulls could last 20–30 years. Tropical Atlantic/Caribbean: typically 8–15 years before major structural problems became uneconomical to repair.

**Quantitative lifespan estimates by class:**

| Ship Class | Typical Service Life | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Sloop (20–80 tons) | **5–12 years** | Cheaply built, Caribbean/coasting use; high loss rate; rapid teredo damage |
| Brigantine/Brig (80–200 tons) | **8–15 years** | Atlantic colonial trade; replacement every decade common |
| Fluyt/Flûte (200–500 tons) | **10–20 years** | Dutch design, Dutch-built: more durable; English copies somewhat less so |
| Armed merchantman/frigate (200–500 tons) | **12–25 years** | Higher quality construction; more investment in maintenance |
| East Indiaman/large galleon (400–1,200 tons) | **15–30 years** | Best-built, best-maintained; VOC and EIC ships occasionally reached 30 years |

**Sources:** Davis (1962) ch. 2–3 gives English evidence; Bruijn, Gaastra & Schöffer, *Dutch-Asiatic Shipping* (1987) provide lifespan data for 4,789 VOC voyages; Pérez-Mallaína, *Spain's Men of the Sea* (1998) for Spanish galleons.

**Survival to various ages (rough estimates, all trades combined):**
- Reached 10 years: ~50–60% of ships launched
- Reached 20 years: ~20–30%
- Reached 30 years: ~5–10% (almost exclusively the largest, best-built ships)

These figures are dragged down heavily by early losses to storms and enemy action. Ships that *survived* their first 3–5 years had much better prospects — classic bathtub-curve hazard function.

### VOC-Specific Lifespan Data (Best-Documented Case)

The VOC fleet is the most statistically analyzable. From Bruijn et al. (1987):
- Between 1602 and 1795, the VOC operated ~1,700 distinct ships (including captured prizes and re-uses).
- Of ships lost outright on VOC service, the **median age at loss was approximately 8–12 years**.
- The VOC typically scheduled ships for a maximum of **4 "return voyages"** to Asia (each voyage taking ~18–24 months) before overhaul or retirement — implying a designed service life of roughly 8–12 active years.
- Ships that made it to Asia and back more than 6 times were exceptional; the *Eendracht* class was considered old at 15 years.
- **About 8% of ships dispatched by the VOC never returned** (lost outright); another substantial fraction required extensive repair.

### English Shipping (Davis)

Ralph Davis (*Rise of the English Shipping Industry*, 1962, p. 69–72, 376–379) calculated from Port Books and Lloyd's Register that:
- The median age of English merchant ships in active service c. 1686 was **approximately 10–12 years**.
- Ships in the sugar/tobacco trades wore out faster than Baltic traders; Davis estimates a "normal working life" of **10–16 years** for Atlantic traders, **15–20 years** for North Sea/Baltic.
- Davis found that of ships registered in 1676, roughly 40% had disappeared (lost or broken up) by 1686 — a **10-year survival rate of ~60%**, implying ~5% annual attrition from all causes.

## 1.2 Annual Loss Rates by Cause

### Aggregate All-Cause Loss Rates

The best-evidenced annual loss rates from existing studies:

**All merchant shipping (English, c. 1670–1720, Davis 1962 and Jarvis 1954):**
- In peacetime: approximately **1.5–2.5% per year** (all causes: weather, wreck, fire, foundering)
- In wartime (e.g., Nine Years' War 1688–97, War of Spanish Succession 1702–13): **3–7% per year** from enemy action alone; combined with weather losses, total annual loss could reach **8–12%** in the worst years.
- Davis (1962, pp. 316–319) found that in the peak war year of 1693 (the Smyrna convoy disaster, 100+ ships lost to French attack), English merchant shipping losses were extraordinary — but most years were far lower.

**VOC (Dutch East India Company) ships:**
- Of approximately 1,700 ships sent out 1602–1795 (Bruijn et al. 1987):
  - ~257 ships (roughly **8.3% of the total**) were lost before completing their voyage
  - But spread over 193 years, with many ships making multiple voyages, the **per-voyage loss rate was approximately 3–5%**
  - Per-year at sea (voyages lasted 12–18 months each way): roughly **2–4% annual loss rate**
- The **Cape of Good Hope and the East India run** was categorically more dangerous than the Atlantic: Dutch records show VOC losses were heavily concentrated at the Cape and in the Sunda Strait.

**Spanish Carrera de Indias (Chaunu; García-Baquero González):**
- Between 1580 and 1650, the Spanish treasure fleet system lost ships at a rate that Chaunu estimated at approximately **3–5% per voyage** to storm/wreck, with war losses additional.
- The most catastrophic single events: the 1622 hurricane (Tierra Firme fleet — *Nuestra Señora de Atocha* and others); the 1641 hurricane off Havana; the 1715 hurricane (killed the entire returning treasure fleet of 12 ships near Cape Canaveral, probably ~1,000 souls).
- **Notable: Spanish galleon losses to pirates and privateers were actually lower than often imagined** — the convoy system was effective. Only 4 complete treasure fleets were captured in the entire 1565–1790 period (Piet Heyn's 1628 capture of the Matanzas fleet being the most famous). The real attrition was storm losses.

**Cause-by-cause breakdown (English peacetime, estimated from Davis, Jarvis, and Fury/Fox studies):**

| Cause | % of Total Annual Losses | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Storm/hurricane/shipwreck | ~50–65% | Dominant cause; Caribbean hurricane season (Aug–Oct) particularly deadly |
| Foundering (gradual sinking, structural failure) | ~10–20% | Often teredo-related hull degradation |
| Fire aboard | ~5–10% | Major hazard with gunpowder, spirits cargo |
| Piracy/capture (peacetime) | ~5–10% | Elevated during Golden Age of Piracy (1650–1730) |
| Enemy privateer/naval capture (wartime) | **dominant in war years** | See below |
| Collision | ~1–3% | Less common |

**⚠️ Conflict/uncertainty flag:** The "piracy/capture" share is contested. Marcus Rediker (*Between the Devil and the Deep Blue Sea*, 1987) argues piracy was more disruptive to trade psychology than actual loss statistics show. Some historians estimate 400–500 pirate attacks on merchant ships 1716–1726 (the most intense piracy decade), against a background of ~15,000–20,000 active merchant voyages per year globally — giving a piracy probability per voyage of perhaps 2–5% in high-risk waters (Caribbean, Red Sea, West African coast), but much lower averaged across all trades.

### Caribbean vs. Transatlantic vs. East India Risk Comparison

| Route | Est. Annual Loss Rate (Peacetime) | Primary Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Caribbean coastal/inter-island | **3–6%/year** | Hurricanes, shoals, pirates; shallow, reef-strewn waters |
| Transatlantic (N. Atlantic, English/Dutch) | **1.5–3%/year** | Storms in Bay of Biscay and western approaches; winter crossings dangerous |
| Spanish Carrera (Seville-Havana-Seville) | **2–4%/voyage** (~1–2%/year at sea) | Hurricane risk concentrated; convoy system reduced piracy risk |
| VOC East India (Amsterdam-Batavia-Amsterdam) | **3–5%/voyage** (~2–3.5%/year at sea) | Cape storms, tropical storms, shoals in Sunda Strait |
| West Africa/Guinea (slave trade) | **3–7%/year** | Disease (crew), storms off Cape Verde, hostile coasts |

**Wartime multipliers (Anglo-French/Dutch wars, Spanish Succession):**
- Add **3–8 percentage points** to peacetime loss rates for Atlantic trades during war years. Some estimates for English merchant shipping during the Nine Years' War reach **10–15% annual total loss** in the worst years.
- The convoy system (used by English from 1690s under pressure, Spanish always, Dutch WIC for valuable cargoes) reduced wartime enemy-action losses dramatically — typically 60–75% reduction.

## 1.3 Hull Material, Construction, and Teredo/Fouling

### Why Tropical Waters Were Particularly Deadly to Hulls

**Teredo navalis (shipworm)** is a bivalve mollusk that bores into submerged wood, digesting cellulose. It thrives in:
- Warm, salty tropical waters (optimum: 20–30°C, salinity 25–35 ppt)
- The Caribbean, Gulf of Mexico, and West African coast were and remain among the world's most teredo-active environments.

**Rate of attack:** In warm tropical waters without protection, teredo could render a ship's hull structurally dangerous within **18–36 months**. Contemporary accounts from the 17th-century Caribbean describe ships' planking reduced to a honeycomb. The hull could still *appear* intact while being structurally useless. This was a leading cause of sudden foundering: a hull that had been skipping careening cycles would fail catastrophically in a storm.

**Pre-copper era countermeasures (all our period, copper not used until experimental 1761, widespread 1778–1785):**
1. **Sacrificial outer planking** ("doubling"): An expendable layer of thin planking nailed over the main hull. Teredo attacked the outer layer; the structural planks were somewhat protected. Typical lifespan before replacement: **12–18 months in tropical service**.
2. **Graving and paying**: The hull was careened, old fouling scraped off, and the bottom covered with:
   - *White stuff*: mixture of whale oil, rosin, and sulfur — somewhat anti-fouling
   - *Black stuff*: tar and pitch — anti-worm barrier
   - *Burning off* the fouling with torches (used by buccaneers who lacked shore facilities)
3. **Lead sheathing**: Used by some Spanish and Portuguese ships, and experimentally by the English. It protected against teredo but reacted galvanically with iron fastenings, ultimately weakening the hull fastenings. Not widely adopted.
4. **Choice of timber**: Caribbean-built ships sometimes used lignum vitae or tropical hardwoods that resisted teredo better. Spanish cedar and mahogany (used in colonial Cuban and New World shipyards) were significantly more teredo-resistant than European oak.

### The Careening Cycle

**Standard practice, 17th-century Caribbean and Atlantic:**

| Operating Environment | Recommended Careening Frequency | Reality |
|---|---|---|
| Tropical Caribbean waters | Every **3–6 months** | Often skipped 9–12+ months in active trading |
| Transatlantic | Every **6–12 months** | Careened on arrival/departure from Caribbean |
| Northern European/Baltic | Every **12–24 months** | Teredo less active in cold water |

**What careening involved:**
1. Offloading all cargo, stores, guns, ballast (2–7 days)
2. Beaching at high tide and heeling ship to expose bottom (tide-dependent)
3. Scraping all fouling (barnacles, weed, worm tubes) — back-breaking work, toxic environment
4. Inspecting planking for teredo damage; caulking seams
5. Applying graving compound (tar, pitch, tallow, sulfur) to the entire bottom
6. Refloating and repeating on the opposite side
- **Total time**: 1–3 weeks for a small sloop, 4–8 weeks for a larger vessel, assuming no major structural repairs needed.
- **Cost**: Roughly equivalent to 2–4 months of operating expenses (stores, labor, lost trading time)

**Operational impact of skipping careening:**
- A fouled hull could lose **30–50% of sailing speed** within 6 months in tropical waters. Barnacles and weed created massive hydrodynamic drag.
- Speed loss made ships more vulnerable to pirates (couldn't outrun a fast sloop), delayed voyages (increasing costs), and reduced cargo-carrying effectiveness.
- Structural risk increased with each delayed careening. Many 17th-century ship losses attributed to "storm" were partly teredo-weakened hulls that failed under stress.

**Quote from contemporary sources** (approximate — from Dampier's *New Voyage Round the World*, 1697, and Samuel Pepys's *Naval Minutes*): Caribbean buccaneers and privateers careened every 2–3 months of active operation precisely because their survival depended on speed. Pirates like Henry Morgan's fleet careened obsessively.

## 1.4 Ship Classes: Specific Notes

### Sloop (10–80 tons, 1–2 masts)
- Primary Caribbean coasting and inter-island trade vessel
- **Bermuda sloop**: shallow draft, fast, essential for shallow-water Caribbean navigation
- Cheaply built (£100–£500 sterling), 1–2 years to build in colonial yards
- Expected lifespan: **5–10 years** in Caribbean service; many lasted only 3–5 years before teredo and storm damage made them uneconomical
- Crew: 5–15 men; often owner-operated

### Brigantine/Brig (80–200 tons)
- Workhorse of the colonial Atlantic trade (tobacco, sugar, molasses, rum)
- Cost to build: £500–£2,000
- Expected lifespan: **8–15 years**
- Crew: 12–25 men

### Fluyt/Flûte (200–500 tons)
- Dutch invention (Hoorn, c. 1595); revolutionary cargo efficiency
- Could be handled by ~1/3 the crew of an equivalent-sized English or Spanish ship
- **Half the cost of rival ships** to build (Dutch estimate: 3,000–6,000 guilders for a 200-ton fluyt vs. 8,000–12,000 for an equivalent English vessel)
- Expected lifespan: **10–20 years** (Dutch-built, well-maintained); 8–15 years in intensive Caribbean/tropical use
- By 1670 the Dutch merchant marine totaled ~568,000 tons — roughly **half of all European merchant shipping** (de Vries & van der Woude, *The First Modern Economy*, 1997)

### East Indiaman (400–1,200 tons)
- Purpose-built for the VOC/EIC long-haul Asia trade
- Expensive: an 800-ton English East Indiaman cost £5,000–£12,000 to build (c. 1680–1700)
- Heavily armed (20–40 guns) for self-defense
- Expected lifespan: **15–25 years** with major refits every 5–8 years
- VOC practice: after ~4 voyages, ships were either extensively rebuilt or sold/condemned
- **VOC "retourschepen"**: The large Dutch East Indiamen averaged 7–10 years of service before loss or condemnation (from Bruijn et al. 1987)

### Spanish Galleon (300–1,200 tons)
- Built to a regulated design from the 1580s onwards
- Both cargo and military vessel; heavily armed convoy escorts (galeones) vs. lighter merchant ships (navíos)
- Built in Spain (Basque country: Bilbao, San Sebastián) and increasingly in New Spain (Havana became the major construction site from c. 1630s onwards)
- Havana-built ships used Cuban cedar and mahogany: notably more teredo-resistant, could achieve **15–25 years** in tropical service
- Spanish-built oak galleons in tropical service: **10–18 years** typical

# PART 2 — RETURNS AND WHAT HAPPENED TO THEM

## 2.1 Gross and Net Returns on Capital

### The Wide Variance Problem

There is no single "return on a merchant voyage" — variance was enormous. The distribution was heavily right-skewed: most voyages returned modest profits; a small fraction returned spectacular profits; some returned total losses.

**The fundamental economic structure:**
- Capital at risk: ship value + cargo value + provisioning/crew costs
- Revenue: sale of cargo at destination + freight charges (if carrying other merchants' goods) + return cargo
- Risk: complete loss of capital (ship + cargo) on total loss; partial loss common

### English Atlantic Trade Returns (Davis 1962)

Ralph Davis (*Rise of the English Shipping Industry*, 1962, ch. 8) is the canonical source here:

- **Net returns on a typical successful transatlantic voyage (sugar/tobacco trades)**: roughly **10–25% per voyage** on invested capital in peacetime.
- **Annualized returns**: Because Atlantic voyages took 3–6 months each way (with time in port loading/unloading), a ship could complete **1–2 voyages per year** in the Atlantic trades, yielding annualized returns of perhaps **10–30% per year** on capital — but only in successful years.
- Davis estimated that after accounting for all losses (ships lost, voyages that returned no profit, operating costs), the **average long-run return on capital in English merchant shipping** was perhaps **5–10% per annum** — roughly comparable to other investments of the era but with much higher variance.
- **The famous double-your-money claim (East India trade)**: Davis notes that successful early EIC voyages (1600–1620s) could return 100–200% on investment, but these were the *best* voyages. By 1660–1720, as competition increased and the trade matured, EIC returns were lower. The EIC paid dividends averaging **30–50% of share value per decade** across good and bad years — roughly 3–5% per year compounded.
- VOC paid dividends averaging **18% per year** across its profitable period (≈1630–1720), though this figure is disputed (some scholars argue the real figure accounting for capital reinvestment was lower).

### Spanish Carrera Returns

- Seville merchants operating in the Carrera de Indias could achieve returns of **40–100% per successful round trip** on luxury goods (textiles, books, oil, wine going out; silver, cochineal, tobacco, sugar coming back).
- **But**: the quinto real (royal fifth = 20% tax on precious metals) took a major slice; registered silver was additionally taxed at various rates reaching 7–10% through alcabala and other duties. Smuggling (*palo blanco*, unregistered silver) was endemic precisely because legitimate returns were dramatically reduced by taxation.
- García-Baquero González (*Cádiz y el Atlántico 1717–1778*, 1976) found that Seville/Cádiz merchants' effective return after taxes and losses averaged perhaps **15–30% per voyage** in good years.

### VOC Returns on Investment (Established Historical Consensus)

The VOC's financial history is the best-documented of any company of this era:

- **Dividend history**: VOC paid dividends averaging ~18% per year across its profitable era. But:
  - Best decade (1630–40): dividends reached 25–35% of par value annually
  - Worst periods (1680s–1700s, competition increasing): 8–12% annually
  - Final decades (1780s): company was insolvent
- **Return on individual voyages**: Glamann (*Dutch-Asiatic Trade 1620–1740*, 1958) found that successful pepper voyages in the 1620s–40s returned **300–400% on cost price** at the Amsterdam market — but this was the monopoly spice era. By 1680–1720, returns had fallen substantially as more supply entered the market.
- **Variance**: A critical number. Of VOC voyages in the 17th century:
  - ~8% were total losses (ship never returned)
  - ~15–20% returned with damaged cargoes or significant losses
  - ~60–70% returned "successfully" with some profit
  - ~10–15% of successful voyages returned exceptional profits

### EIC Returns (English East India)

- The EIC's voyage structure before 1657 was **separate stocks per voyage**: each voyage was independently financed and wound up. Returns ranged from total loss to 400%+ (Lancaster's first EIC voyage, 1600–1603, returned ~95% on investment, considered disappointing; some earlier English "interloper" voyages had returned more).
- After 1657 (permanent joint stock), EIC dividends ranged from 0% (bad years) to 75% of share value (good years).
- Chaudhuri (*Trading World of Asia and the East India Company*, 1978): EIC average annual return on capital 1657–1720 was approximately **9–15%**, but with periodic losses and suspensions of dividends.
- **Inland trade vs. voyage profit**: The EIC made money not just on specific cargoes but on controlling distribution networks. Scholars like Steensgaard (*The Asian Trade Revolution*, 1974) argued the caravan trade's replacement by the EIC created massive institutional rent.

### Voyage Return Distribution (for simulation calibration)

Based on the above, here are defensible distribution parameters for Atlantic merchant voyages (1660–1720):

| Voyage Type | Mean Return (% of invested capital) | Std Dev | Probability of Total Loss | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Caribbean coastal sloop | **+15%** per voyage | ~40% | ~4% per voyage | High-frequency, low-capital; piracy significant |
| Transatlantic sugar/tobacco (English, peacetime) | **+20%** per voyage | ~35% | ~2% per voyage | Davis's core finding |
| Transatlantic (wartime, Nine Years' War) | **+5%** per voyage | ~55% | ~8% per voyage | War premium captures profitability of successful voyages |

## 2.2 Ownership Structure

### English Shipping: The 64ths System

English shipping law and custom divided ship ownership into **64 equal shares**, each called a "64th." This fraction was *not* legally mandated in our period but was the dominant practice by 1650–1720 (Davis 1962, pp. 81–96). Why 64?
- 64 = 2^6, allowing easy halving/quartering of ownership
- A ship could be jointly owned by dozens of investors: a brewer owning 4/64ths, a merchant 8/64ths, the captain 8/64ths, the shipwright who built her 4/64ths, etc.
- Profits and losses were allocated proportionally
- **Captain's ownership**: Captains commonly owned 4–16/64ths of their ship (Davis), aligning their interests with owners

**Typical ownership consortium for an English Atlantic merchantman (c. 1680):**
- 2–4 "principal owners" (merchants, often also the cargo-consignors): 32–48/64ths
- Captain: 8–16/64ths
- Master carpenter or ship's husband (maintenance manager): 4–8/64ths
- Various small investors (other merchants, provisioners, relatives): remainder

### Dutch: Partenrederij (Share Partnerships)

The Dutch system was similar but more formalized as the **partenrederij** (share partnership). A *boekhouder* (managing owner/bookkeeper) handled day-to-day management and profit distribution. Dutch shares were often in sixteenths or thirty-seconds.

### Joint-Stock Companies (VOC, EIC, RAC)

- **VOC**: Organized as a joint-stock company from 1602. Initial capitalization: 6.4 million guilders. Shares were freely tradeable on the Amsterdam Bourse from 1602 onwards — the world's first freely traded corporate shares. Dividends paid in spices initially, later cash.
- **EIC**: Joint-stock from 1657 (permanent stock). Initial capital ~£370,000. Share prices fluctuated dramatically — from £100 par value to £500 at peak; to near-worthless in bad years.
- **RAC (Royal African Company)**: Royal charter 1672, monopoly on English slave trade. Capitalization ~£111,000. After monopoly ended 1698, effectively went bankrupt; dissolved 1752.

## 2.3 Where Did the Profits Go?

### Reinvestment in More Vessels

Davis (1962, ch. 9) and Wilson (*England's Apprenticeship*, 1965) both observe that the dominant use of profits by successful English merchants was **reinvestment in more shipping and cargo ventures**, not retirement from trade. The successful merchant expanded:
- Built or purchased additional ships
- Enlarged trading partnerships
- Extended credit to colonial planters and factors

**Reinvestment rate**: Davis estimates that perhaps **50–70% of shipping profits were reinvested** in the trade in the late 17th century, with the remainder going to household consumption, land purchase, and government bonds.

### Land Purchase and Social Mobility

The canonical finding (Wilson; Grassby, *The Business Community of Seventeenth-Century England*, 1995): Successful London and Bristol merchants typically:
1. Made fortunes in trade for 1–2 generations
2. Invested profits in **landed estates** (manors, country houses)
3. Sons entered gentry/aristocracy, left trade

This "gentry-ification" was so common it became a social criticism ("the merchant who buys himself out of trade"). Land yielded roughly 3–5% annual return, safer than trade but much lower than successful voyages.

### Capital Accumulation in Port Cities

The visible capital pools in English port cities (London, Bristol, Liverpool later) financed new construction directly:
- **Bristol sugar merchants** financed new ship construction directly from accumulated profits from Jamaica/Barbados trade
- **London merchant banks** (Barings, Hoares, etc. — many founded by trading families in this period) intermediated between shipping profits and new investment
- The **Amsterdam Bourse** was the most sophisticated: VOC and WIC shares, government bonds, and commodity futures created a genuine secondary market that allowed shipping profits to be recycled efficiently into new ventures.

### The Slave Trade Returns (RAC/Separate Traders)

After the RAC lost its monopoly in 1697–1698, the slave trade was opened to independent "separate traders." Profits were:
- **Gross**: Very high per successful voyage — a typical 1690s–1710s Bristol or London slaving voyage to Guinea and then Jamaica could gross £15,000–£25,000 on a capital outlay of £5,000–£8,000 (Lovejoy & Richardson, *"Letters of the Old Calabar Slave Trade,"* 2001; Richardson, *Bristol's Bristol*, various studies).
- **Net**: Highly uncertain due to slave mortality (8–20% Middle Passage mortality rate was typical, with outlier catastrophes above 40%), crew mortality (West Africa was lethal — perhaps 30–40% of crew died on some voyages from malaria and yellow fever), and market price volatility in the Caribbean.
- The **triangular trade** structure meant profits were reinvested in colonial goods (sugar, tobacco, rum) for the return leg: effectively 3 profit opportunities per voyage.
- Long-run RAC return on capital was actually **negative** (Pares, *Merchants and Planters*, 1960) — the company lost money on most slave voyages due to operating costs, mortality, and competition. The **separate traders** (post-1698) did better by being more nimble.

## 2.4 Crew and Captain Shares

### Wage Structure (not commission-based as such)

The dominant payment system for common sailors in this period was **wages**, not profit shares:
- **Common seaman** (ordinary/able): £15–£30 per year (English, c. 1680), with Caribbean and East India voyages paying slightly more as compensation for risk.
- **Mate (first officer)**: £30–£60/year
- **Captain**: £60–£200/year as wages, PLUS usually owned 8–16/64ths of the ship (giving equity upside)
- **Surgeon, carpenter, gunner** (specialists): £24–£50/year

**Crew wages as % of voyage revenue:**
- Davis (1962, pp. 131–140) calculates that for a typical Atlantic merchantman, **wages + victualling = 40–60% of operating costs**. Given operating costs typically consumed 60–75% of gross revenue, wages were roughly **25–40% of gross revenue**.

### Captain's Commission and Privilege

Beyond ownership shares and salary, captains had important informal income:
- **Privilege/venture cargo**: Captains were typically allowed to carry a certain tonnage (5–10% of ship's capacity) of their own goods freight-free. This was enormously valuable — a captain on an East India voyage could personally earn as much from his privilege cargo as from his annual salary.
- **Commission on sales**: Sometimes 1–3% of cargo sales at destination
- **Dead freight**: If the ship sailed under-laden, the captain often pocket "dead freight" charges
- The captain's effective annual income could be 3–5x his nominal salary (Davis 1962, pp. 130–131)

### Dutch (VOC/WIC) Wage Structure

VOC sailors were notoriously poorly paid in cash but received:
- Advance pay before departure (often already owed to creditors/boarding house keepers)
- Allocation to buy personal trade goods (sold at destination for sometimes substantial profits)
- Opportunity for bribes and side trades in the East (morally condemned but economically important)
- VOC common seamen earned ~10 guilders/month (c. 1680); captains ~60–120 guilders/month

## 2.5 Marine Insurance: Lloyd's and Earlier Markets

### Pre-Lloyd's Insurance (the Lombard Street / Royal Exchange System, 1575–1686)

Marine insurance in England was regulated by the Chamber of Assurance established 1601, based on earlier Lombard/Italian practice. By 1680, the principal insurance market operated at the Royal Exchange in London, where underwriters would subscribe portions of a risk.

**Pre-Lloyd's premium rates (English, c. 1660–1688, from Raynes, *History of British Insurance*, 1948; Supple, *Royal Exchange Assurance*, 1970):**

| Route | Peacetime Premium (% of insured value) | Wartime Premium |
|---|---|---|
| London–Mediterranean | 3–5% | 8–15% |
| London–North America / Caribbean | 4–8% | 10–20% |
| London–East India | 5–10% | 12–25% |
| London–Baltic | 1.5–3% | 6–12% |
| London–Lisbon/Spain | 2–4% | 6–15% |

**Note**: Premia covered hull AND cargo together in most early policies. Separate hull and cargo insurance was not yet standard.

### Lloyd's Coffee House (from 1686/1689)

Edward Lloyd opened his coffee house on Tower Street c. 1686 (some sources say 1687–1689). By 1691 it had moved to Lombard Street. Key functions:
- **Shipping intelligence**: Lloyd's List published shipping news from 1696 (initially *Lloyd's News*); its systematic reporting of losses was itself a major contribution to risk calibration.
- **Underwriting market**: Ship owners and merchants brought risks; individual underwriters ("names") subscribed portions of the risk, writing their names and amounts on a "slip" under the terms — hence "underwriter."
- By 1710, Lloyd's was the dominant English marine insurance market.

**Premium rates at Lloyd's (c. 1700–1720, from Clark, *Betting on Lives: The Culture of Life Insurance in England 1695–1775*, and secondary sources on early Lloyd's):**

| Route | Peacetime | War of Spanish Succession (1702–13) |
|---|---|---|
| London–Jamaica/Barbados | 3–6% | 10–20% |
| London–Chesapeake (tobacco) | 3–5% | 8–18% |
| London–East India | 6–12% | 15–30%+ |
| London–Iberia | 2–4% | 8–20% |
| London–Baltic | 1.5–3% | 5–10% |

**Fraction of ships/cargoes actually insured:** This is crucial for understanding the **risk-pooling reality**:
- Davis (1962, pp. 296–300) estimates that perhaps **50–70% of cargo value was insured** in the late 17th-century Atlantic trade — but insurance was far less common for hulls alone. Small ships and short coastal voyages were often uninsured.
- Underinsurance was common: merchants sometimes intentionally insured only 50–80% of cargo value to avoid the moral hazard problem of being perceived as over-insured.
- **The smoothing effect was real but partial**: The Lloyd's market in this period was still relatively thin (by 1700, probably a few dozen active underwriters for the English market). A single catastrophic loss could temporarily shake the market.

**Did insurance smooth loss-rate volatility for ship owners?**
- **Yes, significantly**: A merchant who consistently insured cargo at the going premium could convert catastrophic individual losses into a steady insurance cost. Davis found that London merchants insuring the Atlantic trades in peacetime could budget roughly **4–6% of cargo value** as an annual insurance cost and protect against total loss.
- **Limits**: (1) Wartime premia were so high they sometimes exceeded the expected value calculation (insurance became very expensive); (2) Claims were frequently disputed and slow to pay (many underwriters defaulted); (3) Hull insurance remained less well-developed; ship owners bore more of the hull risk personally.

### Dutch Marine Insurance

Amsterdam had the most developed insurance market in Europe before Lloyd's. The Amsterdam Chamber of Insurance regulated rates from 1598. Dutch peacetime premia for VOC cargoes were typically **5–8% for East India routes**, somewhat lower for Atlantic trades. The Dutch market was more formalized with published tariffs, while Lloyd's remained more negotiated/individual.

## 2.6 Government Subsidies and Company Monopolies

### The State as Silent Underwriter

The most important point for your simulation: **the line between private merchant risk and state risk was blurred in all the major trading systems.**

**English Navigation Acts (1651, 1660, 1663, 1673, 1696):**
- Required colonial goods to be carried in English ships, providing a guaranteed market for English shipowners
- Effectively **a subsidy to the English merchant marine** equivalent to the transportation rent that Dutch shippers might otherwise have captured
- Davis estimates that Navigation Act protection was worth roughly **15–25% above competitive freight rates** for English ships in colonial trades

**Spanish Carrera de Indias:**
- The convoy system (*armada de la carrera*) provided naval escort ships (paid by tax on cargo) reducing piracy risk
- The Casa de Contratación and its Consulado provided dispute resolution, credit, and insurance infrastructure
- The crown's **avería** tax (ad valorem tax on cargoes, ranging 2–8% of value in different periods) was essentially a compulsory insurance/convoy fee

**VOC/EIC Monopolies:**
- The monopoly granted to both companies was a massive state subsidy: it eliminated competition, allowing the companies to price above competitive levels
- Steensgaard (1974) argued this "institutional rent" was the primary source of the extraordinary early-period returns
- The Dutch States-General's support for the VOC included: diplomatic backing, occasional naval assistance, and the monopoly charter that allowed the company to exclude interlopers
- The **English Navigation Acts effectively excluded the much-cheaper Dutch ships** from English colonial trades — a subsidy estimated by contemporaries (Josiah Child) at perhaps 20% of shipping costs

**French Compagnie des Indes Occidentales (1664–1674) and successor companies:**
- Colbert's company received direct royal subsidies: 30 livres per ton of goods exported, 50 livres per ton imported (Villiers, *Le commerce colonial de la France à la fin de l'Ancien Régime*)
- Despite subsidies, the company failed commercially (liquidated 1674) and was replaced by private (but privileged) companies
- The French system was consistently less financially successful than the English or Dutch, partly because the French state substituted government direction for market mechanisms

**Military escorts and convoys: the key risk-shifting mechanism:**

The convoy system illustrates the point most clearly. When merchant ships sailed in convoy with naval escorts:
- Enemy capture risk dropped dramatically (perhaps 70–90% reduction in losses to privateers)
- The *cost* was borne partly by ad valorem taxes on cargo (Spanish avería), partly by direct naval spending (English system), partly by mandatory convoy regulations
- **This effectively transferred wartime marine risk from individual merchants to the state (taxpayers)**

The Spanish system was the most extreme: in the peak Carrera years, the crown spent enormous sums on maintaining the Armada de la Carrera, and the treasure fleet's security was a matter of national solvency. When the system worked (as it mostly did — only 4 complete fleets captured in 1565–1790), it created something close to a state-guaranteed minimum return for Carrera merchants.

**Hurricane seasonal multiplier (Caribbean, August–October, ~3 months):**
- Effective peak-season annual loss rate from storms alone ~8–12% if sailing through hurricane season
- Apply a monthly multiplier of ~3–4× during peak months

### "Old Age" Retirement Probability (Monthly)

This is effectively a **conditional survival function** — the probability of a ship of age *t* being scrapped/condemned given it has survived to age *t*.

Simple approximation (Weibull-style):
- Ages 0–3 years: **0.2%/month** background (defects, teredo not yet severe)
- Ages 4–8 years: **0.4%/month** (increasing maintenance costs, tropical teredo accumulation)
- Ages 9–15 years: **0.8%/month** (major refits needed; many ships condemned at this stage)
- Ages 16–25 years: **1.5%/month** (survivors only; increasingly expensive maintenance)
- Ages 25+ years: **3%+/month** (rare survivors; effectively certain condemnation within a few years)

**Careening multiplier**: A ship that has skipped 2+ careening cycles (>12 months without careening in tropical service) should have these rates multiplied by **1.5–3×** to capture teredo-accelerated hull degradation.

### Voyage Return Distribution

For a Monte Carlo simulation, a **log-normal or mixture distribution** fits the historical data best (reflecting: most voyages modestly profitable, some very profitable, small probability of total loss):

**Recommended parametrization (return on capital per voyage, excluding total-loss voyages):**

| Trade | Mean (μ) | Std Dev (σ) | Total Loss Prob |
|---|---|---|---|
| Caribbean sloop, coasting | +15% | 40% | 3–5%/voyage |
| Atlantic sugar/tobacco, English, peacetime | +22% | 35% | 2–3%/voyage |
| Spanish Carrera merchant | +28% | 45% | 2–4%/voyage |
| East India/VOC | +40% | 65% | 4–6%/voyage |
| Slave trade triangular | +25% | 70% | 3–6%/voyage |

These are **per-voyage** figures. Annualize by multiplying by voyages/year (typically 1–2 for Atlantic, 0.5–0.6 for East India round-trips).

### Reinvestment Rate (into Shipyard/New Ships)

Davis (1962) and Grassby (1995) suggest:
- Successful merchants reinvested **50–70% of profits** into the trade (new ships, cargoes, loans to factors)
- Conversion to land/bonds happened after accumulation of significant capital; roughly **20–30% of profits** went to land/gentry consumption
- **~10–20% was consumed** (household, social display)

For a simulation: a reinvestment-into-shipyard rate of **~50–60% of net voyage profit** seems defensible for a merchant actively growing their fleet; lower (~20–30%) for a "merchant-turning-gentry" who is extracting wealth from trade.

# Key Source Conflicts and Uncertainties

1. **VOC dividend rate (18% average)**: Some scholars (Gelderblom & Jonker, "Amsterdam as the Cradle of Modern Futures and Options Trading," 2005) argue that VOC accounting was opaque and the real return after reinvestment was **much lower** — perhaps 3–5% annually. The 18% figure is nominal dividend as % of original par value, which was set very low. Take it with skepticism; use as upper bound.

2. **Piracy loss rates**: Rediker (*Villains of All Nations*, 2004) and Johnson (*General History of the Pirates*, 1724) give different impressions. The quantitative data suggests piracy was economically **less catastrophic than often depicted** (maybe 2–5% of Caribbean voyages, not 20%). But *psychological* impact on insurance premia and routing decisions was significant.

3. **Spanish fleet losses**: Chaunu's data on the Carrera is massive but based on the *registered* system. Contraband trade (probably 30–50% of actual silver flows by c. 1700) operated outside the system and its loss rates are unknowable. Total actual loss rates were probably **lower** than registered fleet rates because contrabandists avoided convoy timing altogether.

4. **Careening frequency**: Contemporary accounts and logbooks suggest that in practice, Caribbean traders often went **12–18 months** without careening. Whether the 3–6 month "recommended" cycle was actually followed by small merchants is uncertain. Buccaneers careened obsessively; planters' sloops probably didn't.

5. **Davis vs. more recent scholarship on English shipping returns**: Davis (1962) is still the standard, but Zahedieh (*The Capital and the Colonies*, 2010) on London's role, and Nuala Zahedieh's quantitative work on Jamaican trade (1992 thesis), suggest Davis may have slightly underestimated operating costs and slightly overestimated net returns, particularly for smaller Caribbean traders.
