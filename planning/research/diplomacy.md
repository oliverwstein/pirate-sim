# Caribbean Diplomacy & Political Mechanics 1600–1720
## Research Report for Pirate Simulation

---

## SUMMARY

The Caribbean 1600–1720 was a theater of near-continuous low-level conflict operating on a separate legal logic from Europe. The "No Peace Beyond the Line" principle meant that European peace treaties were structurally ineffective in the Caribbean until ~1690, when colonial governors began enforcing them. Each major European war produced a Caribbean reflex — typically 6–12 weeks delayed by communication lag — resulting in waves of legitimized privateering that repeatedly transitioned into endemic piracy when wars ended. The period can be understood as three overlapping eras: Spanish monopoly breakdown (1600–1650), buccaneering heyday (1650–1680), and the Golden Age of Piracy (1680–1718/1730), each with distinct diplomatic and factional mechanics.

## PART I: WARS AND THEIR CARIBBEAN MANIFESTATIONS

### 1. Dutch Revolt / Eighty Years' War (c. 1568–1648)

**European dates:** c. 1568–1648  
**Caribbean relevance:** From ~1590s onward; intensified sharply after 1621 (end of Twelve Years' Truce)

**Belligerents:** Dutch Republic + England (1585–1604, 1625–1630) vs. Spanish Empire

**Caribbean theater:**
- The Dutch West India Company (WIC), chartered 1621, was explicitly a war corporation — its mandate included attacking Spanish possessions
- Dutch seized and held **Recife/Dutch Brazil** 1630–1654, providing a Western Atlantic base for Caribbean operations
- Dutch captured the **salt pans at Punta de Araya** (Venezuela) from 1599 — critical as salt was essential for the Dutch herring/fish trade
- Piet Heyn's **capture of the Spanish Silver Fleet at Matanzas Bay** (Cuba), 1628 — the only complete capture of the annual treasure fleet in history; yielded ~11.5 million guilders (~£1 million), funded the WIC's 1630 Brazil invasion
- Dutch occupied **St. Eustatius** (1636), **Curaçao** (1634), **Aruba**, **Bonaire** — Curaçao became the principal Dutch entrepôt
- Dutch occupied **St. Maarten** (shared with French from 1648)

**Trade effects:** Dutch disrupted Spanish silver pipeline; Spanish mercantilist system showed visible cracks; Dutch smuggling with Spanish colonial settlements increased as Spain could not police its own empire

**Territorial outcomes:** Peace of Münster (1648) — Spain recognized Dutch sovereignty; Dutch retained Caribbean island possessions

**Naval deployments:** WIC squadrons of 8–30 ships active in the Caribbean intermittently throughout the 1620s–1640s

### 2. Anglo-Spanish War (1625–1630)

**European dates:** 1625–1630  
**Caribbean dates:** Approximately coincident, with ~6–8 week lag

**Belligerents:** England (+ Dutch Republic) vs. Spain

**Caribbean theater:**
- English Expedition to Cádiz (1625) failed embarrassingly; limited Caribbean operations
- **Providence Island colony** (off Nicaragua coast) established 1630 partly as a strategic forward base — the Providence Island Company included leading Puritan nobles (Warwick, Saye, Brooke) who intended it as both a godly settlement and a base for plundering Spain
- English letters of marque issued against Spanish shipping; privateers out of the Caribbean targeted Spanish coastal towns
- The war was notable more for what it set in motion than for specific Caribbean engagements

**Trade effects:** Trade disruption minimal; England lacked sufficient Caribbean presence for major impact

**Peace:** Treaty of Madrid (1630) — restored status quo, but the Providence Island venture continued outside its terms

### 3. English Civil War Effects on Caribbean (1642–1651)

**European dates:** 1642–1651  
**Caribbean dates:** Concurrent with ~6–8 week news lag

**Belligerents:** Royalists vs. Parliament (within England; Caribbean colonies split)

**Caribbean theater:**
- **Barbados** declared neutrality under Governor Philip Bell, 1643 — refused to enforce either side's navigation restrictions; effectively an independent mercantile entity during the war
- **Virginia, Antigua, Bermuda** initially Royalist; **Providence Island** had already fallen to Spain (1641)
- Royalist privateers commissioned by Prince Rupert operated in the Caribbean 1650–1652
- Parliament's 1650 Navigation Act and follow-on 1651 Act aimed to cut Royalist colonies off from Dutch trade — this was the direct trigger for the First Anglo-Dutch War
- **Barbados submission** (1652): Parliament sent fleet under Admiral Ayscue; Barbados signed Articles of Capitulation, retaining significant local autonomy
- The Caribbean colonies' behavior during the Civil War established precedents for local autonomy vs. metropolitan control

**Trade effects:** Dutch trade with English Caribbean colonies flourished during the 1640s as Parliament's control was weak; the Navigation Acts were a response to this Dutch commercial penetration. Dutch merchants had effectively replaced English as primary intermediaries.

### 4. First Anglo-Dutch War (1652–1654)

**European dates:** 1652–1654  
**Caribbean dates:** War news reached Caribbean ~8–10 weeks after outbreak

**Belligerents:** Commonwealth of England vs. Dutch Republic

**Caribbean theater:**
- Primarily a North Sea/English Channel war; Caribbean operations secondary
- English privateers hit Dutch merchant shipping throughout Atlantic; ~1,700 Dutch merchant vessels captured over the war
- Dutch Brazil fell under increasing pressure (Portuguese rebellion + First Anglo-Dutch War distraction); Portuguese expelled Dutch from Recife, 1654 — permanent loss of Dutch Brazil
- Caribbean itself saw patrol actions but no major fleet engagements
- English squadron under Admiral Penn and General Venables was being assembled (1654) for the **Western Design** — a direct offshoot of the war's momentum

**Treaty:** Westminster (1654) — minor Dutch concessions; Dutch accepted Act of Seclusion (Orange exclusion from Stadtholder)

**Trade effects:** Dutch merchant losses severe; ~1,700 merchant ships captured. Dutch West India Company significantly weakened. English privateers profited enormously.

### 5. Western Design / Conquest of Jamaica (1655)

**Dates:** 1654–1655 (expedition) / 1655 onward (Jamaica possession)  
**Context:** Not a declared war but a planned aggression by Cromwell against the Spanish Empire

**Belligerents:** England vs. Spain (undeclared until Spanish retaliation formalized the Anglo-Spanish War 1654–1660)

**Caribbean theater:**
- Penn (fleet) and Venables (army) sailed with ~38 ships and 2,500 soldiers + Caribbean recruits (~7,000 men total assembled at Barbados)
- **Attack on Hispaniola** (Santo Domingo), April 1655 — **catastrophic failure**; English routed by Spanish and local fighters; disease decimated the army
- **Jamaica** taken by default, May 1655 — small Spanish garrison retreated; Spanish freed their enslaved Africans who became the **Maroons** (guerrilla fighters who resisted English control for decades)
- Jamaica remained a contested possession until **Treaty of Madrid (1670)** formalized English ownership
- English governors of Jamaica immediately began issuing letters of marque to buccaneers — Jamaica's economic survival depended on plunder trade through Port Royal

**Territorial outcomes:** Jamaica acquired by England — became most valuable English Caribbean colony. Spain lost formal but not practical control until 1670.

**Fleet deployments:** 38 ships under Penn (pre-Restoration Royal Navy); ~2,500 English soldiers + ~7,000 Caribbean recruits

**Trade effects:** Spanish Caribbean trade severely disrupted throughout 1655–1660 by English privateers based at Jamaica; Port Royal emerged as primary fencing market for plunder

### 6. Second Anglo-Dutch War (1665–1667)

**European dates:** March 4, 1665 – July 31, 1667  
**Caribbean dates:** War news arrived Caribbean ~8–10 weeks later (late April/May 1665)

**Belligerents:** England + Scotland vs. Dutch Republic + France (from January 1666) + Denmark-Norway

**Caribbean theater:**
- **Tobago** exchanged hands multiple times — Dutch held it, English attacked
- **St. Eustatius** briefly seized by English
- **Dutch Guiana/Suriname** — England ultimately ceded it at Breda in exchange for New York
- **De Ruyter's Atlantic cruise (1665):** Dutch Admiral Michiel de Ruyter swept the West African coast recovering slaving posts then moved to Barbados and Virginia waters — did not land at Barbados (English defenses adequate) but demonstrated Dutch reach
- **New Netherland (New Amsterdam)** seized by England in 1664 — precipitated the war
- Caribbean privateering intensified; Governor Modyford of Jamaica issued letters of marque to buccaneers as English proxy force

**Territorial outcomes:**  
- Treaty of Breda (1667): Dutch ceded New York; England ceded Suriname, Fort Amsterdam (Ghana), and the island of Run (Banda Islands)
- Net result: England gained New York, lost a colony that would become Dutch Guiana (Suriname)

**Trade effects:** Navigation Acts intensified; Dutch West India trade severely disrupted; English privateers under letters of marque very active

**Fleet deployments:** England 139 warships; Dutch 131 warships. No major fleet deployment to Caribbean itself — theater was secondary.

### 7. Third Anglo-Dutch War (1672–1674) / Part of Franco-Dutch War (1672–1678)

**Third Anglo-Dutch War European dates:** March 1672 – February 1674  
**Franco-Dutch War European dates:** April 1672 – September 1678

**Belligerents (Third Anglo-Dutch War):** England + France vs. Dutch Republic  
**Belligerents (Franco-Dutch War):** France vs. Dutch Republic + Holy Roman Empire + Habsburg Spain (from 1673)

**Caribbean theater:**
- England switched sides effectively — Parliament forced Charles II to make peace with the Dutch (Treaty of Westminster 1674) while France continued fighting
- **St. Eustatius and Tobago** again the focus of Caribbean fighting
- **Tobago**: Dutch vs. French; de Ruyter's fleet engaged French fleet off Martinique, April 1674 (Battle of Martinique); French Admiral d'Estrées eventually took Tobago
- **Martinique** French base used to mount operations against Dutch Curaçao
- Henry Morgan conducted major raids during this period under Modyford's commissions — Panama (1671) occurred during the aftermath of the Second Anglo-Dutch War

**Key dynamic:** England was ally of France from 1672–1674, which created complex situations in the Caribbean where Anglo-French cooperation against the Dutch coexisted with English buccaneer licenses that had been issued against the Spanish (nominally at peace with England since 1670 Treaty of Madrid). Morgan's Panama raid (1671) violated the 1670 treaty; Morgan was recalled to England, knighted (!), and sent back as Lt. Governor to suppress the buccaneers he'd led.

**Treaties:** Nijmegen (1678) — France gained Franche-Comté and Flemish towns; Dutch lost little territory but were commercially weakened relative to France; the Dutch abandoned the sugar regions of Tobago to France

### 8. Nine Years' War / King William's War (1688–1697)

**European dates:** 1688–1697  
**Caribbean dates:** ~6–10 weeks after European outbreak; formally established in Caribbean by early 1689

**Belligerents:** Grand Alliance (England + Dutch Republic + Holy Roman Empire + Spain + others) vs. France

**Caribbean theater (the most intense Caribbean war of the period):**
- France vs. England/Dutch/Spanish in the Caribbean simultaneously
- **Martinique** as French base; **Barbados, Jamaica** as English bases
- **French attacks on St. Kitts** (1689): French Governor Du Parquet's forces overran the English half of St. Kitts; English expelled, ~8,000 English colonists fled
- **St. Kitts** remained French until Peace of Ryswick (1697) — partially restored
- **Jamaica**: Governor Inchiquin, then Beeston, faced repeated French raids from Saint-Domingue; island constantly threatened
- **Raid on Jamaica** (1694): French and their local allies raided the eastern parishes of Jamaica extensively — burned plantations, seized ~1,300 enslaved people
- **English counter-raid**: Commodore Wilmot raided Martinique and Guadeloupe (1693)
- **War in Hispaniola**: French Saint-Domingue vs. Spanish Santo Domingo fighting; Jean-Baptiste du Casse (French Governor of Saint-Domingue) coordinated land attacks
- **Cartagena attack (1697)**: Du Casse and Baron de Pointis led a combined French naval/buccaneer force that **sacked Cartagena** (Colombia) — one of the wealthiest Spanish ports. Fleet of ~20 ships, 4,000+ men; took ~8–10 million livres in plunder. Notably, the buccaneers who participated were cheated of their share by de Pointis and had to independently re-sack the weakened city.
- **Communication lag**: The Peace of Ryswick signed September 20, 1697; Caribbean governors received news ~6–8 weeks later (November 1697); sporadic hostilities continued past formal peace

**Territorial outcomes:** Peace of Ryswick (1697) — largely *status quo ante bellum*: St. Kitts restored to joint Anglo-French status; France formally recognized William III as King of England; France recognized Spain's control of the eastern two-thirds of Hispaniola (while retaining Saint-Domingue in the west)

**Fleet deployments:** France sent ~20-ship squadrons to Caribbean theater at peak; England deployed Caribbean-stationed frigates + occasional squadron reinforcements. The Caribbean was a major theater, not secondary.

**Trade effects:** Caribbean trade severely disrupted 1689–1697; sugar prices volatile in London and Amsterdam; smuggling increased as legal routes closed; the war effectively ended the Port Royal era — Port Royal was destroyed by earthquake June 7, 1692

### 9. War of Spanish Succession / Queen Anne's War (1701–1713)

**European dates:** March 1701 – September 1714  
**Caribbean dates:** Caribbean informed ~late 1701/early 1702; formally at war by 1702

**Belligerents (Caribbean):**  
- Pro-Bourbon: France + Spain (Bourbon) = France and New Spain/New France  
- Grand Alliance: England/Great Britain + Dutch Republic

**Caribbean theater:**
- **Saint Kitts**: English seized the French half (1702), France never recovered it — confirmed at Utrecht
- **Nevis**: French and their allies raided Nevis (1706), carried off ~3,200 enslaved people
- **Montserrat**: French raided 1712
- **Raid on Nassau (Bahamas)**: Franco-Spanish fleet attacked Nassau 1703 and again 1706; town burned; English settlers evacuated; Nassau descended into pirate haven (vacuum of authority)
- **Cuba/Florida theater**: English from South Carolina raided Spanish Florida (Queen Anne's War North American theater); Spanish-French raided South Carolina in turn
- **Saint-Domingue vs. Jamaica**: continued raids and counter-raids; Governor Ducasse (French) and Governor Beeston/Handasyd (English) engaged in proxy warfare
- **Trade war**: English privateers devastating to French Caribbean commerce; French privateers from Martinique and Saint-Domingue reciprocated
- **Vigo Bay (1702)**: French/Spanish silver fleet destroyed by Anglo-Dutch fleet returning from the Americas — massive impact on Spanish treasury and Caribbean commerce

**Territorial outcomes — Treaty of Utrecht (1713):**
- **Britain gained**: Acadia (Nova Scotia), Newfoundland, Hudson Bay, **St. Kitts** (in full), Gibraltar, Menorca
- **Asiento de Negros** granted to Britain (South Sea Company) — exclusive right to supply enslaved people to Spanish America; 4,800 persons per year for 30 years + annual trading ship (*Navío de Permiso*)
- France ceded St. Kitts to Britain, retained other Caribbean islands
- France-Spain alliance formally broken by Philip V's renunciation of French succession

**Trade effects:** Utrecht created the Asiento as a diplomatic-commercial instrument of enormous Caribbean significance. British merchants legally entered Spanish Caribbean for the first time. The *Navío de Permiso* (annual trading ship) was used to leverage massive covert smuggling.

**Post-war effect on piracy:** The most critical consequence. ~thousands of British privateers suddenly unemployed (1713–1714) — many unable or unwilling to return to merchant sailor wages (1/4 the effective income). Nassau became the focal point. The Republic of Pirates (Flying Gang) 1713–1718 was a **direct product** of the War of the Spanish Succession ending.

## PART II: "NO PEACE BEYOND THE LINE"

### What Was the Line?

The "Line" (*les lignes*) referred to two geographic boundaries agreed informally between France and Spain at the **Peace of Cateau-Cambrésis (1559)** and elaborated in subsequent diplomacy:

1. **The meridian line** through the Canary Islands (approximately 20°W longitude)
2. **The Tropic of Cancer** (approximately 23.5°N latitude)

Together these lines enclosed the Caribbean and tropical Americas in a zone described variously as:
- *Beyond the Equinoctial* (equator)
- *Beyond the Line* 
- *Hors de ligne* (outside the line)

The concept held that **European treaties applied only within Europe proper** — west of the Canary Islands meridian and south of the Tropic of Cancer, each power could attack the others' ships and colonies without constituting an act of war requiring a diplomatic response in Europe. As the Wikipedia article on Caribbean piracy states: "south and west of these lines, respectively, no protection could be offered to non-Spanish ships, 'no peace beyond the line.'"

Spain never formally agreed to this principle — it was essentially a French and later English/Dutch rationalization for operations against Spanish colonial monopoly. Spain maintained it owned the entire Western Hemisphere by papal grant (Inter caetera, 1493) and Treaty of Tordesillas (1494).

### When Did the Principle Apply?

**Applied most strongly:** ~1559–1690. During this period:
- English and French privateers raided in the Caribbean even when nominally at peace with Spain in Europe
- Governors granted letters of marque that were explicitly "beyond the Line" commissions
- Spanish retaliation in the Caribbean did not require diplomatic apology in Europe
- The Dutch used the same logic after ~1600

**Began breaking down:** ~1660–1690:
- Treaty of Madrid (1670) — England and Spain agreed to recognize each other's Caribbean possessions; this was the first major treaty that explicitly governed Caribbean territories. Morgan's Panama raid (1671) violated it and created a diplomatic crisis.
- By 1690, as the Golden Age article notes: "Caribbean colonial governors began to discard the traditional policy of 'no peace beyond the Line,' under which it was understood that war would continue... in the Caribbean regardless of peace treaties signed in Europe; henceforth, commissions would be granted only in wartime, and their limitations would be strictly enforced."

**Fully abandoned:** Peace of Ryswick (1697) was enforced in the Caribbean. Utrecht (1713) terms were actively enforced in the Caribbean. By 1718, Woodes Rogers arrives at Nassau with explicit royal authority to end piracy — the era of ambiguity is over.

### Day-to-Day Effects

- A governor could issue a letter of marque against Spanish shipping even in peacetime (pre-1670) by invoking "Beyond the Line" status
- Spanish *guarda costas* could seize English, French, or Dutch ships in Caribbean waters with no diplomatic consequences in Europe
- Piracy was institutionally tolerated as proxy warfare — ships would operate without valid commissions knowing no European court would adjudicate Caribbean seizures
- The line created a **two-zone legal system**: Europe (treaties apply) vs. Caribbean (force decides)

### Communication Lag

**Typical transit time London/Seville to Caribbean:** 6–12 weeks depending on season and winds
- Fastest crossings (favorable winds, summer): ~4–6 weeks  
- Average: ~8 weeks
- Slowest (winter, contrary winds): 12–16 weeks

**Practical consequences:**
- A war declared in April in Europe might not be known in Jamaica until June/July
- A peace signed in September might not be known in Barbados until November/December
- During this lag, actions taken in good faith by either side created diplomatic incidents
- Governors who received war news first could attack before their enemies knew
- Governors receiving peace news *last* had already suffered or committed hostile acts
- Morgan's Panama attack (January 1671) — Treaty of Madrid signed July 1670 in Europe; news arrived Jamaica approximately October 1670 but Modyford dispatched Morgan before receiving it (or arguably before it arrived)

## PART III: LOCAL DIPLOMATIC MECHANICS

### Governor-to-Governor Negotiations

Colonial governors held significant **de facto** diplomatic authority in the Caribbean that their metropolitan principals formally denied but practically relied on:

**What governors could negotiate locally:**
- **Neutrality agreements** during European wars — especially common among weaker or equidistant colonies (e.g., Danish St. Thomas often declared neutrality)
- **Local truces** (*trêves locales*) — governors could agree to cease hostilities pending confirmation from Europe
- **Prisoner exchanges** — standard practice regardless of official war status
- **Mutual assistance against pirates and privateers** — governors of opposing European powers frequently cooperated against common pirate threats when it served their interests
- **Trade licenses** — informal agreements to allow limited legal trade bypassing metropolitan mercantilist restrictions
- **Evacuation agreements** — negotiated surrenders of under-defended outposts

**What governors could NOT do (formally):**
- Cede territory (required metropolitan authorization)
- Declare or end war (royal prerogative)
- Grant the Asiento or major trade concessions (imperial-level decisions)
- Authorize colonial subjects to make peace with a power at war with the Crown

**In practice:** The gap between formal authority and practical behavior was wide. Governors at remote posts operated for months without instructions. A governor who signed a local truce and then received instructions to attack faced an impossible situation.

### Local Truces Contradicting Metropolitan Policy

This was common and structural. Examples from the period:

- **Jamaica's governors** (especially Modyford 1664–1671) repeatedly issued letters of marque against the Spanish even during periods of nominal Anglo-Spanish peace, justifying this as defensive necessity against Spanish *guarda costas*
- **Barbados during the Civil War** (1642–1652): Governor Bell maintained neutrality against Parliamentary orders; traded with both Dutch and Royalist ships; Parliament eventually forced submission by blockade (1652)
- **Trinidad's Spanish governors** throughout this period accepted bribes from Dutch and English smugglers, effectively maintaining informal trade neutrality regardless of official policy
- **Saint-Domingue and Jamaica** negotiated local neutrality arrangements during parts of the Nine Years' War even as their metropoles were at war
- **Danish St. Thomas** (neutral throughout the period) became a key intermediary port where merchants of warring nations could trade in safety — Danish governors actively profited from this neutrality

### Bribery and Corruption

Corruption was **endemic and structural** — Caribbean governors were poorly paid relative to the opportunities available:

**Mechanisms:**
- **Cutting out of prizes**: Governor takes a percentage of prize goods as a "license fee" — formally the Admiralty's tenth belonged to the Crown, but governors took their cut from local admiralty courts
- **Smuggling licenses**: Governors issued informal permissions for foreign ships to trade at their ports; a Dutch merchant wanting to sell manufactures at an English port might pay the governor £50–200 for "looking the other way"
- **Pirate protection**: Henry Every's bribe to Nassau Governor Nicholas Trott (1696) included gold, silver, and the *Fancy* itself (ship loaded with 50 tons of ivory and 100 barrels of gunpowder) — a gift worth thousands of pounds
- **Slave trade kickbacks**: Governors took fees from slave traders, both legal and illegal
- **Plantation enrichment**: Henry Morgan himself used his position as Lt. Governor to acquire three large sugar plantations; governors used insider knowledge and authority to corner valuable land

**Going rates (approximate period estimates):**
- Small "look the other way" payment for a smuggling vessel: £20–100
- Protection for a larger operation (pirate harbor access for a season): £200–1,000+  
- Full pirate harbor arrangement like Trott/Every: thousands of pounds in goods + ship value
- Governor's salary: typically £200–600 per year (English colonies), making corruption a financial necessity for many appointees

### Marriage Alliances Between Colonial Families

The Caribbean colonial elite was small enough that inter-colonial and cross-national marriages were strategic:
- **English-Dutch intermarriage** in trading communities was common — Dutch merchants who settled in English colonies often married into established English planter families
- **Spanish creole families** in border zones (Cuba, Hispaniola) sometimes had multigenerational trade relationships with English Jamaica that included family connections
- **The Whetstone connection**: Woodes Rogers married Sarah Whetstone, daughter of Rear Admiral Sir William Whetstone — maritime family connections were the mechanism by which privateering ventures were financed and authorized
- Colonial councillors and assembly members were a tight kinship network; challenging a governor who had connected himself to that network was politically very difficult

### How Small/Weak Colonies Played Larger Powers

**Strategy of deliberate ambiguity:**
- **Danish St. Thomas** declared perpetual neutrality and levied customs on all comers regardless of flag — by the 1680s it was the entrepôt of choice for merchants of warring nations; the Danish governor prospered from neutrality
- **Curaçao (Dutch)** maintained trade with Spanish colonies throughout the Anglo-Dutch Wars by exploiting Spanish need for enslaved people — Spain needed the Asiento goods more than it needed to punish the Dutch
- **Small French Caribbean islands** during the Nine Years' War: when clearly unable to defend themselves, governors might negotiate local non-aggression agreements with English governors even while at war
- **The Providence Island Company** model: English Puritan lords used the ambiguity of "no peace beyond the Line" to establish a colony whose primary purpose was raiding Spanish shipping, while maintaining the fiction of a legitimate settlement

## PART IV: PRIVATEERING AND LETTERS OF MARQUE

### Legal Framework

A letter of marque was a **government license** authorizing a private vessel to attack and capture enemy shipping, with prizes adjudicated before an Admiralty court. The key legal elements:

**English system (most well-documented for this period):**
1. **Authorization**: Crown (via Secretary of State or Privy Council) or a Governor acting under delegated authority issued commissions
2. **Scope**: Commission specified the enemy (France, Spain, Dutch, etc.) and sometimes the geographic area
3. **Bond requirement**: Privateer captain posted a bond (in English system: typically **£1,500–3,000** depending on the size of the vessel) guaranteeing lawful behavior; bondsmen (usually merchant backers) would be liable if the privateer violated terms
4. **Term**: Usually for the duration of the war; commissions could specify shorter periods
5. **Prize court**: Captured vessels had to be brought before an Admiralty court for condemnation — the court determined legality of the capture

**Prize distribution (English system, 17th century):**
- **Admiralty Tenth (Lord High Admiral's share)**: 10% of total prize value went to the Crown/Admiralty
- **Owners of the privateer vessel**: ~20–25% of the remaining 90%
- **Captain**: ~2 full shares of crew division (so if crew received 1 share each, captain received 2)
- **Officers** (master, boatswain, gunner): 1.5 shares each approximately
- **Common crew**: 1 share each
- **Actual distribution varied** by the specific articles signed before departure — privateering articles, like pirate articles, specified shares

**French system (*lettre de course*):** Similar structure; commission from the King via Admiralty; prizes judged at *Amirauté* courts in colonial ports (Martinique, Saint-Domingue); the French system was notably more liberal in the Caribbean, with governors issuing commissions more freely

**Dutch system:** WIC monopoly held formal authorization for Caribbean privateering; private *kaperbrief* (letters of marque) issued by States-General or Prince of Orange

### Process for Obtaining a Commission

1. Captain/merchant consortium approached colonial governor (or directly the Admiralty)
2. Submitted petition stating nationality, vessel details, enemy to be targeted
3. Posted bond with surety (often leading merchants of the colony)
4. Received written commission specifying target enemy, theater of operations, and term
5. Signed/agreed to article of conduct governing prize-sharing and prohibited acts
6. Commission signed by governor; copy retained by admiralty court

**Who could get one:** Any subject of the issuing power who could provide a suitable vessel and post bond. In practice: established merchants, former naval officers, experienced mariners with merchant backing. The social range was wide — Morgan started as a relatively obscure soldier/sailor; Woodes Rogers was from a respectable Bristol merchant family.

### When Privateering Became Piracy

The legal line between privateer and pirate was crossed when:
1. **Commission expired** and captain continued operating — the most common mechanism; when wars ended, privateers continued
2. **Commission was specific to one enemy** but captain attacked others (e.g., attacking English ships while commissioned against Spanish)
3. **Commission was forged** — some operators purchased or fabricated commissions
4. **Commission was from a foreign power** one's home country didn't recognize — the issue with many buccaneer commissions (English captains sailing under French Tortuga commissions in peacetime)
5. **Captain exceeded commission terms** — attacked neutral ships, used excessive violence against prizes
6. **Home port refused to recognize commission** — if England was at peace with Spain and a captain brought in a Spanish prize, the admiralty court would refuse to condemn it as lawful prize, making the captain technically a pirate

**The ambiguity was structural:** "In practice, the differences between privateers and pirates were sometimes slight, even merely a matter of interpretation." (Letter of marque article) Many operators worked in a legal gray zone deliberately.

### How Many Privateers Were Active?

- **Nine Years' War (1689–1697)**: England licensed hundreds of privateers; Jamaica alone had ~10–20 active at any time; Barbados, Nevis, other islands added more; French from Martinique and Saint-Domingue similarly active
- **War of Spanish Succession (1701–1713)**: "More than 1,000 privateers already licensed" (Britannica, American Revolution comparison) — scale suggests hundreds active in the Caribbean at peak
- **1663 estimate** (buccaneer period): ~15 buccaneer ships with ~1,000 men "belonging to Jamaica and Tortuga" — this is specifically for buccaneers, not including regular privateers

## PART V: PIRATE FACTION DYNAMICS

### Pirate Governance — The Articles System

Pirates governed themselves through **Articles of Agreement** (also called the *Chasse-Partie*, *Charterparty*, or "Jamaica Discipline" during the buccaneer period). These were binding contracts signed by all crew before a voyage:

**Key elements of surviving articles (Bartholomew Roberts, c. 1720; John Phillips 1724; Edward Low/George Lowther, 1722):**

**Governance:**
- "Every man has a vote in affairs of moment" (Roberts Article I) — democratic decision-making on major questions
- Captain elected by majority vote; could be deposed by vote for cowardice or bad command
- Quartermaster elected separately — held the balance of power against the captain in non-combat matters; distributed shares, adjudicated disputes, led boarding parties

**Discipline:**
- No striking another man aboard — disputes settled ashore with pistols and cutlass (dueling under supervised conditions, Roberts Article VIII)
- No gaming for money  
- Lights out at 8pm below decks (Roberts Article IV)
- Keep arms clean and fit for service (Roberts Article V)

**Shares (Roberts):**
- Captain: 2 shares
- Master, Boatswain, Gunner: 1.5 shares
- Other officers: 1.25 shares
- Common crew: 1 share each
- Phillips: Captain 1 full and a half; Master, Carpenter, Boatswain, Gunner: 1 share and a quarter

**Compensation for injury (Phillips, Roberts):**
- Loss of a limb: 400–800 pieces of eight (Roberts: 800 for a limb; Phillips: same)
- Lesser injuries: proportional to severity
- This was essentially **disability insurance** — more advanced than most legitimate contracts of the era

**Prohibition on women:** Most articles explicitly banned women or boys from the ship; penalties for bringing a woman aboard ranged from marooning to death

**The oath:** Each man signed or made his mark, then swore on a Bible, an axe, or crossed pistols; this inducted him formally as a crew member with voting rights

### Pirate Bases and Their Evolution

**Tortuga (1630s–1670s):**
- Settled 1625 by French and English from St. Kitts
- Spanish repeatedly attempted to expel settlers (1629, 1635, 1638, 1654)
- French consolidated control by 1640; French West India Company purchased island 1664 for 15,000 livres
- Governor Bertrand d'Ogeron (1665–1675) was the key figure — issued commissions to both French and English buccaneers, organized Tortuga as a commercial enterprise
- Peak buccaneer population: hundreds of men; perhaps 15 ships at peak in 1663
- Decline: 1670s as Port Royal superseded it and French government tightened control

**Port Royal, Jamaica (1655–1692):**
- English seized Jamaica 1655; Port Royal grew into the Caribbean's largest and most prosperous city
- 5 forts defending the harbor by 1692; ~2,000 buildings; population of ~8,000
- Governors (especially Modyford 1664–1671) freely granted letters of marque
- The *entrepôt* for plunder: buccaneers sold prize goods here; merchants fenced them; it was cheaper and faster than sending prizes to England
- Henry Morgan's base of operations for all his major raids
- **Earthquake June 7, 1692**: Two-thirds of Port Royal sank into the sea in under 2 minutes; ~2,000 immediate deaths; fires and tsunami followed; ~5,000 total dead
- Port Royal effectively ended as a major pirate/privateer base; Kingston developed on the mainland

**Nassau/New Providence (1695–1718):**
- Charles Town founded 1670; burned by Spanish 1684; rebuilt as Nassau 1695 under Governor Nicholas Trott (who famously accepted Henry Every's bribe of ship + cargo)
- Franco-Spanish attacks burned Nassau again in 1703 and 1706; **no English government presence** 1706–1718
- From ~1706, Nassau was effectively ungoverned — pirates occupied it:
  - ~1,000+ pirates reportedly present at peak
  - The "Flying Gang" (Republic of Pirates) 1713–1718
  - Key figures: Benjamin Hornigold, Henry Jennings, Charles Vane, Samuel Bellamy, Edward Teach (Blackbeard), Calico Jack Rackham
  - Internal politics: Hornigold refused to attack English ships; this caused a split; Hornigold eventually took the King's Pardon
- **Woodes Rogers arrives July 26, 1718** with royal commission as governor, backed by a small naval squadron; offered King's Pardon; hanged 8 pirates who refused

### The Pirate Lifecycle

**Recruitment:**
- Most pirates were former naval or merchant sailors — experienced men who knew seamanship
- Common recruitment points: end of wars (unemployed privateers), merchant ships where crew conditions were brutal (floggings, starvation rations, press-ganged from other ships)
- Some recruited under duress from prize ships; "forced men" who signed articles under threat
- The Welsh captain's quote captures the recruitment pitch: *"In an honest Service, there is thin Commons, low Wages, and hard Labour; in this, Plenty and Satiety, Pleasure and Ease, Liberty and Power"*

**Active career:**
- Average pirate career: 2–3 years before death, capture, pardon, or retirement
- Geographic progression: Caribbean → Indian Ocean (Pirate Round, 1690s) → return to Caribbean/North American seaboard (post-1715)
- Typical voyage: 3–6 months; home base between voyages for selling prizes, resupplying

**Outcomes:**
- **Hanged**: Significant minority; piracy trials were public spectacles; bodies often gibbeted at harbor entrances as warnings
- **Pardoned**: Multiple general pardons issued — 1698, 1717, 1718 (King's Pardon / Act of Grace); many pirates took pardons, some repeatedly
- **Retired**: Those who accumulated enough wealth sometimes retired to colonial life — Morgan is the extreme example (knighted, became Lt. Governor)
- **Died at sea**: Combat, disease, or storms killed many before authorities could
- **Turned informant**: Some captured pirates gave evidence against others in exchange for clemency

### Pirate Economics — the "Sweet Trade"

**Merchant sailor wage (period estimate):** ~25–30 shillings per month for able seaman; perhaps £15–18 per year

**Pirate share from a successful prize voyage:** A good prize might yield £100–1,000+ per share; multiple prizes in a successful voyage could mean £200–2,000+ per crew member (as much as 10–100 years of sailor wages in a single voyage)

**But risk was severe:**
- Most voyages ended with modest or no prizes
- Roberts Article IX: "No man to talk of breaking up their way of living, till each had shared one thousand pounds"
- Most pirates never accumulated that much; those who did were rare

**The comparison:** A privateer's expected annual income during wartime was roughly 2–4× a merchant sailor's. A pirate's *expected value* (probability-weighted) was roughly similar to a privateer's, but with much higher variance — very high upside, death/hanging downside.

### Escalation Triggers: Trade Competition → Warfare

European wars typically escalated from commercial rivalry through these stages:

1. **Trade competition** — competing merchants drive down prices, each seeking exclusive contracts
2. **Regulatory friction** — Navigation Acts, mercantilist restrictions, and *guarda costa* seizures create incidents
3. **Prize seizures** — each side's armed merchants and *guarda costas* start seizing ships as "smugglers" or "illegal traders"
4. **Informal violence** — merchants arm their ships; coastal raids for supplies
5. **Letters of marque issued** — one or both sides formally authorize privateering; war not yet declared but fighting legitimized
6. **Formal declaration** — after sufficient casus belli accumulate in Europe (often one side's trade losses reach unacceptable level); Caribbean receives news with lag

**Key Caribbean-specific triggers:**
- **Seizure of a treasure fleet** (one-time; immediately causus belli)
- **Attack on a colonial settlement** (always provokes escalation)
- **Killing of a prominent colonist or official** (diplomatic incident)
- **Blocking of a major smuggling route** (economic provocation)
- **Governor's refusal to return seized prizes** (formal complaint → metropolitan pressure)

### Conditions for Peace

- **Battlefield exhaustion** in Europe — neither side can maintain army/navy indefinitely
- **Financial collapse** — Spanish treasury crises, English/Dutch credit exhaustion
- **Diplomatic settlement** of succession or territorial dispute in Europe
- **Common threat** (Louis XIV as threat drove Anglo-Dutch-Spanish alignment repeatedly)
- **Caribbean exhaustion** — repeated raiding depletes local Spanish settlements; Maracaibo alone sacked 3 times (1667, 1669, 1678); raiders themselves exhausted "the mine"
- **Metropolitan intervention** — London or Paris recalling governors, sending naval squadrons, threatening prosecution of pirates; this ultimately broke the buccaneer system post-1680

## PART VII: RELATIONSHIP MECHANICS

### What Improves Relations Between Factions

| Event/Action | Relationship Effect | Notes |
|--------------|---------------------|-------|
| **Profitable legal trade** | Positive | Both sides gain; creates political constituencies for peace |
| **Observing treaty terms** | Positive | Especially when tempting to violate |
| **Return of captured prizes** without formal demand | Strong positive | Rare; signals good faith |
| **Joint action against pirates** | Positive | Common interest overrides rivalry |
| **Bribe/gift to governor** | Positive (local) | Improves governor's personal disposition; may not affect formal relations |
| **Prisoner exchange on favorable terms** | Positive | Humanitarian gesture; builds personal relationships between governors |
| **Marriage alliance between colonial families** | Positive (local/long-term) | Binds economic interests across factional lines |
| **Timely notification of European peace treaty** | Positive | Demonstrates good faith; prevents accidental hostilities |
| **Local truce observed without violation** | Strong positive | Trust-building |
| **Warning of incoming hostile fleet** | Strong positive | Extraordinary gesture; unusual but documented |

### What Damages Relations Between Factions

| Event/Action | Relationship Effect | Notes |
|--------------|---------------------|-------|
| **War in Europe (received news)** | **Auto-declares war in Caribbean** after communication lag | Hard trigger; overrides all local arrangements |
| **Piracy/privateering against another faction's ships** | Strongly negative | Even if technically legal (letter of marque), the victim faction's governor protests |
| **Territorial raid / attack on colony** | Strongly negative; may require metropolitan resolution | Standard act of war; diplomatic crisis |
| **Seizure of neutral/non-combatant ships** | Negative | *Guarda costa* excess; major source of Anglo-Spanish friction 1680s–1730s |
| **Smuggling (if caught)** | Mildly negative with the catching party | Spanish *guarda costas* the primary enforcers; each seizure generates a protest |
| **Violation of local truce** | Strongly negative | Damages personal trust between governors; breaks the informal system |
| **Failure to control one's own pirates** | Negative over time | "Your governor is licensing pirates to attack our ships" — the Morgan/Modyford dynamic |
| **Trade embargo** | Moderately negative | Signals hostility; hurts both sides |
| **Competing territorial claim** | Ongoing tension | Major structural source of conflict throughout the period |
| **Communication lag causing accidental hostility** | Negative but potentially repairable | Both sides recognize the problem; compensation sometimes offered |

### The Specific Logic of Faction Relationships

**England vs. Spain:**
- Structural tension throughout: Spain's mercantilist exclusion vs. English desire for Caribbean trade
- Treaty of Madrid (1670) was a turning point — England recognized Spanish possession of most Caribbean colonies; Spain recognized Jamaica and other English possessions
- But *guarda costa* seizures of English "smugglers" continued throughout (leading eventually to War of Jenkins' Ear, 1739)
- Relationship modulated by: the Asiento (1713) created a commercial relationship; by 1713 England and Spain were actually co-belligerents (at Utrecht) against France

**England vs. France:**
- Intermittently allied, frequently at war
- The Glorious Revolution (1688) and William III's anti-French policy made England and France structurally opposed for most of the period
- Buccaneers in the 1660s–70s operated across national lines: English Port Royal and French Tortuga cooperated; this ended after the Glorious Revolution
- The earthquake at Port Royal (1692) + the Glorious Revolution combined to end the English-French buccaneer cooperation definitively

**England vs. Dutch Republic:**
- Wars 1652–54, 1665–67, 1672–74 driven by commercial rivalry
- After 1688, William III's dual role as Dutch Stadtholder and English King briefly unified them; they were the core of every Grand Alliance against France through this period
- Dutch commercial power declining through the period; by Utrecht (1713) Dutch had become a secondary rather than primary Caribbean power

**France vs. Spain:**
- Allied after 1700 (Bourbon dynasty on Spanish throne)
- Before 1700: frequent friction, but France concentrated on European expansion rather than direct Caribbean confrontation with Spain
- French buccaneers in the Caribbean had French government support implicitly even when France and Spain were at peace in Europe

**Pirate Factions vs. Colonial Powers:**
- Broadly: pirates sold prizes to any willing buyer; bought supplies from any governor willing to look away
- Nassau/Republic of Pirates (1713–1718) declared they would not molest English ships but would attack French and Spanish — an explicit political alignment
- Some pirates (e.g., Hornigold) refused to attack ships of their home nation; others (Roberts, Vane) attacked indiscriminately and were truly stateless
