## SECTION 2: DEMOGRAPHIC RATES

### 2A. Natural Vital Rates by Population Category

**Source: Dunn (1972); Higman (1984) Slave Populations of the British Caribbean; confirmed via Wikipedia Barbados/Jamaica articles and British-French Caribbean slavery article**

#### Enslaved Africans (sugar colonies)

The critical fact: enslaved populations in Caribbean sugar colonies had **negative natural growth** — they required constant importation just to maintain numbers.

| Category | Birth Rate (per 1,000/yr) | Death Rate (per 1,000/yr) | Net Rate | Notes |
|----------|--------------------------|---------------------------|----------|-------|
| Enslaved (Barbados) | ~25–30 | ~50–60 | **−25 to −30** | ~−2.5% per year |
| Enslaved (Jamaica) | ~22–28 | ~52–62 | **−30 to −34** | ~−3% per year |
| Enslaved (smaller islands) | ~20–25 | ~55–65 | **−35 to −40** | ~−4% per year |
| Enslaved (newly arrived, yr 1–2) | ~0–5 | ~200–300 | **−200 to −300** | "Seasoning" mortality |
| Free people of color | ~35–40 | ~30–40 | ~0 to +5 | Slightly better conditions |

*(Source: British/French Caribbean Slavery article confirms Jamaica ~−3%/yr, smaller islands ~−4%/yr; confirmed)*

**Causes of negative growth in enslaved populations:**
1. **Overwork/brutality**: Sugar cultivation and processing was extremely physically demanding (18-hour days during harvest/grinding season); cane cutters and mill workers sustained severe injuries
2. **Malnutrition**: Enslaved persons were chronically underfed — provisions allocated at minimum survival levels; vitamin deficiencies (scurvy, pellagra) widespread
3. **High infant mortality**: ~30–40% of enslaved infants died before age 1; enslaved women often forced to work through pregnancy; wet-nursing for planters' children diverted nutrition
4. **Gender imbalance**: African slave trade delivered 2:1 or 3:1 male-to-female ratios; reduced natural reproduction rates
5. **Tropical disease**: Dysentery, malaria, yaws, smallpox, yellow fever. Newly arrived Africans had no immunity to New World strains of diseases
6. **Deliberate policy of import vs. breed**: Before ~1807 (abolition of slave trade), it was cheaper to import than breed — so planters had little incentive to improve survival rates

### 3C. Middle Passage Mortality Rate — Detail
**Source: Middle Passage Wikipedia (confirmed); RAC Wikipedia (confirmed)**

- **Overall average**: ~14% died en route (range: 10–20% depending on period, route, and season)
- **Early period (1600–1650)**: Likely higher — ~15–20% (longer voyages, worse conditions)
- **Later period (1680–1720)**: ~10–14% as ships improved, routes optimized
- **RAC 1672–1731 verified rate**: 8.6% (186,748 total, 16,077 deaths)
- **1663–1666 verified rate**: ~24% (anomalously high — may reflect disease epidemic on ships)
- **Causes of death**: Dysentery (#1), scurvy (#2), smallpox, measles, syphilis. Disease spread rapidly in cramped holds.
- **Duration**: Typically 5–12 weeks (Atlantic crossing); faster routes from Angola than Senegambia
- **Captive-to-crew ratio**: ~350–450 enslaved per ship; ~30 crew members
- **Seasonal patterns**: Trade linked to African seasonal climate. Ships typically departed West Africa in dry season (December–April), reducing disease loads; Atlantic weather favored crossing November–May. Summer crossings from Caribbean back to Britain. The slave trade had a roughly annual rhythm.

| Annual maintenance per servant | ~£8–15 | Food, clothing, housing, minimal tools |
| Freedom dues at end of service | ~£10 in goods | Standard "freedom dues" — confirmed; also 5–10 acres pre-1635 |
| Total 5-year indenture cost to planter | ~£55–90 | vs. ~£15–22 for enslaved person (1640s) |
| Total 7-year indenture cost | ~£70–115 | For longer terms |

**Economic shift away from indenture, c. 1645–1660:**
After sugar revolution began, enslaved Africans became more cost-effective than indentured servants because:
1. Enslaved person was owned for life; servant only 4–5 years
2. An enslaved person's children were also enslaved (after 1660s legal consolidation)
3. Indenture costs were approaching the price of an enslaved person, especially as transport costs fell
4. Caribbean indenture became difficult to recruit for after word spread about tropical disease mortality

### 4C. Terms and Conditions
- **Standard English Caribbean indenture terms**: 4–5 years (pre-1635 could be 5–7 years in Barbados)
- **Age profile**: ~75% under age 25; many teenagers; ~75% male
- **Push factors**: English Civil War, Irish wars, Highland clearances, agricultural enclosure movement; ~30% were victims of fraud or outright kidnapping ("Barbadosing")
- **Pull factors**: Land grant (pre-1635 only); eventual freedom; adventure; debt escape
- **Post-indenture outcomes**: Before 1635, ~50% survived and received small land grants; after 1635, most received only £10 in goods, no land — leading to large landless white underclass in Caribbean which later emigrated

### 4D. Free Passage vs. Company-Sponsored
- **1627–1640**: Company-sponsored (Courten company, then Earl of Carlisle for Barbados)
- **1640–1660**: Individual planters buying indenture contracts from ship captains; captain was effectively a labor broker
- **After 1660**: Indenture to Caribbean largely replaced by free migration for skilled workers; some convict transportation continued
- **French engagés**: Sponsored by Compagnie des Îles de l'Amérique (1635–1649) then private planters

## SECTION 5: DISEASE MODEL

### 5A. Yellow Fever
**Source: Yellow Fever Wikipedia (extensively confirmed); Atlantic Slave Trade Wikipedia (confirmed); Piracy in the Caribbean Wikipedia (confirmed)**

**The pathogen:** Flavivirus, spread by *Aedes aegypti* mosquito. Arrived in Americas with the slave trade from West Africa, first major Caribbean outbreak ~1647 in Barbados.

| Parameter | Value | Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Incubation period | 3–6 days | Wikipedia: confirmed |
| % of infected who develop severe disease | ~15% | Wikipedia: confirmed |
| Case fatality rate (severe cases) | 20–50% | Wikipedia: confirmed |
| Overall case fatality rate | ~3–7.5% | Wikipedia: confirmed |
| Post-recovery immunity | Lifelong | Wikipedia: confirmed |
| Primary vector | *Aedes aegypti* | Wikipedia: confirmed |

**Differential vulnerability by origin:**

| Group | Vulnerability | Mechanism |
|-------|--------------|-----------|
| Newly arrived Europeans | **EXTREME** (nearly virgin population) | No prior exposure; 0% immune |
| Seasoned Europeans (3+ yrs in Caribbean) | Moderate | Those who survived prior exposure are immune; ongoing new arrivals die |
| Africans from West/Central Africa (endemic zone) | **LOW** | Childhood exposure in endemic areas; substantial population-level immunity (~40–60% of adults immune) |
| Creole-born Europeans | Moderate to low | Childhood exposure possible; immunity builds |
| Children of all groups (yr 1–5 in endemic area) | High | First exposure during childhood |

**McNeill's "Mosquito Empires" thesis** (McNeill 2010): European armies invading Caribbean islands suffered catastrophic losses to yellow fever and malaria — far exceeding combat deaths. Key examples:
- **Penn/Venables Expedition (1655, Jamaica)**: English invasion force of ~9,000 soldiers. ~2,000–3,000 died of disease within first weeks; over 5,000 total disease deaths before stabilization. [INFERRED from literature; not directly confirmed in this session]
- **Structural effect**: Yellow fever epidemics disproportionately killed European soldiers who had no immunity while protecting Afro-Caribbean populations who had partial immunity — this gave a systematic defensive advantage to islands with large enslaved populations when resisting European attack.
- **Seasonal peak**: Mosquito season peaks June–October (Caribbean wet season). Military campaigns in dry season (January–April) had lower disease losses; summer campaigns were disasters.

**Expected monthly disease mortality for European soldiers (tropical campaign):**
| Season | Monthly mortality rate (disease only) | Notes |
|--------|--------------------------------------|-------|
| First month, wet season | ~10–20% of unit strength | Worst period: full exposure, no immunity |
| Second month, wet season | ~5–10% | Survivors building immunity; fresh arrivals continuing to die |
| Dry season campaign | ~2–5% per month | Reduced mosquito activity; dysentery/flux still high |
| "Seasoned" garrison, any season | ~0.5–2% per month | Mostly immune or resistant |

*(Note: A 1,000-man European regiment deployed in Jamaica/Barbados in August could reasonably lose 100–200 men to disease in the first month, and another 50–100 in the second month before stabilizing. Only seasoned garrisons had survivable rates.)*

**Major Caribbean yellow fever outbreaks (verified or well-attested):**
- **1647**: First major documented outbreak, Barbados (reportedly killed ~6,000 settlers)
- **1655**: Jamaica, following English conquest
- **1691**: Barbados epidemic
- **1692**: Jamaica — combined with earthquake disaster, severely damaged Port Royal
- **1694, 1703, 1715**: Recurring Jamaica epidemics

### 5B. Malaria
**Source: Atlantic Slave Trade Wikipedia (confirmed for differential immunity); [INFERRED for rates]**

- **Dominant strain in Caribbean**: *Plasmodium falciparum* (most deadly)
- **Europeans**: Highly vulnerable, especially *P. falciparum* causing cerebral malaria; CFR untreated ~10–25%
- **West Africans**: Significant protection through:
  - Sickle cell trait (HbS) — carrier frequency ~20–30% in West African populations — reduces *P. falciparum* severity
  - G6PD deficiency — similar protective effect
  - Historical exposure (partial immunity in adults)
- **Seasonal pattern**: Peaks with rainy season (June–October); swamp/lowland areas most dangerous
- **Effect on settlement**: Fort Royal at Martinique deliberately built in "malarial swamp" (1674 — confirmed from Martinique article) — severe ongoing mortality for French garrison

### 5C. Seasoning Mortality
**Source: Indentured Servitude Wikipedia (high death rates confirmed); Barbados Wikipedia (4x deaths to marriages confirmed); [INFERRED for specific percentages]**

The **"seasoning"** period referred to the first 1–2 years in a new tropical environment when new arrivals faced peak mortality from yellow fever, malaria, dysentery, and other tropical diseases.

**European settlers/servants — seasoning mortality:**
| Year of Residence | Annual Mortality Rate (per 1,000) | Survival Rate |
|------------------|----------------------------------|---------------|
| Year 1 | ~250–400 | 60–75% survive year 1 |
| Year 2 | ~80–150 | Further 8–15% die |
| Year 3+ (if survived) | ~25–50 (normal "seasoned" rate) | Dramatically improved |

- Approximately **25–40% of newly arrived Europeans died in their first year** in the Caribbean
- The survival of "seasoning" conferred significant immunity to yellow fever (lifelong) and some malaria resistance
- **Implication for indenture**: Of 5,000 servants arriving in Barbados in a year, approximately 1,250–2,000 would die in year 1; by year 5 end, perhaps only 2,000–2,500 would survive to earn freedom dues

**Enslaved Africans — seasoning mortality:**
- Also experienced high first-year mortality due to:
  - Trauma and shock of capture/Middle Passage
  - Exposure to New World diseases (different *P. falciparum* strains, different gut bacteria)
  - Overwork and malnutrition immediately upon arrival
- Estimated first-year mortality for newly arrived Africans: **~20–33%** [INFERRED from Dunn; not directly confirmed in this session]
- Planters were aware of this; some gave newly arrived enslaved persons a few weeks of lighter work before full sugar labor

### 5D. Dysentery, Typhus, and Endemic Disease
- **Dysentery** ("bloody flux"): Affected everyone regardless of origin; endemic in all Caribbean colonies; caused by contaminated water/food. Annual mortality contribution: ~5–15/1,000 in endemic years; can spike to ~30–50/1,000 in epidemic years. The #1 killer on Middle Passage ships.
- **Typhus**: Less common in Caribbean, more of a siege/winter disease in Europe; some presence in crowded ship and plantation conditions.
- **Smallpox**: Episodic epidemic disease hitting all groups; particularly devastating for indigenous populations (though they were nearly gone by 1600). Periodically swept through Caribbean colonies.
- **Yaws**: Treponemal disease endemic in West Africa; transmitted in Caribbean among enslaved populations; debilitating but rarely fatal directly.

### 6C. Constraints on Expansion

| Constraint | Before 1640 | 1640–1670 | 1670–1720 |
|-----------|------------|----------|----------|
| **Labor supply** | Land available; labor scarce (indenture) | Critical: not enough enslaved; RAC pipeline insufficient | Improving but still bottleneck in new colonies |
| **Land** | Abundant in 1627 Barbados; filled by 1640 | Land fully occupied in Barbados by 1650; Jamaica opens 1655 | Land abundant in Jamaica, Saint-Domingue; access issue |
| **Capital** | Scarce; tobacco boom then bust | Improving; Dutch credit critical for early sugar | Credit from London/Amsterdam merchants available |
| **Skilled labor** | Severe: no sugar masters, coopers, millwrights | Still scarce; Dutch-Brazilian refugees critical 1654+ | Gradually developed through apprenticeship |

**Primary bottleneck 1640–1720**: **Labor supply** (enslaved Africans). The entire system was slave-supply constrained. Planters consistently demanded more enslaved persons than could be delivered. The RAC's supply of ~5,000/year to all English colonies was grossly insufficient (Barbados alone needing ~3,000–4,000/year to replace natural decrease plus expansion).

## SECTION 7: SUPPLEMENT — KEY QUALITATIVE MECHANISMS

### 7A. The Sugar Revolution (c. 1640–1660)
The transition from tobacco (labor-light, small farm) to sugar (capital-intensive, large plantation, massive labor needs) fundamentally transformed every English and French island:
1. **Drax's 1640 visit to Dutch Brazil** introduced triple-roller mill and copper cauldrons to Barbados — confirmed Sugar Plantations Wikipedia
2. **Dutch Jews from Recife (1654)** brought expertise to Martinique and Barbados — confirmed both articles
3. Within 20 years (1640–1660), Barbados went from 3/4 European to majority enslaved
4. Land concentration: Small tobacco farms (~10 acres) absorbed into large sugar estates (50–500 acres)
5. Indentured servants replaced by enslaved Africans; dispossessed small whites emigrated

### 7B. Dutch Role as Trade Enablers (1630–1660)
- Dutch fluyts were the most efficient cargo ships; Dutch merchants extended credit to Barbados planters to buy sugar mills
- Dutch WIC supplied enslaved Africans to English/French islands before RAC monopoly
- Dutch Brazil refugees (1654) transferred sugar expertise globally
- After English Navigation Acts (1651, 1660), Dutch direct role declined but credit relationships persisted

### 7C. The "Ten Percent Act" Turning Point (1697)
- Parliament forced RAC to open English Africa trade to all merchants who paid 10% levy to RAC
- RAC insolvent by 1708; private traders took over ~75% of English slave trade immediately
- Result: Volume of enslaved persons to British Caribbean roughly doubled in 1698–1720 vs. RAC period
- Jamaica and Saint-Domingue were the major growth recipients
