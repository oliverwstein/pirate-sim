# Caribbean Trade Restriction Regimes, 1600–1720: Research Findings for Simulation Rules Engine

## Summary

This report synthesizes findings from Wikipedia, primary legislative texts (via British History Online), and scholarship consistent with Harper (1939), Klooster (2003), Pares (1936), Stein & Stein (2000), and Fisher (1985). ## I. SPAIN — The *Sistema de Flotas*, Casa de Contratación, and Tariff Regime

### 1.1 The Casa de Contratación Monopoly Port System

**Founding and Mandate (1503–1717):** The *Casa de Contratación* was established in Seville in 1503 by Queen Isabella I. It functioned as the Crown's sole licensing, taxing, and regulatory authority over all transatlantic commerce. By law, **no ship could sail to or from the Americas without Casa approval**. It registered voyages, licensed pilots, collected duties, probated the estates of colonists dying overseas, maintained secret navigation charts (*Padrón Real*), and ran a navigation school.

**Single-port monopoly:** All legal trade with the colonies was funneled through **Seville** as the mandatory metropolitan port from 1503 until **1717**, when the Bourbon reforms transferred the Casa (and the monopoly) to **Cádiz**. The Guadalquivir river approach to Seville was difficult for large ships; this increasingly made Seville impractical for late-17th-century vessels and contributed to the Cádiz transfer. The Cádiz shift was foreshadowed in practice decades earlier, as larger vessels increasingly staged from Cádiz bay while paperwork was filed in Seville.

**The Consulado de Mercaderes (founded 1543):** The Casa worked in concert with the Seville merchant guild (*Consulado*), which held virtual monopoly rights over goods shipped to America. The *Consulado* controlled what went on the fleets, kept prices artificially high in the colonies, and wielded enormous political influence. A sister *consulado* was established in Mexico City in 1594 (requiring minimum assets of 28,000 pesos for membership); Lima had one too. These American consulados were the other end of the monopoly pipe.

---

### 1.2 Convoy System: Flotas and Galeones

**Formal system established:** c. 1564–1566, institutionalized under Pedro Menéndez de Avilés on the recommendation of Philip II.

**Structure — Two Annual Convoys:**

| Fleet Name | Spanish Name | Destination Port(s) | Primary Cargo OUT | Primary Cargo IN |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| New Spain Fleet | *Flota de Nueva España* | **Veracruz** (Mexico) | European manufactures, wine, oil, textiles | Silver, cochineal, silk/porcelain (transhipped from Manila galleons via Acapulco–Veracruz mule train) |
| Tierra Firme Fleet | *Galeones* (or *Armada de Tierra Firme*) | **Cartagena de Indias**, **Portobelo** (Panama) | European goods | Peruvian/Colombian silver (transhipped overland across the Panama Isthmus from Callao via mule), gold, emeralds, pearls |

**Rendezvous:** Both fleets assembled at **Havana** for the return voyage to Spain in convoy.

**Route:** Seville/Cádiz → Canary Islands (provisioning stop) → Cross Atlantic on the trade winds → split at Caribbean → respective ports → rejoin Havana → Bermuda Current north → Azores → Spain.

**Composition and Scale:**
- 1550: ~17 ships
- Late 16th century peak: 50+ ships per convoy
- Late 17th century (decay period): ~20–25 ships
- Ships were galleons of 200–1,000 toneladas, plus smaller *pataches* (dispatch/scout vessels) and *hulks* (*urcas*) for bulk cargo
- Warships (*galeones de guerra*) provided armed escort — typically 8–14 warships per combined fleet

**Frequency:** Theoretically **annual**, in practice highly irregular. Disruptions caused by: war, hurricane season, silver production cycles, bureaucratic gridlock, and epidemic. In the late 17th century (1650–1700), some years saw no fleet at all; others had multi-year gaps. The 1640s–1680s saw extreme irregularity.

**Portobelo Fairs:** When the *galeones* arrived at Portobelo, a great trade fair (*feria*) was held, lasting 2–6 weeks. Silver from Peru arrived via the Pacific and overland mule train; European goods were exchanged. Portobelo replaced Nombre de Dios as the Caribbean terminal in 1597.

**Side notes on inter-colonial redistribution:** A third, smaller convoy route existed from Portobelo/Cartagena to Caribbean subsidiary ports — the *Armada de Barlovento* (Windward Fleet), created 1635, was meant to patrol the Caribbean and suppress contraband, though often poorly funded. Havana received some secondary distribution. The island of Margarita (Venezuela) was a licensed pearling station with its own small fleet connections.

---

### 1.3 Licensed Colonial Ports

Legal entry ports for the *flotas* and *galeones* — the only places that could lawfully receive registered cargoes:

**Primary terminals (direct fleet calls):**
- **Veracruz** (New Spain/Mexico)
- **Portobelo** (Panama — Tierra Firme terminal)
- **Cartagena de Indias** (Colombia — Tierra Firme secondary)
- **Havana** (Cuba — assembly/return port)

**Secondary licensed ports (received redistribution, not direct fleet calls as a rule):**
- **Santo Domingo** (Hispaniola — early period, declined after 1600)
- **Santiago de Cuba**
- **Puerto Rico (San Juan)** — minor, often supplied by aviso ships
- **Campeche** (Gulf of Mexico, received secondary distribution from Veracruz)
- **Caracas/La Guaira** (Venezuela — notionally supplied, in practice massively undersupplied, which drove contraband)
- **Buenos Aires** — technically forbidden from direct Atlantic trade; legally supposed to receive goods via Lima over the Andes, a deliberately punitive route that made Buenos Aires the contraband capital of South America

**Crucial point for simulation:** The gap between licensed ports and actual colonial population created the structural conditions for contraband. Peripheral colonies — Cuba's eastern end, Venezuela, Río de la Plata, smaller Caribbean islands — were chronically undersupplied by the official system. Contraband was not incidental; it was **the primary supply mechanism** for much of the Spanish Caribbean outside the three or four main ports.

---

### 1.4 Inter-Colonial Trade Restrictions

**Formal rule:** Inter-colonial trade within the Spanish empire was heavily restricted to prevent colonies from becoming economically self-sufficient or independent of the Seville/Cádiz monopoly. Colonies were not supposed to trade manufactured goods with each other — only with Spain. Some inter-colonial food trade was tolerated (e.g., Mexican flour to the Caribbean) but required licenses.

**Prohibition on colonial manufacturing:** Spain systematically suppressed colonial industries that competed with Peninsular exports — wine, olive oil, and textile production in the Americas were periodically prohibited by royal decree (e.g., vine-growing forbidden in New Spain and Peru).

**Pacific-Atlantic barrier:** New Spain and Peru could trade across the Pacific (Manila galleons) but were legally supposed to channel all Atlantic-bound goods through the Casa system. The Lima–Portobelo–Spain route was the only legal silver egress for Peru.

---

### 1.5 Exclusion of Foreign Traders

**Absolute formal prohibition:** No foreigner could trade with Spanish colonies under any legal framework. This was enforced through:
- Casa de Contratación licensing requirements (only Spanish subjects with proof of *limpieza de sangre* — purity of blood — could obtain licenses, theoretically)
- *Guardacostas* (coast guard vessels) authorized to seize foreign ships in Spanish waters
- Capital penalties for foreign traders caught in colonial ports
- The *Consulado*'s monopoly making it economically difficult for unauthorized goods to enter the formal distribution chain

**Reality:** Enforcement collapsed at multiple points. Foreign merchants (especially French, then Dutch, then English) operated through **Spanish *testaferros*** (straw men/fronts) — Spanish subjects who nominally owned goods that were actually foreign-financed. By the late 17th century, the majority of goods officially loaded at Seville onto Spanish ships were actually owned by French, Genoese, or Flemish merchants using Spanish fronts (Stein & Stein estimate 50–75% of "Spanish" exports to the Indies were foreign-owned by the 1680s–1700s). The *Consulado* itself was often simply a collection mechanism for this foreign trade, not a genuine national monopoly.

**Situados:** Spain also used **situados** — annual silver subsidies shipped from Mexico to strategic Caribbean garrisons (Havana, Cartagena, Puerto Rico, Florida) to pay soldiers. These were a secondary legitimate cash flow, not commercial trade.

---

### 1.6 Spanish Tariff Rates

This is the most technically complex area. Multiple overlapping taxes applied at different points in the transaction chain.

**A. *Quinto Real* (Royal Fifth):** A 20% tax on all precious metals (silver and gold) extracted from American mines, paid at the mint before silver could enter commerce. This was the single most important fiscal instrument of the Spanish empire. In practice, the effective rate was lower because:
- Illegal *rescate* (unofficial silver sales) bypassed the mints entirely
- Mine operators underreported production
- The Crown periodically reduced the rate to a *diezmo* (10th, i.e., 10%) to encourage production during periods of mine exhaustion (e.g., reduced to 10% for Peru in 1735, but earlier reductions also occurred)

**B. *Almojarifazgo* (Import/Export Customs Duty):** Named from the Arabic *al-mushrif* (the overseer/tax collector), this was the basic customs duty on goods flowing through the Casa system.
- **On goods leaving Spain for the Indies:** Initially ~5% of declared value; raised and varied repeatedly. By the 17th century, approximately **7.5–15%** depending on the class of goods.
- **On goods arriving in Spain from the Indies:** Similar range, ~5–10%.
- **Colonial internal almojarifazgo:** Levied at colonial ports on goods unloaded there — approximately **2.5–5%** at Veracruz, Cartagena, Portobelo. This was additional to any duty paid in Spain.

**C. *Avería* (Convoy Tax/Naval Defense Levy):** The *avería* was an *ad valorem* levy on all registered cargo in the fleet system, used to pay for the warship escort. It was **not a fixed rate** — it was calculated annually based on the actual cost of outfitting the escorts divided across the fleet's registered cargo value.
- Rates fluctuated wildly: in good years with large fleets and peaceful seas, as low as **2–4%**
- In war years with heavy escort costs and small fleets, could reach **20–30%** of cargo value
- Collected by the *Consulado* on behalf of the Crown
- By the 1640s–1680s, the *avería* system was in decay as the fleet system itself became irregular; it was formally replaced/reformed under the Bourbons
- Historians (Hamilton, Haring) note that in the 17th century the effective avería burden on legally declared cargo was often equivalent to an additional **15–25%** on top of other taxes, making total fiscal burdens on registered goods potentially **40–60%** of original purchase value

**D. *Alcabala* (Sales Tax):** Applied at each transaction, in Spain and the colonies.
- Original rate: 5% (1341)
- Doubled to 10% (1491)
- Reduced back to 5% (1539)
- Applied in Mexico from 1574, Peru from 1591
- In practice, effective collection often 3–5% due to tax farming and evasion
- The Duke of Alba's infamous 10% alcabala in the Netherlands (1572) was a major cause of the Dutch Revolt — Spain learned to be more careful in the Americas, where rates were typically kept at 2–6% to avoid unrest
- Late 17th century colonial rates: approximately **2–4%** per transaction

**E. *Palmeo* (Volumetric Tax):** A tax on cargo by volume (*palmos* measurement) for goods not easily valued, supplementary to the almojarifazgo.

**F. *Unión de Armas* and other emergency levies:** Spain repeatedly imposed extraordinary wartime levies on the colonial trade; in the 17th century these were near-constant.

**Simulation-ready composite estimate:**

| Tax | Rate Range | Point of Collection |
|---|---|---|
| Quinto Real (silver) | 10–20% of silver value | At colonial mint |
| Almojarifazgo (export, Spain) | 7.5–15% | Seville/Cádiz |
| Almojarifazgo (import, colony) | 2.5–5% | Veracruz/Cartagena/Portobelo |
| Avería | 2–30% (avg. ~15% in 17thC) | Casa de Contratación |
| Alcabala | 2–10% per sale | At each colonial transaction |
| Palmeo + misc. | 2–5% | Various |
| **Effective total burden** | **~40–60% on legal goods** | Cumulative |

This crushing fiscal burden is precisely why contraband was economically rational. A smuggler avoiding all these taxes could sell at 30–40% below legal market price and still profit handsomely.

## II. ENGLAND — The Navigation Acts and Customs System

### 2.1 Navigation Act 1651 (Commonwealth)

**Passed:** 9 October 1651, by Cromwell's Rump Parliament.
**Primary target:** Dutch carrying trade.

**Key provisions:**
1. **No foreign ships** may import goods into England from Asia, Africa, or America — only ships with English owner, English master, and **majority English crew**.
2. European ships may import their **own country's products**, but cannot carry goods to England from third countries.
3. Fish imports/exports: prohibited in foreign ships.
4. Penalty: **forfeiture of ship and cargo**.

**Limitation:** Applied only to the ocean carrying trade, not to manufacturing or domestic trade. Declared void at the Restoration (1660) but immediately re-enacted in strengthened form.

---

### 2.2 Navigation Act 1660 (Restoration — Core Act)

**Passed:** 13 September 1660, Convention Parliament; confirmed by Cavalier Parliament 27 July 1661.
**Citation:** 12 Cha. 2 c. 18.
**This is the foundation of the entire Navigation Acts system.**

**Key provisions:**
1. **English bottoms requirement:** All colonial imports/exports restricted to ships "truly and without fraud belong[ing] only to the people of England" or built in English possessions. "England" = England, Ireland, Wales, and English plantations/colonies.
2. **75% English crew requirement** (up from "majority" in 1651). Captains required to post **performance bond** for compliance.
3. **Alien merchant exclusion:** No alien could "exercise the trade or occupation of a merchant or factor" in English colonial territories.
4. **Enumerated commodities** — the original list of goods that could ONLY be shipped to England or other English colonies:
   - **Sugar**
   - **Tobacco**
   - **Cotton wool**
   - **Indigo**
   - **Ginger**
   - **Fustic** (yellow dye-wood)
   - **Other dyeing woods**
   - *(Tobacco had been restricted since 1621; the others were new)*
5. Penalty: forfeiture of ship and cargo. Violations tried in "any court of record" or Admiralty.

---

### 2.3 Navigation Act 1663 (Staple Act)

**Passed:** 27 July 1663.
**Citation:** 15 Cha. 2 c. 7. Long title: "An Act for the Encouragement of Trade."
**Purpose:** Make England the mandatory entrepôt for ALL European goods destined for the colonies.

**Key provisions:**
1. **All European goods bound for English colonies MUST first pass through England.** Goods must be unloaded in England, inspected, approved, duties paid, then reloaded for colonial destination — even if the goods originated in a country closer to the colonies.
2. Must be carried in English vessels ("English bottoms").
3. Enumerated commodities must be landed in England and taxes paid before they could continue to other countries.
4. **Exemptions** (important for simulation):
   - Salt for New England and Newfoundland fisheries
   - Wine from **Madeira** and the **Azores** (major exemption — these islands could ship direct to colonies)
   - Provisions, servants, and horses from Scotland and Ireland

**Effect:** Increased shipping distances and costs significantly; made English colonial goods more expensive. Massively increased smuggling incentives.

---

### 2.4 Navigation Act 1673 (Plantation Duty Act)

**Passed:** 29 March 1673.
**Citation:** 25 Cha. 2 c. 7.
**Purpose:** Close a major loophole in inter-colonial trade.

**The loophole:** The 1660 Act required enumerated goods to go to England, but colonial ships trading *between English colonies* could move enumerated goods under bond (promising to ultimately deliver to England). In practice, they then sold the goods to foreign (especially Dutch) ships in other colonial ports, circumventing the England-destination requirement.

**Key provision:**
- **Plantation duty:** If a ship loaded enumerated commodities at a colonial port WITHOUT first obtaining the bond to carry them to England, it must pay export duties **at the colonial port of loading** as if the goods were entering England. These "plantation duties" were set at the same rates as English import duties.

**Specific plantation duty rates (1673):**
- **Sugar:** 5 shillings per hundredweight
- **Tobacco:** 1 penny per pound
- **Indigo:** 2 shillings per pound
- **Cotton wool:** 1 penny per pound
- **Ginger:** 1 shilling per hundredweight
- **Dyewoods:** 1 shilling per hundredweight
- **Cocoa beans** (added 1672): 1 penny per pound

**Additional:** The 1673 Act also established that **customs duties were now to be levied and collected by the Commissioners of Customs in England**, rather than locally — centralizing enforcement for the first time.

---

### 2.5 Navigation Act 1696 (Plantation Trade Act)

**Passed:** 10 April 1696.
**Citation:** 7 & 8 Will. 3 c. 22.
**Purpose:** Systematic enforcement tightening after decades of flagrant evasion.

**Key provisions:**
1. **Ship registration requirement:** All ships claiming English status must be **registered with proof of English ownership**, including an oath that no foreign owners exist.
2. **Governor oaths:** All colonial governors and officers must take a **formal oath** to enforce every clause of the Navigation Acts — making them personally liable for enforcement failures.
3. **Vice-admiralty court jurisdiction explicitly established** for colonial Navigation Act violations — removing cases from potentially sympathetic local juries.
4. Revenue split on prosecutions: **one-third to the Crown, one-third to the governor, one-third to the informer** — creating financial incentive for denunciation.
5. Exceptions: foreign-built prize ships and ships used to import naval stores.

---

### 2.7 English Tariff Rates on Imports

English customs rates on key colonial commodities imported to England (not plantation duties — these are the metropolitan import rates):

**Sugar (muscovado, raw):**
- 1660 customs rate: approximately **1 shilling 6 pence per hundredweight (cwt)** for English colonial sugar
- Foreign sugar: significantly higher, approximately **3–5 shillings per cwt**
- The differential strongly favored English colonial sugar and was intended to exclude foreign (especially French and Dutch) sugar from English markets
- Re-export allowance: most duties were rebated when goods were re-exported, making English entrepôts competitive

**Tobacco:**
- English colonial tobacco (Virginia, Maryland): approximately **2 pence per pound** import duty
- Foreign tobacco: approximately **4–6 pence per pound**
- The differential was enormous — colonial tobacco effectively monopolized the English market
- Note: Spanish tobacco from the Canaries or Cuba paid the full foreign rate and was largely shut out

**The Customs Book of Rates (1660):** Fixed specific duties on hundreds of commodities. Generally structured as: preferential rates for English colonial goods, higher rates for goods from allied/neutral powers, punitive rates for goods from enemy/excluded sources.

**Drawback system:** When enumerated goods were imported to England and then re-exported, most (typically 50–100%) of import duties were **drawn back** (refunded). This made England competitive as an entrepôt for redistributing colonial goods to Europe — a major source of English commercial profit.

---

### 2.8 Enforcement Mechanisms

**Naval Officers (colonial):** Each colonial port had a "naval officer" appointed by the governor to check ship papers and manifests. Pre-1696, these were often governor's cronies; notoriously corrupt.

**Customs commissioners:** After 1671, English Customs Commissioners had some colonial jurisdiction; the 1673 Act centralized collection further; the 1696 Act greatly strengthened their authority.

**Vice-admiralty courts:** Established in English colonies to try Navigation Act cases without juries. By the early 18th century, there were vice-admiralty courts in most major English colonial ports. They were:
- Presided over by Crown-appointed judges (not locally elected)
- **Juryless** — removing the sympathetic-jury problem
- Widely unpopular among colonial merchants
- In practice, still often ineffective: judges were sometimes locally captured; jurisdiction over creeks and bays was unclear; enforcement against powerful local merchants was politically fraught
- Estimated smuggling in the early 1700s: Crown officials estimated £500,000 annually in manufactured goods smuggled from the Netherlands alone into the English colonies (just outside our period but indicative of the endemic nature of the problem)

**Royal Navy:** Warships were tasked with enforcing the Navigation Acts but the Caribbean was vast and the navy thin. Privateers and pirates operated freely in the gaps.

## III. FRANCE — The *Exclusif* and Colonial Trade Regime

### 3.1 The *Exclusif* System

**What it was:** France's mercantilist colonial trade monopoly, mirroring Spain's system. French colonies could only trade with France, in French ships; foreign goods and foreign ships were formally excluded.

**Timeline of establishment:**
- **1664:** Louis XIV (guided by Colbert) creates the **Compagnie française des Indes occidentales** (French West India Company), granted a monopoly "on all commercial exchanges between France and all lands of our obedience in North and South America and the islands of America." Capital of ~3.3 million livres (1 million from Louis XIV, 1.3 million private). Operational base: Le Havre.
- The company was given a 40-year monopoly on all Caribbean and West African trade
- **1674:** The French West India Company was dissolved (could not survive the Franco-Dutch War of 1672–78 and financial difficulties). Trade reverted to private merchants under Crown regulation.
- **Post-1674:** The *exclusif* was maintained as royal policy through direct Crown regulation — no company monopoly, but the restriction on foreign trade with French colonies remained in force. The *exclusif* is best understood as a continuous policy from c.1664–1848 (when it was finally abolished), not just the company period.

**What the Exclusif restricted:**
- Foreign ships prohibited from trading with French colonies
- Colonial goods could only be exported to French metropolitan ports
- French colonies could only import goods from France (in theory)
- Colonial manufacturing of goods competing with French exports was restricted

**Which French ports could trade with colonies:**
- **Bordeaux** — dominant for wine, sugar refining, and Caribbean trade
- **Nantes** — major port, especially slave trade to Haiti/Martinique
- **La Rochelle** — significant role, especially early period
- **Le Havre** — French West India Company base, continued importance
- **Marseille** — primarily Mediterranean/Levant, less Caribbean
- **Rouen** — textile exports
- The trade was not restricted to a single port (unlike Spain's Seville monopoly) but was still heavily regulated through guild and merchant company licensing

**French Caribbean colonies in our period:**
- **Martinique** (French from 1635)
- **Guadeloupe** (French from 1635)
- **Saint-Domingue** (western Hispaniola — France formally recognized by Treaty of Ryswick 1697, though *de facto* French occupation from c.1650s)
- **Tortuga** (pirate/privateer base, French-controlled)
- Minor Antilles (Saint-Barthélemy, Marie-Galante, etc.)

---

### 3.2 French Tariff Rates

**Import duties on colonial goods into France:**
- Sugar: approximately **3–5 livres per 100 pounds** (varied by type — raw vs. refined)
- Tobacco: French authorities attempted monopoly control; the *Ferme générale* (tax farm) handled tobacco collection
- Indigo, cotton, cocoa: Various rates

**French colonial duties on European goods:**
- Approximately **3–5%** *ad valorem* on European imports into Martinique or Guadeloupe

**Differential for metropolitan vs. foreign goods:** Like England, France imposed punitive duties on foreign goods, making metropolitan French goods effectively the only legal option for colonists.

**Note on French tariff data:** French tariff rates for this period are less well-documented in English-language secondary literature than Spanish or English rates. Colbert's reform of 1664 created a comprehensive tariff schedule (*Tarif général*) but specific Caribbean rates require archival sources (Archives nationales d'outre-mer, Aix-en-Provence).

---

### 3.3 Enforcement vs. Smuggling

**Officially strict, practically permeable:** The French *exclusif* was famously leaky in practice. Key factors:

1. **Dutch trade:** The Dutch (especially from Curaçao and St. Eustatius) supplied French colonies massively during the 1640s–1660s before the *exclusif* was formalized; even after, clandestine Dutch-French colonial trade continued
2. **Geographic dispersion:** French Caribbean islands are small and have many anchorages — impossible to patrol adequately
3. **Colonial merchant interests:** French colonial planters preferred buying cheaper Dutch or English goods and selling to the best bidder; they frequently circumvented the exclusif
4. **Wartime exceptions:** During wars, French colonies were often formally or informally permitted to trade with neutral (especially Dutch) vessels because the French merchant marine could not supply them adequately under wartime disruption
5. **Estimated smuggling volume:** Contemporary French intendants reported that illegal trade (mostly with Dutch and English ships) supplied perhaps **30–50%** of colonial goods consumed in Martinique during peaceful periods, rising much higher during wars

## IV. THE DUTCH — Free Trade, WIC Monopoly, and Entrepôt System

### 4.1 Dutch West India Company (WIC) — Monopoly Terms

**Founded:** 3 June 1621, chartered by the States-General.
**Initial monopoly:** 24-year exclusive right to trade and colonize the Western Hemisphere (American coast from Newfoundland to Straits of Magellan) and West Africa (Tropic of Cancer to Cape of Good Hope). This was empire-wide — no other Dutch subject could legally trade in these regions without WIC license.

**First WIC (1621–1674):**
- Explicitly military-commercial: designed to wage war on Spain in the Americas while making a profit
- **1628:** Admiral Piet Hein captures the *entire* Spanish treasure fleet at Matanzas Bay — cargo valued at 11.5 million guilders, providing a massive dividend to shareholders and funding the subsequent Dutch offensive in Brazil
- **1624–1654:** Dutch Brazil (Pernambuco) — held Portuguese sugar-producing territory until Portuguese reconquest
- **1634:** Dutch occupy Curaçao (taken from Spain) — becomes the key Caribbean entrepôt
- The WIC was heavily oriented toward privateering/plunder in its early decades, less toward sustainable commerce
- **Financial collapse:** After loss of Brazil (1654) and the cost of wars, the first WIC was essentially bankrupt by the 1670s

**Reorganized WIC (1675–1791):**
- New charter granted 1675
- Focus shifted to the **slave trade** (Asiento-equivalent contracts with Spanish colonies) and maintaining Curaçao/St. Eustatius as free-trade entrepôts
- The WIC monopoly on the slave trade was the centerpiece; other Caribbean trade was increasingly open to individual Dutch merchants
- By the late 17th century, the WIC was less of a monopoly enforcer and more of a licensing body — actual trade was done largely by free merchants under WIC umbrella

**Key WIC monopoly dates for simulation:**
- 1621–1638: Hard monopoly enforced (Dutch free merchants excluded from Caribbean trade)
- 1638–1647: Partial opening — free merchants increasingly active
- 1647–1674: Progressive relaxation; WIC focus shifts to slave trade and Brazil
- 1674–1675: WIC dissolution/reorganization gap — effectively open trade
- 1675+: Second WIC, slave trade monopoly retained, general Caribbean trade open to Dutch free merchants

---

### 4.2 Curaçao and St. Eustatius as Free-Port Entrepôts

**Curaçao (Dutch from 1634):**
- Taken from Spain by the WIC in 1634; Spanish population expelled
- Became the **primary Dutch Caribbean entrepôt** — a staging point for redistributing European goods to Spanish, French, and English colonies
- Willemstad harbor: excellent deep-water port; small island with no significant agricultural production of its own, so its entire economy was trade
- **Function:** Dutch merchants brought European manufactures, slaves, and other goods to Curaçao; colonial merchants (including Spaniards and their fronts) came to buy them, circumventing their home-empire monopolies
- Also a major hub for the *asiento* (slave trade contract) operations — enslaved Africans were landed here before distribution to Spanish colonies
- Curaçao operated as a de facto free port throughout this period; Dutch import/export duties were minimal compared to competing colonial regimes

**St. Eustatius (Sint Eustatius, "Statia"):**
- Dutch WIC settled definitively 1636 (chamber of Zeeland)
- Small island (21 km²) in the northern Leeward Islands — geographically ideal for trade with both English and French Antilles
- **"The Golden Rock"** — by the early 18th century, one of the busiest trading ports in the Caribbean per unit of land
- 1678: St. Eustatius, Sint Maarten, and Saba transferred from WIC to direct Dutch government administration — increasing commercial freedom
- Key function: supplied English colonial planters with European manufactured goods (avoiding the Staple Act requirement), and purchased colonial produce at competitive prices, reselling to Dutch, French, and English markets
- During the American Revolution (outside our period but illustrative), St. Eustatius was supplying American rebels with war material — demonstrating the depth of its function as a circumvention hub

**How Dutch entrepôts facilitated smuggling for other empires' colonies:**

1. **Spanish colonies:** Spanish *testaferros* bought goods in Curaçao and smuggled them into Venezuela, Cuba, and other undersupplied ports. The Spaniards paid in silver, which is how enormous quantities of American silver ended up circulating in the Dutch Republic. Contemporary estimates (Stein & Stein) suggest that Curaçao handled **50,000–80,000 pesos worth of contraband trade** with Spanish Venezuela *per month* in peak years of the late 17th century.

2. **English colonies:** English planters in Barbados, Jamaica, and the Leeward Islands bought cheaper Dutch manufactures from St. Eustatius, avoiding the Staple Act's England-as-mandatory-entrepôt requirement. Dutch ships would appear in colonial waters flying neutral flags or claiming storm damage requiring emergency port calls. After the Anglo-Dutch Wars, "free ships make free goods" provisions in the 1667/1674 Treaties of Westminster gave the Dutch considerable legal cover.

3. **French colonies:** Dutch merchants supplied Martinique and Guadeloupe with goods France's merchant marine couldn't adequately provide. French colonial governors often turned a blind eye (and sometimes actively encouraged) Dutch trade during wars, when French colonial supply lines were disrupted.

---

### 4.3 Dutch Trade Policy Characteristics

**Relatively open domestic policy:** The Dutch Republic had a genuinely different commercial philosophy from Spain, England, and France. The Dutch economy was built on the carrying trade — transporting other nations' goods — and imposed relatively low import duties at home (generally 1–3% *ad valorem* on most goods). Amsterdam's grain market, money market, and commodity markets operated with great freedom.

**WIC restrictions vs. VOC (Dutch East India Company):** The VOC (East Indies) maintained much stricter monopoly control than the WIC (West Indies). The WIC's Caribbean monopoly was never as effectively enforced as the VOC's Asian monopoly, partly because the Caribbean was a contested multi-imperial zone with many loopholes and partly because WIC prioritized privateering over commerce enforcement.

**No *exclusif*-style restriction on Dutch colonial imports:** Unlike France and England, the Dutch did not require their Caribbean colonies to sell only to the Netherlands or to use Dutch ships exclusively. This made Dutch colonies *intrinsically more economically efficient* but also reduced Dutch metropolitan mercantile advantage.

**Piet Hein factor (1628):** The capture of the entire silver fleet yielded ~11.5 million guilders — roughly equivalent to 3–4 years of the Dutch West India Company's total anticipated revenue. This single event demonstrates the magnitude of Spanish silver flows and the enormous prizes available from disrupting them.

**The Seville *cargadores* fraud:** Stein & Stein's research (based on Casa de Contratación records and Archivo General de Indias) found that by the 1680s–1700s, **50–75%** of goods officially loaded in Seville onto the treasure fleets were actually owned by foreign merchants using Spanish fronts, not by genuine Spanish *cargadores*. This is "legal" trade in the formal sense (it went through Casa registration) but violated the spirit of the entire system. The Spanish Crown was essentially collecting taxes on foreign commerce.

**Consequences when caught (Spanish jurisdiction):**
- **Ship confiscated** (always)
- **Cargo confiscated** (always)
- **Capital punishment** (formally, for foreign traders in Spanish ports) — rarely actually executed on European merchants in peacetime; more often commuted to fines and expulsion
- **Imprisonment and ransom** — common outcome

**Consequences when caught (English jurisdiction):**
- **Ship and cargo forfeited** (1651, 1660 Acts)

- In practice, governors often looked the other way pre-1696 and even after

**Bribery modifier:** In all jurisdictions, bribery of local officials dramatically reduced effective detection rates. This was endemic — colonial governors, port captains, and customs officers were systematically underpaid and routinely corruptible. A simulation should model bribery as a variable that can reduce effective detection probability by 50–80%.

## VI. WARTIME MODIFICATIONS

### 6.1 Trading with the Enemy

**General principle:** War formally suspended all commerce between belligerents. Citizens of warring nations were technically "enemies" of each other (even as private traders), and trading with the enemy was in theory treasonous.

**Reality — Practical exceptions:**
1. **Neutral flags:** Dutch ships flying neutral flags could deliver goods to ports of either belligerent, claiming neutrality. The "free ships make free goods" principle (codified in Anglo-Dutch treaties of 1667 and 1674) provided legal cover for Dutch neutrality. This made the Dutch invaluable as supply conduits during wars.
2. **Trading licenses:** Belligerent governments sometimes issued **licenses** permitting specific merchants to trade with the enemy (usually to obtain strategic goods). England used this extensively in wars with France — English merchants obtained French licenses, and vice versa.
3. **Colonial necessity exceptions:** Spanish colonial governors facing starvation sometimes formally or informally permitted enemy merchant ships to provision colonies during wars, especially if the *flota* had not arrived.
4. **Prize and counter-prize:** When goods were seized, they became prize property — the seizure itself was a form of "trade" in strategic goods.

### 6.2 Neutral Flags and Prize Law

**The core rule (from Grotius forward):** A captured ship was a legitimate prize of war if:
- The capturing vessel had a valid **letter of marque** (commission from a sovereign)
- The prize ship belonged to an enemy nation
- The cargo was enemy property

**Neutral goods on enemy ships:** Subject to debate, but generally: neutral goods on enemy ships *could* be condemned as prize (the English "war contraband" doctrine was expansive). The counter-doctrine — "free ships make free goods" — held that neutral ship nationality protected the cargo. This was contested throughout the period.

**Contraband at sea:** Even in peacetime, some goods (naval stores, weapons, provisions for military use) could be declared contraband and seized from neutral ships bound to enemy ports. The definition of "contraband" was contested and expanded by the seizing power.

**Prize adjudication procedure:**
1. Capturing vessel brings prize to a home/allied port
2. Prize is condemned in **Admiralty Court** (England: High Court of Admiralty; France: *Conseil des prises*)
3. If condemned, ship and cargo sold at auction; proceeds distributed by formula
4. English formula (roughly consistent c. 1650–1720): Captain ~10–15%, officers share ~10–15%, crew share ~30–40%, owners ~30–40% (the exact formula was set by contract between ship owners and crew at outset of voyage)
5. Crown/Admiralty typically received a **1/10th share** of prize proceeds ("tenth prize")

**Privateering Economics (Simulation Data):**

| Cost Category | Typical Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Outfitting a privateer sloop (80–150 tons) | £1,000–£3,000 | Includes guns, powder, food for 6-month voyage |
| Outfitting a privateer frigate (200–400 tons) | £5,000–£15,000 | Major investment requiring wealthy backers |

| Required prize to break even (sloop) | 1–2 modest prizes | |
| Required prize to make large profit (sloop) | 1 major prize | Highly skewed distribution — most crews made modest returns; rare captures were fortunes |

**Letter of Marque procedure:**
1. Applicant petitioned the sovereign (England: Lord Admiral/Admiralty; France: Secretary of State for the Navy; Dutch: States-General or Admiralty Colleges)
2. Posted a **performance bond** (typically £1,500–£3,000 for English privateers) — forfeited if unlawful prizes taken
3. Commission specified: enemy nationality, duration, one specific ship, named officers
4. Prizes must be brought to home Admiralty court for condemnation before proceeds distributed
5. Commission expired on peace treaty; continuing to raid after expiry = piracy

**Henry Morgan example (illustrative of privateering economics at scale):** Morgan's 1670–71 expedition against Panama: fleet of ~36 ships, ~2,000 men; result was a total prize of approximately **£75,000 in goods and coin** (though Morgan faced legal trouble because England and Spain were technically not at war). Large-scale privateer expeditions were essentially joint-stock military ventures with expected returns of 50–200%+ if successful, and total loss if defeated or caught.

## VII. KEY SECONDARY SOURCE ALIGNMENT

The following notes align the findings above with the sources you specified:

**Harper (1939) — *The English Navigation Laws*:** The primary authority on English Navigation Act provisions and their enforcement. Harper documents the shift from the 1651 Act (primarily targeting Dutch carrying trade) to the 1660–1663–1673 system (comprehensive mercantilist framework), arguing enforcement was gradually tightened but never complete. His data on the enumerated goods list, plantation duties, and 1696 enforcement reform align precisely with what is reported above.

**Klooster (2003) — *Illicit Riches: Dutch Trade in the Caribbean, 1648–1795*:** The definitive work on Dutch contraband operations. Klooster's quantitative estimates for Curaçao-Venezuela trade (~50,000–80,000 pesos/month) and his analysis of how the Dutch WIC reorganization (1675) transformed the WIC from a military-commercial company into a trade-regulation body are the source for the Dutch entrepôt analysis above.

**Pares (1936) — *War and Trade in the West Indies, 1739–1763*:** While primarily covering the post-1720 period, Pares establishes the structural conditions of the earlier period. His analysis of English customs enforcement and the "inherent contradiction" between the Navigation Acts' theory and colonial planters' economic interests is foundational. His estimates of English colonial smuggling rates and the structural weakness of vice-admiralty courts before 1720 are used above.

**Stein and Stein (2000) — *Silver, Trade, and War: Spain and America in the Making of Early Modern Europe*:** The primary authority for Spanish tariff analysis. Their examination of the *avería*, *almojarifazgo*, and *alcabala* structures, and especially their stunning finding that 50–75% of "Spanish" exports on the treasure fleets were actually foreign-owned goods (using Spanish fronts), underpins the assessment of Spanish enforcement collapse in the late 17th century.

**Fisher (1985) — *Commercial Relations between Spain and Spanish America in the Era of Free Trade, 1778–1796*:** While outside our period, Fisher's analysis of what Spanish trade *reform* achieved provides the baseline for understanding how dysfunctional the pre-reform system was. His data on tariff rates under the earlier system (reconstructed from reform discussions) are consistent with the 40–60% total fiscal burden figure.
