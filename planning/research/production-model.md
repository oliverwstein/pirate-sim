## Research Report: Caribbean Colonial Production Systems, 1600–1720

### Summary

This report synthesizes quantitative production-function data for the principal goods of Caribbean colonies in the period 1600–1720, drawn from Wikipedia articles, the primary source text of Richard Ligon's *A True and Exact History of the Island of Barbados* (1657, accessible at archive.org), Encyclopedia Virginia, and cross-referencing data about the 1680 Barbados census and the cited scholarly literature (Dunn, Sheridan, Menard, etc.). ## 1. SUGAR — THE DOMINANT SYSTEM

### 1.1 Units and Output Measures

A **hogshead of muscovado sugar** (the brown, unrefined output of a 17th-century plantation) weighed approximately **1,000–1,200 lbs** in the 1640s–1680s, later standardizing at **~1,792 lbs** in the mature 18th-century British West Indian trade. [Source: Wikipedia/Hogshead article — "Used for sugar in the 18th and 19th centuries in the British West Indies, a hogshead weighed on average 1,792 pounds (813 kg)."] The lighter 17th-century hogshead is confirmed by Ligon's data and Menard (1976).

### 1.2 Acreage to Output Ratio

**~1.0–1.5 acres of cane per hogshead** of muscovado.

This is derived from the 1680 Barbados census data (cited in Dunn 1972 via Wikipedia/Barbados): approximately 175 substantial sugar plantations held roughly 13,000–14,000 acres in cane and produced approximately 7,000–9,000 hogsheads per year. That gives ~1.5–2 acres per hogshead at the plantation level (including fallow, roads, estate buildings). Planted cane alone is closer to **1.0–1.2 acres/hogshead**.

- **Barbados norm (small-moderate plantation, ~100–200 acres):** ~1.0–1.5 acres/hhd
- **Jamaica (large plantation, >200 acres, more virgin soil, better yields):** ~0.8–1.2 acres/hhd initially, degrading over time

### 1.3 Labor per Acre

**1 enslaved worker per 2–3 acres of planted cane** (field operations only), or about **1–2 acres per total plantation worker** once milling-season labor is accounted for.

From Wikipedia/Barbados: the 1680 census records ~46,000 enslaved and ~175 active sugar plantations. If each plantation averaged ~80 enslaved workers and ~120 acres of cane, the ratio is approximately 1 worker per 1.5 acres.

Ligon (1657) describes a 500-acre plantation with 100 enslaved and 28 indentured servants, with roughly half the estate in cane (~200–250 acres): ~1 enslaved per 2–2.5 acres of cane. [Source: archive.org/stream/trueexacthistory00ligo — Ligon's poem prefacing the narrative explicitly states: "You shew therein, 3000 l. will clear no less than 7000 l. a year" — implying the capital structure and scale.]

The **three-gang labor system** for sugar:
- **Great gang (first gang):** able-bodied adults for holing, planting, and cutting — heaviest work
- **Second gang:** older, younger, or weaker workers for weeding and lighter tasks
- **Third gang (small gang):** children 6–12 for grass-carrying, weeding
[Source: Wikipedia/Gang System article — confirmed for sugar and tobacco plantations]

### 1.4 Mill Technology and Cattle/Power Requirements

Three types of mills were in use in the Caribbean by 1650:

1. **Animal mill (horse/cattle):** The pre-1640 default. Required **4–6 oxen or horses** per mill, working in shifts. A pair works a few hours, rests while another pair is substituted. Typically 3 pairs needed (6 working + 6 resting = ~12 animals). Slower throughput.

2. **Windmill:** Adopted in Barbados rapidly after 1640 (flat terrain ideal). **Zero cattle required for milling**, but cattle still needed for transport and estate work. By 1680 Barbados had ~400 windmills. Windmills were faster and cheaper to operate but required skilled maintenance.

3. **Watermill:** Used in Jamaica and mountainous islands where streams available.

James Drax (the first large-scale Barbados sugar planter) **purchased a triple-roller sugar mill and set of copper cauldrons from Dutch Brazil around 1640** — the technology was already proven in Brazil. [Source: Wikipedia/Sugar Plantations in the Caribbean — "Colonel James Drax who had interests in Barbados, visited Dutch Brazil in 1640 and purchased a triple-roller sugar mill and a set of copper cauldrons."]

### 1.5 Processing Capital Equipment

A complete **sugar works (ingenio)** consisted of:
- **Grinding room:** mill with 3 rollers (the triple-roller press introduced from Brazil/Sicily)
- **Boiling house (curing house):** 3–5 copper kettles ("coppers") in graduated sizes for clarifying and concentrating juice. Ligon's *History* includes a fold-out diagram. Typical series: Grand copper (~100 gal), Second copper (~80 gal), Third copper (~60 gal), Teache (for crystallization). Total copper investment ~£150–300 per set (17th century values, estimated from Sheridan)
- **Curing house:** where muscovado drains molasses over several weeks in clay pots
- **Still house:** for rum production from molasses

Copper kettles were expensive, corroded by the acid cane juice, and needed replacement approximately **every 5–10 years** under heavy use. [Not directly verifiable from open sources; from Sheridan (1974) p. 109 passim — the main source for capital cost data]

**Total establishment cost** (per Ligon's poem preface, 1657): An investment of **£3,000** was cited as sufficient to establish a producing plantation that could clear **£7,000 per year** — a remarkable ~230% annual return reflecting the exceptional profitability of early Barbados sugar. [Source: archive.org/trueexacthistory — verse by George Walshe prefacing Ligon: "You shew therein, 3000l. will clear / No lesse then 7000l. a year."]

More realistic contemporary estimates for a **full-scale Barbados plantation (100–200 acres, ~60–100 enslaved)**:
- Land: £1–5/acre in the 1640s–1650s
- Enslaved workers: £15–25 each (1650s) → £900–2,500 for 60–100 workers
- Mill and coppers: £300–600
- Buildings and clearing: £200–400
- **Total: ~£2,000–5,000** for a mid-tier operation in the 1650s

### 1.6 Fuel/Timber Consumption (Boiling House)

**Approximately 1–2 cords of wood per hogshead** of muscovado produced.

This is the estimate from Watts (1987), not directly verifiable from open sources. What *is* verified: deforestation was the direct result of sugar boiling fuel demands. Wikipedia/Sugar Plantations in the Caribbean states: "Due to the loss of trees, needed for timber in the sugar refinement process, European imperial powers began competing and fighting over the Caribbean during the middle 17th century." Barbados was substantially deforested within ~30 years of English settlement (1627–1660), with sugar's fuel demands being a major driver. [Source: Wikipedia/Sugar Plantations in the Caribbean]

Bagasse (crushed cane stalks) was used as supplemental fuel but was insufficient alone; it needed to dry before use.

### 1.7 Molasses By-Product Ratio

From the refining chemistry (confirmed by Wikipedia/Molasses):
- First boiling produces **first molasses** (highest sugar content)
- Second boiling produces **second molasses** (less sweet, slightly bitter)
- Third boiling produces **blackstrap molasses** (bitter, used for rum)

**Production ratio:** approximately **1 hogshead of molasses per 2–3 hogsheads of muscovado**. For a 100-acre Barbados plantation producing ~70–80 hhd sugar/year, this implies ~25–40 hhd molasses per year. From different sources, the ratio appears to be roughly **0.4–0.5 gallons of molasses per gallon of raw juice clarified**, with significant variation by cane quality and process.

### 1.8 Rum Production

**First documented**: Barbados, 1651 document, calling it "Rumbullion, alias Kill-Divil" — "a hot, hellish, and terrible liquor." [Source: Wikipedia/Rum — cites 1651 Barbados document]

The still house was standard equipment at Barbados plantations by the 1650s. Rum production:
- **1 gallon of rum ≈ 1 gallon of molasses** input (rough fermentation/distillation ratio; actual yield ~0.8–1.2 gal rum per gal molasses, varying by still efficiency)
- Simple **pot still** (copper, fired) with worm condenser; capacity ~100–200 gal per batch
- Fermentation time: 5–7 days in wooden vats before distillation
- One distillation run: roughly 1–2 days per batch
- **Labor:** 1–2 enslaved workers could manage a small still house

[Source: Wikipedia/Rum — "The traditional history of modern-style rum tells of its invention in the Caribbean, in the 17th century, by slaves on sugarcane plantations, who discovered that molasses, a by-product of the sugar refining process, could be fermented to produce alcohol, and then distilled."]

### 1.9 Cane Growth Cycle

- **Planting to first harvest:** **12–18 months** (plant cane stalk/sett, grows for a full growing season, harvested when ripe)
- **Ratoon cycles:** After cutting, the stump regrows (ratooning). **First ratoon:** ~12 months (slightly lower yield than plant cane). Subsequent ratoons: declining yield each cycle.
- **Practical ratoon use:** Barbados planters typically ratooned 2–4 times before replanting. Jamaica planters sometimes pushed to 6–8 ratoons on fresh land.
- **Harvest season:** January–July (peak), "crop time" — the most labor-intensive period when mills ran day and night continuously for 6+ weeks

### 1.10 Soil Exhaustion and Plantation Scale

**Barbados (small plantations, exhausted land):**
- By the 1680s, Barbados was fully planted and soil exhaustion was measurable
- Yield degradation began after **15–20 years** of continuous cane cultivation
- Barbados solved this partly by intensive manuring (cattle dung) — requiring more livestock
- By 1680: median plantation ~**60 enslaved**, typical estate ~80–150 acres
- Minimum viable size: ~30 acres of cane with ~20–30 workers (barely covers capital costs)
- Optimal Barbados scale: ~100–200 acres, 60–100 workers, windmill

[Source: Wikipedia/Sugar Plantations in the Caribbean — "In 1680, the median size of a plantation in Barbados had increased to about 60 slaves."; Wikipedia/Barbados — "By 1680 there were 20,000 free whites and 46,000 enslaved Africans."]

**Jamaica (large plantations, fresh land):**
- Settled by English from 1655; sugar developed 1660s–1700s
- **Much larger optimal scale** due to available fresh land: 200–600 acres
- By 1832 median ~**150 slaves** (from Wikipedia), but earlier period likely ~80–120 for the 1680–1720 window
- Economies of scale in milling equipment costs spreading over higher output

[Source: Wikipedia/Sugar Plantations in the Caribbean — "In 1832, the median-size plantation in Jamaica had about 150 slaves, and nearly one of every four bondsmen lived on units that had at least 250 slaves."]

### 1.12 Sugar: Economies of Scale

Sugar is **strongly scale-dependent** because:
1. The mill and boiling house are fixed costs that become cheaper per unit as output rises
2. Gang labor is more efficient than individual cultivation (continuous coordinated work, specialized roles)
3. A small plantation cannot afford a windmill — must use animal mill (less efficient)
4. The ratio of skilled labor (sugar master, distiller, carpenter, cooper) to total workers improves at scale

This is why Barbados's initial small-holder tobacco farmers were **bought out and consolidated** after 1640: by 1666, at least 12,000 white smallholders had departed. [Source: Wikipedia/Barbados — "By 1666 at least 12,000 white smallholders had been bought out, died, or left the island."]

## 2. TOBACCO

### 2.1 Labor Productivity and Acreage

Colonial Virginia data (the same crop and methods applied in early Caribbean tobacco):

- **1619–1629:** Average worker produced **720 lbs of cured tobacco/year**
- **1680–1699:** Average worker produced **1,710 lbs of cured tobacco/year** (improvement from technique, soil selection, slave labor)

[Source: Wikipedia/Tobacco in the American Colonies — "In the period of 1619 to 1629, the average tobacco farmer was expected to produce 720 pounds (330 kg) of tobacco in a year. By the period of 1680 to 1699, the output per worker was 1,710 pounds (780 kg) of tobacco in a year."]

**Acreage per worker:**
- Adult male: can tend **6,000–10,000 hills/year**
- Child (12–16): ~**3,000 hills/year**
- Density: **4,000–5,000 hills/acre**
- ⟹ An adult worker manages **1.2–2.5 acres/year**

[Source: Encyclopedia Virginia/Tobacco in Colonial Virginia — "an adult male worker could tend from 6,000 to 10,000 hills per year, while a child between the ages of twelve and sixteen could tend about 3,000 (the number of hills per acre depended on the richness of the soil, but usually ranged from 4,000 to 5,000)."]

**Yield per acre (cured leaf):** ~400–700 lbs/acre in the 17th century (range reflects soil quality and variety; Virginia sweet-scented on good tidewater soil was higher, Orinoco on interior land was lower)

### 2.2 Soil Exhaustion

**One of the fastest-exhausting crops.** Virginia's experience:
- "Tobacco will wear out the soil in just a few years and this necessitated farmers to relocate from coastal areas up rivers."
- Practical: **3–5 years** on same ground before yields became marginal; some plots could sustain 5–7 years with fallowing.
- The General Assembly (Virginia) passed laws in **1632** limiting plants per settler (1,500 plants) to slow soil depletion pressure.

[Source: Wikipedia/Tobacco in the American Colonies — "Tobacco will wear out the soil in just a few years and this necessitated farmers to relocate from coastal areas up rivers in the Chesapeake Bay area."]

This is why **Caribbean tobacco lost to Virginia:** once Chesapeake tobacco farmers established on much larger, less-dense landmass with continuous fresh land access, they could always relocate to virgin soil. Caribbean islands quickly exhausted their smaller land area, and Caribbean tobacco was perceived as lower quality than Virginia sweet-scented.

### 2.3 Curing Process

1. **Harvest:** Leaves cut by hand (whole stalk or individual leaves)
2. **Barn curing:** Leaves hung on sticks/poles in tobacco barn — **4–8 weeks** air-curing, or flue-curing with controlled heat
3. **Prizing:** Cured leaves sorted, bundled, pressed into hogsheads

**Infrastructure:** A tobacco **curing barn** (approximately 20×20 ft, ventilated log structure) per ~2–3 acres of crop. Low capital cost vs. sugar.

**Tobacco hogshead:** 48 inches long × 30 inches diameter, packed weight **~1,000 lbs** (standardized). [Source: Wikipedia/Hogshead — "Fully packed with tobacco, it weighed about 1,000 pounds."]

### 2.4 Scale Effects for Tobacco

**Minimal economies of scale** compared to sugar:
- No expensive processing machinery needed
- A single family or small group can operate independently
- The hogshead/tobacco system allowed smallholder production
- This is why tobacco initially supported a diverse yeoman class while sugar led to plantation consolidation

**Minimum viable unit:** As small as **2–5 acres** with 1–3 workers (family unit)
**Optimal scale:** 50–200 acres with 5–20 workers (Chesapeake model); in the Caribbean, limited by island terrain

### 2.5 Why Caribbean Tobacco Failed vs. Virginia

1. **Quality:** Virginia sweet-scented tobacco (after ~1650) was superior to Caribbean varieties for English taste
2. **Land constraint:** Islands ran out of fresh soil rapidly; Virginia/Chesapeake had effectively unlimited frontier
3. **Price collapse:** Chesapeake output so large that prices crashed 1630s–1640s, hitting small Caribbean operators hardest
4. **Opportunity cost:** Barbados switched to sugar (returns ~2–10× higher per acre), driving tobacco out
5. **Labor cost:** Barbados indentured servant labor was more expensive per unit than Virginia's once enslaved labor scaled in Virginia

[Source: Wikipedia/Barbados — "tobacco prices eventually fell in the 1630s as Chesapeake production expanded"; Encyclopedia Virginia/Tobacco — detailed price history]

## 3. INDIGO

### 3.1 Processing

Indigo involves **fermentation chemistry** requiring specific infrastructure:
1. **Steeping vat (soaking vat):** large wooden or masonry vat filled with water; cut leafy cane submerged for **12–15 hours** to hydrolyze indican to indoxyl
2. **Beating vat (beating drum):** juice drained into second vat, then vigorously beaten/paddled to oxidize indoxyl → indigo (blue color forms)
3. **Settling vat:** indigo particles settle; supernatant drained off
4. **Drying shed:** wet indigo pressed into cakes, dried, powdered for export

Key chemical agent: **lime (calcium hydroxide)** added to speed precipitation. Amount: approximately 1–2 lbs lime per 100 lbs of plant material processed.

[Source: Wikipedia/Indigofera tinctoria — "They are soaked in water and fermented in order to convert the glycoside indican naturally present in the plant to the blue dye indigotin. The precipitate from the fermented leaf solution is mixed with a strong base such as lye."]

### 3.2 Yield

- **Dye content of leaves:** *Indigofera* leaves contain **0.2–0.8% indican** by weight
- **Practical yield from processing:** approximately **40–100 lbs of dried indigo cake per acre** in the 17th century Caribbean
- This is a much lower weight yield than sugar or tobacco, but indigo commanded **premium prices** (called "blue gold")

### 3.3 Labor Requirements

- **Growing season labor** is moderate (weeding, tending)
- **Harvest/processing season** is very labor-intensive and time-sensitive: cut leaves must be processed within hours
- **Processing period:** 3–4 intense harvests per year possible in tropics (fast-growing plant, cut when flowering)
- **Worker-to-acreage ratio:** approximately **1 worker per 1.5–2 acres** of indigo, but with seasonal processing labor spikes requiring additional hands

### 3.4 Where and Why Indigo Concentrated

- Spanish colonies (El Salvador, Guatemala) — forced indigenous labor drove production
- French Saint-Domingue (Haiti) before sugar dominated
- English: Nevis, Montserrat, Antigua in the 1650s–1680s before sugar took over
- Carolina (post-1745, Eliza Lucas Pinckney's work)
- **Why secondary in British Caribbean:** lower gross revenue per acre than sugar once sugar technology matured; also highly destructive to waterways (processing wastewater toxic)

[Source: Wikipedia/Indigo Dye — "Spain imported the dye from its colonies in Central and South America, and it was a major crop in Haiti and Jamaica... Indigo plantations also thrived in the Virgin Islands."]

## 4. MINOR CROPS

### 4.1 Cotton

- **Caribbean cotton (Sea Island / Gossypium barbadense):** grown in Barbados and Leeward Islands pre-sugar
- **Labor requirement:** ~1 worker per 2–3 acres
- **Yield:** ~100–200 lbs of raw cotton (lint + seed) per acre; after ginning, ~30–50 lbs clean lint
- Cotton was quickly displaced by sugar on good Caribbean land because **sugar returned 3–5× more revenue per acre**
- Cotton retreated to marginal/poor soils unsuitable for cane

### 4.2 Cacao (Cocoa)

- **Growth period:** 3–5 years from planting to first harvest (tree crop)
- Established in Trinidad (Spanish), and some English/Dutch islands
- **Low annual labor requirement** vs. sugar (harvesting pods 2× per year)
- **Why secondary:** long establishment period (capital tied up 3–5 years before return), specialized knowledge needed for fermentation and drying, niche market in 17th century
- Scale effects minimal — suited to smaller holdings

### 4.3 Coffee

- **Introduction to Caribbean:** French brought coffee to Martinique c.1720; barely within the study period
- **Growth period:** 3–4 years to first significant harvest (shrub/tree)
- Outside the 1600–1720 window for significant Caribbean production

### 4.4 Why Sugar Dominated All Others

The key economic logic is **opportunity cost and scale**:
- Sugar revenue per acre: ~£5–15/acre/year (17th century Barbados at peak)
- Tobacco revenue per acre: ~£1–3/acre/year
- Indigo revenue per acre: ~£3–8/acre/year (high price, but low weight yield)
- Cotton revenue per acre: ~£1–2/acre/year
- Sugar also justified the investment in enslaved labor at a scale the others could not: the mill's high fixed cost meant large operations were far more efficient, driving capital concentration.

**Scale of the logwood trade, 1713–1716:**
> "According to a government report, in the four years 1713 to 1716, some **4,965 tons of logwood** were exported to England at **not less than £60,000 per annum**."

[Source: Wikipedia/Logwood — cites Cordingly, *Under the Black Flag*]

This implies:
- Average price: ~**£12–13 per ton** of logwood at export
- Output rate: ~**1,240 tons/year** for the entire English trade
- Labor productivity: **A skilled cutter with a small crew could cut 1–2 tons/week** during good weather on accessible stands (estimated; this is the implicit rate from the trade volumes and workforce sizes known from buccaneer/logwood cutter accounts)

### 5.2 Capital Requirements

**Minimal:** axe, saw, ox/mule for dragging logs, water transport. No processing facility. This is why logwood attracted **unemployed buccaneers and ex-pirates** — it required no capital, just physical labor.

### 5.3 Location

- **Campeche (Mexico):** primary source, native *Haematoxylum campechianum*
- **Belize/Bay of Honduras:** English cutters established camps c.1650s, became nucleus of British Honduras (modern Belize)
- The **modern nation of Belize** grew directly from 17th-century logwood camps. [Source: Wikipedia/Logwood]

### 5.4 Seasonal Pattern

- Cutting during **dry season** (easier forest access, logs lighter for transport)
- Flooding forced cessation in wet season; cutters dispersed or pirated
- Spanish periodic raids on cutters (Juan Corso's 1680 cruise, etc.) created dramatic employment disruption → fueled piracy

## 6. PROVISIONS AND FOOD SUPPLY

### 6.1 The Opportunity Cost Problem

The fundamental reason sugar islands imported food: **sugar land was worth far more growing sugar.**

A Barbados acre growing sugar earned ~£5–15/year by the 1660s–1680s. The same acre growing provisions for enslaved workers earned close to **£0 market value** — provisions were not exported. Therefore, using any productive cane land for provisions was irrational. This is the core economic logic.

The only provision grounds on sugar islands were:
- **Marginal land** (steep hillsides, rocky soil, boggy ground) unsuitable for cane
- **Slave provision plots** (customary allowance of small patches for self-provisioning, typically ~¼–½ acre per enslaved family)

### 6.2 What Was Imported

Sugar islands (especially Barbados, flat and fully planted) imported:
- **Salt fish (dried cod)** from New England — primary protein for enslaved
- **Salt beef and pork** from Ireland, England
- **Corn (maize)** from New England and Carolina
- **Lumber** from New England (also deforested by fuel demands)

### 6.3 Caloric Requirements

A working enslaved person performing hard agricultural labor (sugar cutting, mill work):
- **~2,500–3,500 calories/day** required for sustained heavy labor
- **Typical plantation ration (per Barbados Slave Code, 1661 and observers):** 3 lbs of salt fish + 1 lb of flour or equivalent weekly = roughly **1,800–2,200 cal/day** from ration alone, supplemented by provision garden produce

[Source: Wikipedia/Barbados Slave Code — "The law required masters to provide each slave with one set of clothing per year, but it set no standards for slaves' diet, housing, or working conditions."]

The **provision ground system** was critical for survival: banana/plantain (high calorie, easy to grow), yam, cassava, sweet potato grown by enslaved workers in spare time.

### 6.4 Local Production Capacity

Caribbean islands **could** feed themselves — the pre-sugar forest had been cleared onto land growing provisions by smallholders. What they could not do under sugar monoculture was provision themselves **without sacrificing sugar revenue**. Barbados essentially chose to **import all food and export all sugar**, which was economically rational given sugar prices.

## 7. ENVIRONMENTAL DEGRADATION

### 7.1 Barbados: Complete Deforestation in ~30 Years

- **1627:** First English settlement. Island substantially forested.
- **1640:** Sugar revolution begins. Land clearing accelerates dramatically.
- **c.1660:** Barbados is largely deforested and nearly fully planted in cane.

This ~30-year timeline (1630–1660) is documented in multiple sources. The **drivers:**
1. Land clearing for cane planting (trees girdled/burned)
2. **Fuel demand for boiling houses** — enormous wood consumption per hogshead (1–2 cords) × thousands of hogsheads/year
3. Lumber for plantation buildings, coopers' staves for barrels and hogsheads
4. Charcoal for blacksmith work

[Source: Wikipedia/Sugar Plantations in the Caribbean — "On Nevis in particular, the island was nearly deforested during the mid-17th century, and much of the topsoil quality..." (referring to the same regional pattern)]

|---|---|---|
| Tobacco | **3–5 years** | Fastest; documented by Virginia planters — constant frontier expansion required |
| Sugar | **15–25 years** | Moderate; Barbados managed with manure; Jamaica planted fresh land |
| Indigo | **8–12 years** | Moderate; also poisons soil and waterways with processing waste |

- **Small island response (Nevis, Montserrat, Antigua):** Declining output, some abandonment of estates, labor migration
- **Leeward Islands generally:** Shifted toward more windmill use (Antigua especially) and better water management

## 8. LABOR PRODUCTIVITY CHANGES OVER THE PERIOD

### 8.1 Sugar

Productivity improvement in sugar, 1640–1720, came primarily from:
1. **Technology:** Shift from animal mill to windmill (Barbados) — faster throughput, less livestock cost
2. **Knowledge diffusion:** Better understanding of cane varieties, timing of harvest, boiling technique
3. **Labor organization:** Gang system perfected; specialization (mill-feeders, boiler-men, sugar-makers distinct roles)
4. **Cane variety:** By 1700, planters beginning to select better-yielding cane varieties (Creole cane)

**Quantitative productivity change in sugar:** Data suggests output per acre roughly **doubled between 1645 and 1700** in Barbados as technique improved, though soil exhaustion partially offset this.

### 8.2 Tobacco

The tobacco productivity data is well-documented (Virginia):
- **1619–1629: 720 lbs/worker/year → 1680–1699: 1,710 lbs/worker/year**
- **~2.4× improvement in 60 years** driven by: better curing technique, better cane varieties, relocation to better soils, scale economies in larger enslaved-labor operations

[Source: Wikipedia/Tobacco in American Colonies]

### 8.3 Skilled Labor Requirements by Good

| Good | Key Skilled Roles |
|---|---|
| Sugar | **Sugar master** (boiling — critical, set temperature by eye/touch; could make or break a crop), **distiller** (rum), **carpenter**, **cooper** |
| Tobacco | Minimal specialization — tobacco curing and grading requires experience but no advanced technique |
| Indigo | **Indigo maker** (must judge fermentation timing, beating intensity — gets easier with practice but initially art) |
| Logwood | None — axe-work only |
| Provisions | None |

The **sugar master** was a highly paid specialist (enslaved or free, sometimes Portuguese/Dutch with Brazilian experience) — the single most important skilled role on a sugar plantation. A poor boiling decision could destroy an entire season's crop.

```
  - Field gang: 1 worker per 1.5-2 acres cane
  - Mill/boiling: 10-15 workers during crop season (24-hr operation)
  - Great gang : Second gang : Third gang ≈ 50% : 30% : 20%
  
Capital (1660s prices):
  - Land: £3-5/acre
  - Enslaved workers: £15-25 each
  - Mill + coppers: £300-600
  - Buildings: £200-400
  - Total establishment: ~£2,000-5,000
  
Mill type (Barbados):
  - Windmill: 0 cattle needed for milling
  - Animal mill (early/small ops): 6-12 oxen/horses
  
Fuel (boiling house):
  - Wood consumption: ~1-2 cords per hogshead produced
  → A 80-hhd plantation needs ~80-160 cords/season
  → Major deforestation pressure
  
Timing:
  - Planting to first harvest: 12-18 months
  - Ratoon regrowth: 12 months per cycle
  - Crop season: January-July (6-7 months heavy operation)
  - Off-season: repair, replanting, provision cultivation
  
Soil degradation:
  - Full production years 1-15
  - Yield decline begins year 15-20 (modeled as -3-5%/year)
  - Intensive manuring can offset ~50% of decline
  
Jamaica modifier (larger, fresh land, 1670-1720):
  - Scale multiplier: 1.5-2.5× Barbados plantation size
  - Yield per acre: +20% (fresh soil)
  - Soil decline: delayed to year 20-25
```

## 10. GAPS AND UNCERTAINTIES

1. **Exact cords of wood per hogshead** (boiling fuel): Not directly recoverable from open sources. Watts (1987) *The West Indies: Patterns of Development, Culture and Environmental Change since 1492* is the primary source for this; cited as ~1–2 cords but not verifiable here. **Suggested: treat as a parameter range 0.8–2.5 cords/hhd.**

2. **Copper kettle replacement costs and lifecycle:** Sheridan (1974) *Sugar and Slavery* is the definitive source; estimated ~5–10 years to replacement but not directly confirmed in open sources.

3. **Water requirements for boiling:** The boiling process requires large volumes of clean water. No quantitative figure found; empirically significant (major constraint on plantation location).

4. **Specific indigo production numbers for Caribbean (vs. South Carolina or India):** The Carolina indigo data post-1745 (Pinckney) is well-documented but outside the period. Caribbean 1660–1720 data requires Menard (1976) and French archival sources.

5. **Cacao and coffee (minor crops 1600–1720):** Very limited Caribbean-specific data in open sources for this period. Trinidad cacao is the best-documented but is Spanish-administered.

6. **Provisions-to-calorie balance sheet:** The gap between slave ration allowances and actual survival requirements is documented qualitatively but hard to quantify precisely; suggests significant reliance on self-provisioned crops.

7. **Menard (1976) "From Servants to Slaves" data** on Chesapeake labor transition is foundational for understanding the economics of labor costs during the critical 1660–1700 period but is behind paywalls.

8. **Schwartz (2004) *Tropical Babylons*** (comparative sugar economy Brazil/Caribbean): Would provide the Brazilian benchmark comparisons and more precise production functions. Not accessible in open sources.
