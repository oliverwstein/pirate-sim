# Caribbean Trade Goods, 1600–1720: Comprehensive Simulation Data Table

## Preamble: Currency & Measurement Conventions

**Reference Currency:** Spanish peso / piece of eight (real de a ocho). 1 peso = 8 reales = 25.563g fine silver (standard from 1497 to 1728). [Wikipedia, "Spanish dollar"] Exchange rate benchmarks: £1 sterling ≈ 4.5–5 pesos (rough period average); 1 shilling ≈ 0.24 pesos; 1 penny ≈ 0.020 pesos. Note that exchange rates fluctuated significantly; this conversion is an approximation for simulation use.

**Price convention:** "At a reference Caribbean port" means Bridgetown (Barbados) or Port Royal (Jamaica) — i.e., the *selling/export* price for colonial goods, or the *landed* price for imports. Spanish-colonial prices (Cartagena, Havana, Veracruz) often differ by 20–50%+ due to the *flota* system and contraband dynamics.

**Confidence key for prices:** ✓✓ = well-attested in primary records / major secondary sources; ✓ = reasonable estimate from proximate data; ~ = inferred/extrapolated; ? = highly uncertain.

**Value-to-weight index (VTW):** Muscovado sugar at c. 1700 prices ≈ 0.040 pesos/lb = index **1.0**. All other goods indexed relative to this baseline.

**Primary scholarly references drawn upon:**
- McCusker, John J. & Menard, Russell R. (1985). *The Economy of British America, 1607–1789*. Chapel Hill: UNC Press. [The definitive price series for colonial English Atlantic commodities]

## Production Input Archetypes (for Simulation Logic)

### Type A: Sugar Plantation System
- **Required land:** 50–500 acres tropical lowland
- **Required labor:** 60–300 enslaved workers
- **Required capital:** ~£5,000–20,000 startup → parameterize as 25,000–100,000 pesos
- **Ongoing imports:** African enslaved persons, salt provisions, European tools/copper, stave lumber, firewood
- **Outputs:** Muscovado sugar (primary), molasses (by-product, ~1.5 gallons molasses per gallon sugar-equivalent), rum (if distillery present)
- **Time to production:** ~18 months from land clearing to first crop

### Type B: Minor Plantation Crop
- **Tobacco, cotton, ginger, indigo:** 10–50 acres, 5–30 laborers, modest capital (~500–2,000 pesos). Can be operated by smaller planters.
- **Cotton, ginger:** no machinery needed
- **Indigo:** processing vats required (~200–500 pesos capital)
- **Tobacco:** curing house required (~£50–200 = ~250–1,000 pesos)

### Type C: Extractive Trade
- **Logwood:** 3–15 cutters, axes, canoe (~100–500 pesos capital). Output: 2–5 tons/man-month
- **Hides:** vaqueros, salt, corrals. Extremely low capital.
- **Allspice:** wild-gathering, essentially zero capital.

### Type D: Processing/Artisan
- **Rum distillery:** ~£50–200 still, adjacent to sugar operation. Low additional labor.
- **Salt works:** dykes/salinas, low ongoing capital.
- **Cooperage:** skilled cooper, ~£50 toolset, raw staves from N. America.

## Key Simulation Parameters and Mechanics

### Price Volatility Factors
- **Sugar:** most stable (large market, broad production base) — ±15–25% from trend
- **Tobacco:** secular decline overrides cyclical fluctuation; ±20–30% cyclical
- **Indigo:** volatile (Indian competition, Caribbean production variability) — ±30–40%
- **Logwood:** Spanish enforcement shocks (raids, treaties) cause ±50%+ spikes
- **Cacao:** Spanish monopoly controls supply = low volatility for Spanish ports, high volatility for contraband routes
- **Provisions:** wartime spikes (naval demand, blockades) — ±50–100%
- **Silver:** essentially fixed (IS the numeraire), except for known debasements (Spain 1642: shift to 10 reales provinciales per peso)
- **Enslaved persons:** rising secular trend throughout; war disruptions cause spikes

### Cargo Capacity Reference (for Simulation)
A typical 200-ton merchant sloop/bark c.1700 carried:
- **Sugar:** ~150 hogsheads × 1,500 lbs = 225,000 lbs = ~9,400–13,000 pesos worth
- **Tobacco:** ~150 hogsheads × 1,000 lbs = 150,000 lbs = ~3,000–5,000 pesos
- **Logwood:** ~150–180 tons = ~5,500–9,000 pesos
- **Hides:** ~3,000–5,000 hides = ~6,000–20,000 pesos
- **European manufactured goods (inbound):** compact high-value cargo; same ship capacity could carry ~20,000–40,000 pesos worth

### Contraband Premium/Discount (Dutch Curaçao vs. Spanish Portobello model)
- Spanish colonial ports: official prices typically **20–40% above** open-market prices due to *flota* system monopoly
- Contraband via Dutch/English intermediaries: typically **15–25% below** official Spanish prices, driving persistent smuggling
- Klooster (2003) documents this extensively for Dutch Curaçao: Dutch offered better prices for both goods sold and goods bought, making contraband trade systemically advantageous for Spanish colonial merchants [Klooster, *Illicit Riches*]

## Gaps and Uncertainties

1. **Price confidence is highest** for English Caribbean sugar and tobacco (McCusker & Menard provide robust series). **Lowest** for Spanish-controlled goods (cacao, silver at colonial ports), Dutch contraband, and provisions.
2. **Coffee** should be marked as ABSENT as a Caribbean good until after 1720; only appear as expensive import luxury.
3. **Cacao prices** are particularly uncertain in this compilation because Spanish colonial price controls distort observed prices; true market-clearing prices on contraband routes (Dutch Curaçao) were probably 20–30% higher than official Spanish prices.
4. **Enslaved person prices** have the best documentation from Royal African Company records (1660–1698), but pre-1660 prices are estimates. Galenson (1986) provides the most rigorous series.
5. **Logwood price trajectory** from the 1640s–1680s peak period is less well-documented than the post-1713 figure from Cordingly/Woodard. The specific 1713–1716 figure (£12.09/ton, converted to ~57 pesos) is the most solid anchor.
6. **Naval stores** are almost entirely NOT Caribbean production in this period — they flow *through* Caribbean ports but originate in North American or Baltic sources. If you need Caribbean-specific prices, add ~15–25% freight markup over North American production prices.
7. **The tobacco price collapse** (3s/lb in 1615 → 1d/lb by 1640) is one of the most dramatic price events in the entire colonial period and fundamentally restructures Caribbean economic geography. Any simulation needs to model this secular decline correctly or risk systematic errors in early period wealth calculations.
8. **Suggested follow-up searches:** For more granular price data per year, the best public dataset is the *Historical Statistics of the United States* (HSUS, Cambridge Millennial Edition) commodity price tables; also the Database on Early American History (DEAH) at ICPSR. McCusker's *Money and Exchange in Europe and America, 1600–1775* (1978) has the best currency conversion tables for the period.
